## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem Statement

Aerial manipulation requires precise, fast-response arm control under the disturbances introduced by UAV flight dynamics. Traditional model-based controllers struggle with the coupled dynamics of flight and manipulation. Reinforcement learning in simulation (Isaac Lab) offers a promising path to learning robust controllers, but sim-to-real transfer for aerial manipulation remains challenging due to aerodynamic disturbances, payload variations, and contact dynamics.

### 1.2 Contributions

1. **C1: Isaac Lab Aerial Manipulation Environment**: Simulation environment with realistic UAV-arm coupled dynamics, aerodynamic disturbances, and contact modeling.
2. **C2: Fast-Response RL Controller**: RL policy trained for high-frequency (>100Hz) arm control under flight disturbances.
3. **C3: Sim-to-Real Transfer Protocol**: Domain randomization and adaptation strategy for aerial manipulation.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Isaac Lab RL for Manipulation

Isaac Lab has emerged as the de facto GPU-native successor to Isaac Gym for large-scale robot learning, providing modular environments, batched simulation, and a cleaner bridge between reinforcement learning, imitation learning, and sim-to-real deployment [REF: 2511.04831]. For D07, its key value is not merely faster training throughput, but the ability to standardize environment definition, disturbance injection, reset logic, and deployment-facing evaluation under one reproducible stack. This makes Isaac Lab a suitable substrate for aerial-arm control, where controller comparison is otherwise confounded by inconsistent simulators and reward plumbing.

Beyond pure infrastructure, recent Isaac-Lab-centered works also show that training workflow quality matters as much as policy architecture. AGILE [REF: 2603.20147] emphasizes deterministic pre-training validation, scenario-based testing, and unified evaluation pipelines, while WHOLE-MoMa [REF: 2604.12509] shows that control priors and offline RL can be staged rather than learned end-to-end from scratch. These works collectively suggest that D07 should treat Isaac Lab not as a benchmark wrapper, but as the backbone for a staged research protocol: environment verification first, then warm-started policy learning, and only then deployment-oriented stress testing.

### 2.2 Aerial Manipulation Control

Aerial manipulation sits in a uniquely difficult regime because the arm controller cannot be analyzed independently from the UAV base: arm motion perturbs base attitude, while flight disturbances degrade end-effector precision. Aerial Manipulator RL [REF: 2512.21085] provides a strong hybrid baseline by combining an RL outer loop with INDI/PID inner-loop stabilization, achieving centimeter-level end-effector precision under payload perturbation. This result is important because it reframes hybrid inner loops as a first-class method family rather than an engineering patch.

Recent comparative evidence further warns against evaluating only within the RL family. A Comparison of Reinforcement Learning and Optimal Control for Aerial Manipulators [REF: 2604.12628] directly places RL and pseudo-spectral optimal control in the same problem setting, showing that the real design question is not “which RL algorithm wins,” but which control interfaces should remain learned, which should remain optimized, and where hybridization provides the best stability-to-complexity trade-off. For D07, this motivates a three-way baseline structure: pure RL, fixed-flight or manipulator-only local compensation, and deployable hybrid control.

### 2.3 Sim-to-Real Transfer for Fast Manipulation

Sim-to-real transfer for manipulation has recently shifted from naive domain randomization toward more structured bridges spanning perception, reward, and adaptation. RL-Based Sim-Real Co-Training for VLA [REF: 2602.12628] shows that simulation RL can be paired with real-data anchoring instead of replaced by it, suggesting that sim and real should be treated as a co-training loop rather than two isolated stages. Squint [REF: 2602.21203] adds another important signal: for low-cost arms and limited compute, the main win may come from sample-efficient visual RL and rapid adaptation, not maximal simulator realism.

Perception-side transfer is equally critical. ViserDex [REF: 2604.11138] demonstrates that 3D Gaussian Splatting can serve as a structured visual bridge for robust sim-to-real manipulation, outperforming ordinary rendered data in downstream policy transfer. Although developed for dexterous in-hand reorientation, its deeper message for D07 is that perception gap reduction can become an independent source of transfer gain, and therefore should not be conflated with control-policy improvement. In aerial-arm settings, this implies that visual bridge modules, warm-start policies, and hybrid controllers must be evaluated as separable contributors.

Find the Fruit [REF: 2505.16547] further strengthens this separation by showing that zero-shot sim-to-real success can emerge from a carefully staged manipulation stack—high-level interaction planning, low-level compliant control, and plant-property randomization—without yet proving a stronger controller interface. Its main lesson for D07 is that structured sim-to-real robustness may come from exposing the policy to a broader uncertainty set and decomposition-aware execution protocol, rather than from a better late-window disturbance interface per se. This is especially relevant for aerial-arm tasks involving occlusion, contact preparation, or obstacle clearing, where a row may look deployment-ready simply because the environment decomposition is stronger.

### 2.4 Geometry-Aware Structure Priors

Not all improvements in manipulation come from larger policies or richer rewards; some arise from better structural priors over robot-object interaction. RoTri-Diff [REF: 2603.07165] explicitly models triadic robot-object spatial relations and shows that geometric interaction constraints can reduce collision and instability in coordinated manipulation. For D07, the direct takeaway is not to adopt diffusion imitation learning wholesale, but to recognize that relation-aware pose structure may be the right abstraction for local disturbance compensation around grasping and contact.

This observation is especially relevant because aerial-arm control often fails in the final approach and contact phases, where relative pose consistency matters more than long-horizon planning capacity. Thus, geometry-aware priors should be treated as a local compensation layer that complements, rather than replaces, RL controllers. In our framing, they help explain when D07 should be written as relation-aware fast manipulation under coupled disturbance, instead of an overclaimed whole-body autonomy story.

### 2.5 Shared Autonomy and Human-Guided Adaptation

A growing body of manipulation work suggests that deployment-oriented control should not be framed purely as autonomous policy optimization, but also as a question of how human guidance is represented, denoised, and reused. Real-to-Sim-to-Real shared autonomy [REF: 2603.17016] shows that a small amount of real teleoperation can be converted into a human surrogate and residual copilot policy, indicating that human guidance can serve as a structured adaptation prior rather than only an evaluation tool. DiSCo [REF: 2603.22787] extends this logic from single-step correction to sequence-level action refinement through diffusion-based inpainting, suggesting that assistance can be applied over temporally coherent action chunks instead of per-step overrides.

For D07, the main implication is not that shared autonomy should replace reinforcement learning, but that it provides a plausible interface between noisy teleoperation, demonstration collection, and warm-started control. In aerial-arm settings, this matters because human operators may express goal intent correctly while failing to maintain stable high-frequency compensation under delay and coupled disturbance. A sequence-level copilot can therefore be interpreted as a data-quality and adaptation layer that improves demonstration usefulness before or during RL fine-tuning, rather than as the final controller itself.

Hand-in-the-Loop [REF: 2605.15157] sharpens this boundary by showing that intervention quality itself is a first-order variable in dexterous manipulation: seamless blending between autonomous actions and human correction can drastically reduce takeover jitter and generate cleaner corrective data for later policy refinement. For D07, this means human-in-the-loop benefit should be decomposed into at least two parts—**intervention-hygiene gain** and **controller-interface gain**. If a method improves mainly because takeover trajectories become smoother and more learnable, the honest narrative is still data shaping or bounded assistance support rather than a stronger autonomous aerial-arm interface.

### 2.6 Semantic State and Knowledge-Guided Manipulation

Most Isaac-Lab manipulation pipelines still rely on RGB, proprioception, and hand-crafted task variables, leaving object-object and robot-object relations implicit inside the policy backbone. KG-M3PO [REF: 2603.24083] argues for a different route: online 3D scene-graph maintenance, relation-aware updates, and knowledge-guided querying inside the reinforcement-learning loop. Its value for D07 is not that scene graphs should replace control, but that semantic relations can become a compact intermediate state for long-horizon or partially occluded manipulation settings.

For aerial-arm control, this matters in two ways. First, relation-centric state can help separate base drift from task-relevant geometry, e.g., `end-effector ↔ target ↔ obstacle/container` relations, instead of forcing the policy to infer everything from raw pixels. Second, such structure offers a more natural bridge to disturbance-aware local compensation: the controller can reason over whether failure comes from pose misalignment, occlusion, or contact-stage instability. We therefore treat semantic relation state as a candidate support layer for relation-aware local compensation, not as a standalone autonomy claim.

### 2.7 Limitations of Existing Work

Despite rapid progress, four gaps remain unresolved. First, most existing manipulation papers do not explicitly separate training acceleration, perception-bridge gain, shared-autonomy data shaping, semantic-state gain, and deployable control gain, making it hard to tell whether success comes from better policy learning or simply cleaner transfer and supervision conditions. Second, aerial manipulation remains underexplored compared with tabletop or dexterous-hand settings, so the role of coupled base-arm disturbance is still weakly characterized in sim-to-real studies. Third, current literature provides many ingredients—warm starts, geometry priors, semantic relation state, hybrid inner loops, perception bridges, shared-autonomy copilot layers—but lacks a decision protocol for determining when each ingredient deserves to occupy the main narrative of a deployable aerial-arm paper. Fourth, there is still no clear protocol for deciding when teleoperation assistance should be used only for data collection, when it should remain as an online copilot, and when it should be distilled away into a fully autonomous controller.

A fifth gap, sharpened by the recent Isaac Lab platform paper [REF: 2511.04831], is that infrastructure quality is usually treated as neutral background rather than an experimental variable with claim-level consequences. Isaac Lab unifies batched GPU physics, sensor simulation, actuator models, data pipelines, and multi-platform training under one modular stack, but existing aerial-arm drafts rarely specify whether an apparent control gain depends on this stronger infrastructure or would survive under weaker validation hygiene. For D07, this means the field still lacks a clean way to separate **platform-enabled reproducibility gain** from **controller-interface gain**. Without that separation, better resets, disturbance injection, and evaluation tooling can be accidentally over-credited as method innovation.

A sixth gap is that recent positive results in reward modeling and human correction quality still do not cleanly answer the controller question. Robo-Dopamine [REF: 2512.23703] shows that process reward modeling can dramatically improve online manipulation learning with minimal task adaptation, while Hand-in-the-Loop [REF: 2605.15157] shows that seamless corrective blending can sharply improve intervention quality and downstream refinement. Both are highly relevant to D07, but they also expose a methodological risk: a row may improve because **reward credit becomes denser** or because **human correction trajectories become smoother**, even when the underlying aerial-arm control interface has not fundamentally advanced. Existing work rarely forces reward-origin gain, intervention-hygiene gain, and late-window disturbance-interface gain into the same accounting frame.

## 3. Method

### 3.1 Overview

We study fast-response aerial-arm control as a staged decision problem rather than a monolithic end-to-end learning problem. The core hypothesis is that not every gain observed in Isaac Lab should be credited to the same layer: some gains come from better data or warm-start initialization, some from perception-side sim-to-real bridging, some from local geometric relation priors near contact, and only a subset from truly deployable hybrid control under coupled disturbance.

Accordingly, our method is organized into four layers: **L1 warm-started RL training**, **L2 relation-aware local compensation**, **L3 perception-bridge sim-to-real transfer**, and **L4 deployable hybrid stabilization**. The paper's central contribution is not merely to instantiate these layers, but to provide a decision protocol that determines which layer is allowed to become the main claim after the first experimental round.

### 3.2 Layered Control Formulation

At the training layer, we initialize a policy in Isaac Lab using task-specific demonstrations, behavior-cloning warm starts, or real-data anchored pretraining when available. This layer is designed to answer a narrow question: does warm-starting reduce optimization cost and improve early learning stability relative to scratch RL? Any benefit observed here is counted only as **sample-efficiency gain**, not as deployable control evidence.

At the execution layer, we introduce a relation-aware local compensation module operating on end-effector pose, object-relative pose, and short-horizon disturbance state. Inspired by triadic interaction modeling in RoTri-Diff [REF: 2603.07165], this module emphasizes near-contact geometric consistency rather than full-scene autonomy. It is therefore evaluated as a local manipulation stabilizer under UAV-induced perturbation, especially in the final approach, grasp, and lift phases.

At the transfer layer, we allow structured visual bridging between simulation and real deployment using perception-side adaptation modules, including 3DGS-style rendered randomization motivated by ViserDex [REF: 2604.11138]. This layer isolates how much sim-to-real gain can be obtained by reducing visual distribution mismatch before changing the low-level controller. Finally, at the deployment layer, we combine the learned outer-loop controller with a stabilizing inner loop, following the logic of RL-plus-classical-control hybridization in aerial manipulation [REF: 2512.21085]. Only this final layer is allowed to support claims about deployable hybrid aerial-arm control.

### 3.3 Narrative Decision Protocol

We define a narrative decision protocol to prevent overclaiming from partial gains. Let **SEG** denote sample-efficiency gain from warm start, **PBG** denote perception-bridge gain under sim-to-real transfer, **RLG** denote relation-aware local compensation gain near contact, and **DHG** denote deployable hybrid gain under disturbance and deployment-oriented evaluation. Then the paper narrative is selected by the dominant stable gain category rather than by absolute best-case success rate.

If only `SEG` improves while disturbance robustness and deployment coverage remain weak, the paper must be framed as a **data-gated acceleration recipe** rather than a new deployable controller. If `RLG` and/or `PBG` dominate, while full hybrid robustness is not yet established, the paper should be framed as **relation-aware local compensation with a perception bridge**. Only when `DHG` remains positive under coupled-disturbance stress tests, risk coverage checks, and maintenance-cost constraints may the method be presented as a **deployable hybrid control pipeline**. This protocol is crucial for D07 because it keeps warm-start gains, structured perception transfer, and true deployment readiness from collapsing into a single inflated claim.

### 3.4 Minimal System Instantiation for the First Round

For the first experimental round, we instantiate the four-layer view with one concrete design choice per layer so that the draft can immediately map onto an executable research plan. **L1 warm-started RL** uses Isaac-Lab batched simulation together with demonstration or behavior-cloning initialization, motivated by the sample-efficiency logic behind Squint [REF: 2602.21203]. **L2 relation-aware local compensation** augments the policy input with short-horizon end-effector pose, object-relative pose, and contact-stage indicators, following the geometric-prior intuition of RoTri-Diff [REF: 2603.07165]. **L3 perception bridge** adds structured visual randomization and deployment-facing visual adaptation, with 3DGS-style bridge modules treated as an optional but separately measurable source of transfer gain following ViserDex [REF: 2604.11138]. **L4 deployable hybrid stabilization** combines the learned outer-loop policy with a disturbance-rejection inner loop for flight-base attitude and high-frequency arm stabilization.

This minimal instantiation is intentionally asymmetric: lower layers are allowed to improve optimization speed, local contact quality, or transfer robustness, but they are not automatically promoted into a deployable-control claim. In particular, the first-round system is designed so that every added module maps to exactly one dominant gain family by default—warm start to `SEG`, perception bridge to `PBG`, relation-aware local structure to `RLG`, and hybrid stabilization to `DHG`. This explicit mapping is necessary because aerial manipulation papers often bundle all useful ingredients into one large stack before it is clear which ingredient actually explains the main stable gain.

### 3.5 Layer-Specific Hypotheses

We formulate three first-round hypotheses that can be falsified independently. **H1 (Warm-Start Efficiency)**: behavior-cloning or demonstration initialization reduces time-to-threshold and stabilizes early training, but may not substantially change disturbance robustness after convergence. **H2 (Relation-Aware Contact Robustness)**: adding local robot-object geometric structure mainly improves final approach, grasp onset, and lift stability under D2/D3 disturbance, even if nominal D0/D1 success changes little. **H3 (Bridge-to-Deployment Promotion)**: only when perception bridging and hybrid stabilization together preserve gains under disturbance-stratified and deployment-oriented evaluation should the paper be promoted from a local-compensation or transfer story to a deployable hybrid-control story.

These hypotheses are useful not because they guarantee a positive result, but because they determine how negative results should be interpreted. If H1 is positive while H2 and H3 remain weak, the draft should narrow toward acceleration rather than deployment. If H2 is positive without H3, the correct narrative is relation-aware local compensation under coupled disturbance. If H3 is also positive, then the paper earns a deployment-facing title and abstract. Writing these hypotheses directly into the method section keeps the paper aligned with evidence instead of retrospective ambition.

### 3.6 Training Regimes and Interface Variants

The first experimental round instantiates D07 with a deliberately small but decision-relevant training matrix. At the optimization level, we compare **scratch PPO**, **warm-start PPO**, and a **real-data-anchored co-training variant** motivated by RL-Based Sim-Real Co-Training for VLA [REF: 2602.12628]. The goal is not to optimize for raw leaderboard score, but to answer whether early policy shaping through demonstrations or limited real anchoring changes only convergence speed or also improves downstream disturbance robustness and transfer stability. In this framing, any improvement that disappears after matched-compute evaluation remains a `SEG`-only gain.

At the control-interface level, we explicitly separate three families that are often blurred together in aerial-arm papers: **pure learned control**, **fixed-flight / manipulator-only local compensation**, and **hybrid outer-policy + inner-loop stabilization**. This split is directly motivated by the existing local D07 evidence base: DSAM-like results suggest that inner-loop stabilization can be a real method family rather than an engineering patch, while manipulator-only planning under a fixed flight envelope remains a strong intermediate baseline for testing whether the current bottleneck is truly whole-body coupling. In practice, this means D07 should not ask "which policy wins" before asking "which interface layer deserves to learn at all."

We additionally treat **Isaac-Lab-native infrastructure discipline** as an explicit protocol axis rather than invisible background. Following Isaac Lab [REF: 2511.04831], every training regime inherits the same reset semantics, disturbance injection API, logging schema, and multi-platform environment definition so that differences across B1-B4 cannot be attributed to ad hoc simulator glue. This matters for D07 because aerial-arm claims are especially sensitive to hidden platform variance: if a row only looks stronger because its disturbance slicer, actuator model, or replay logging is cleaner, then the result should be interpreted as infrastructure-enabled reproducibility rather than controller-interface superiority. By writing this protocol lock into the method section, the paper commits to comparing learning and control routes on a common validated substrate.

We also preserve one additional axis for later extension: **shared-autonomy-assisted warm start**, where teleoperation is filtered or refined by a sequence-level copilot before being used as demonstration data or online corrective prior. Motivated by DiSCo [REF: 2603.22787] and Real-to-Sim-to-Real shared autonomy [REF: 2603.17016], this axis is not yet expanded into a full large-grid comparison in the first round; instead, it is treated as a structured fallback if naive demonstrations prove too noisy for stable warm-starting under aerial disturbance. By writing the training regimes and interface variants explicitly into the method section, the paper commits in advance to an evidence-first decomposition of gain sources rather than a post hoc fusion narrative.

### 3.7 Human-Guided Data Shaping as an Optional Adaptation Layer

We introduce an optional human-guided data-shaping layer that sits before or alongside RL fine-tuning. The purpose of this layer is not to claim that the final aerial-arm system requires a human copilot online, but to test whether sequence-level refinement of teleoperated actions yields better initialization and safer adaptation under disturbance. Concretely, raw human action sequences can be passed through a sequence copilot that preserves high-level intent while denoising unstable local corrections, in the spirit of diffusion-based action inpainting [REF: 2603.22787]. The refined sequences are then used either as behavior-cloning warm-start data or as a bounded online correction prior during early training.

This layer is evaluated under a narrow interpretation. If it mainly reduces demonstration noise and speeds up optimization, the gain is recorded under `SEG` or a teleoperation-data-quality auxiliary metric rather than being promoted to a controller contribution. If it materially improves transfer robustness or disturbance recovery only when kept online, then the correct narrative shifts toward shared-autonomy-assisted deployment rather than fully autonomous control. In other words, D07 treats human-guided data shaping as a diagnostic interface for deciding whether teleoperation should remain part of the deployed system, not as an automatic source of inflated autonomy claims.

### 3.8 Semantic Relation State as a Support Layer for Local Compensation

We further introduce an optional semantic relation state layer that augments the controller with compact, disturbance-aware relational structure. Inspired by KG-M3PO [REF: 2603.24083], this layer maintains task-relevant relations such as `end-effector ↔ target`, `target ↔ container`, and `arm ↔ obstacle` rather than requiring the policy to infer every failure mode from pixels and proprioception alone. The purpose is not to turn D07 into a full scene-graph reasoning paper, but to test whether relation-centric state helps local compensation identify which geometric inconsistency is causing failure under base drift and partial occlusion.

This support layer is especially relevant for aerial-arm operation because the same raw image perturbation may correspond to very different control errors: target misalignment, base-induced viewpoint shift, or imminent obstacle conflict. By exposing these relations explicitly, the policy may allocate capacity more efficiently to disturbance compensation near contact. However, any gain from this layer is conservatively routed to `RLG` unless it survives deployment-oriented evaluation together with the rest of the stack.

### 3.9 Disturbance-Bounded Shared-Autonomy Interface

We further formalize a disturbance-bounded shared-autonomy interface motivated by recent local evidence that sequence-level human guidance can act as a data-shaping or bounded online correction layer rather than an always-on teleoperation fallback. Building on DiSCo [REF: 2603.22787] and Real-to-Sim-to-Real shared autonomy [REF: 2603.17016], we assume that human intent is often globally correct but locally noisy under aerial delay, base drift, and contact transients. Instead of interpreting human intervention as a binary switch between autonomy and teleoperation, we model it as a constrained interface that may operate in three modes: **offline demonstration refinement**, **early-training correction prior**, and **deployment-time bounded copilot assistance**.

This formulation matters for D07 because noisy aerial teleoperation can pollute behavior-cloning warm starts and obscure whether the observed gain comes from better policy learning or merely better demonstration hygiene. By explicitly separating these three modes, the method can ask a sharper question: when does human guidance improve only the initialization distribution, and when must it remain online to stabilize disturbance-heavy contact phases? Any benefit that appears only through cleaner initialization is routed to `SEG`, while persistent online benefit is treated as evidence for a shared-autonomy-assisted deployment route rather than a fully autonomous hybrid-control claim.

### 3.10 Gain Attribution Under Coupled Disturbance

To keep the paper evidence-first, we define a simple gain-attribution rule over the main candidate modules introduced above. Let the measured first-round deltas be `ΔSEG`, `ΔPBG`, `ΔRLG`, `ΔDHG`, together with auxiliary human-guidance benefit `ΔHAG` and semantic-state benefit `ΔSSG`. We do not allow these gains to be pooled blindly. Instead, each gain is only considered valid if it appears under its intended evaluation gate: `ΔSEG` under matched-compute acceleration tests, `ΔPBG` under sim-to-real bridge evaluation, `ΔRLG` under D2/D3 contact-heavy disturbance slices, `ΔDHG` under deployment-oriented hybrid evaluation, `ΔHAG` under teleoperation-quality or bounded-copilot ablations, and `ΔSSG` under semantic-state ablations.

The practical consequence is a hard narrative constraint. If the strongest stable deltas are `ΔSEG + ΔHAG`, then the paper must be framed around data shaping and acceleration. If `ΔRLG + ΔSSG` dominate while `ΔDHG` remains weak, then the correct story is relation-aware local compensation with structured state support. If `ΔPBG` dominates, the story becomes a perception-bridge paper. Only when `ΔDHG` remains positive after bridge cost, risk coverage, and maintenance overhead are all accounted for may the draft escalate to a deployable hybrid-control narrative. This explicit attribution rule is necessary because D07 otherwise risks collapsing data quality, visual transfer, geometric stabilization, and controller architecture into one oversized claim.

### 3.11 Disturbance Regimes and Phase-Aligned State Partition

We partition aerial-arm execution into four disturbance regimes and three manipulation phases so that D07 can localize gains instead of over-reporting aggregate success. The disturbance regimes are **D0 nominal hover**, **D1 low-amplitude drift**, **D2 sustained platform disturbance**, and **D3 contact-compounded disturbance**. The manipulation phases are **P1 pre-contact alignment**, **P2 contact establishment**, and **P3 post-contact stabilization / lift**. Every reported gain must therefore be attached to a regime-phase pair `(Di, Pj)` rather than only to a whole-task average.

This partition matters because different ingredients in D07 should help in different places. Warm-start initialization should primarily act in optimization space and may only weakly affect `(D0, P1)` to `(D1, P1)` execution behavior after convergence. Relation-aware geometric priors and semantic relation state are expected to matter most in `(D2, P2)` and `(D3, P2/P3)`, where end-effector–object consistency and contact-sensitive correction dominate. Hybrid inner-loop stabilization, by contrast, only earns a deployment-facing claim if it preserves gains across the full disturbance ladder rather than winning in one narrow phase slice.

We therefore define a phase-aligned state summary `s_t = (x_base, x_arm, r_geo, r_sem, d_t, p_t)`, where `x_base` captures base attitude and translational drift, `x_arm` captures arm and gripper state, `r_geo` denotes local geometric relations, `r_sem` denotes optional semantic relation state, `d_t` is the disturbance regime tag, and `p_t` is the current phase tag. This state partition is not introduced as a new representation novelty by itself; instead, it serves as the accounting scaffold that lets D07 distinguish optimization acceleration from actual disturbance-phase robustness.

### 3.12 Phase-Bounded Compensation Interface

We formalize the local compensation layer as a phase-bounded interface instead of an unconstrained auxiliary policy. Let the main controller output an action proposal `a_t^π`, and let the compensation module output a correction `δ_t` conditioned on local relation state and phase context. The executed action is `a_t = a_t^π + M(d_t, p_t) ⊙ δ_t`, where `M(d_t, p_t)` is a regime-phase gate that only allows substantial correction in disturbance-heavy or contact-sensitive slices. In particular, D07 should allow stronger correction in `(D2, P2)`, `(D3, P2)`, and `(D3, P3)` than in nominal `(D0, P1)` motion.

This interface is important because it prevents the compensation layer from silently becoming a second full controller. If `δ_t` dominates everywhere, then any measured gain can no longer be honestly attributed to warm start, perception bridge, or hybrid stabilization. By bounding compensation to phase-appropriate windows, we make a narrower and more falsifiable claim: relation-aware structure helps mainly where geometric and contact transients destabilize aerial manipulation. If gains later appear only inside these gates, D07 should be written as local compensation under coupled disturbance, not as a broad whole-body autonomy story.

### 3.13 Shared-Autonomy Sequence Copilot as a Bounded Adaptation Interface

The recent D07 evidence base suggests that shared autonomy should not be treated merely as a teleoperation convenience layer. DiSCo [REF: 2603.22787] shows that sequence-level diffusion copilots can repair noisy human action sequences while preserving high-level intent, and Real-to-Sim-to-Real shared autonomy [REF: 2603.17016] suggests that small amounts of human interaction can be recycled into structured adaptation priors instead of discarded after demonstration collection. For aerial-arm manipulation, this matters because operators may still specify the correct target and phase intent under uncertainty, yet fail to provide stable low-level compensation under delay, base drift, or contact transients.

We therefore model shared autonomy as a bounded adaptation interface with three modes: **offline sequence refinement**, **early-training correction prior**, and **deployment-time bounded copilot assistance**. In the first mode, noisy teleoperation trajectories are denoised into behavior-cloning warm starts; in the second, a sequence copilot provides limited corrective priors during early RL fine-tuning; and in the third, human intent remains online but the copilot regularizes low-level execution over short action chunks. This formulation prevents D07 from collapsing all human contribution into one vague “HITL helps” statement. Any gain that only improves demonstration quality or convergence speed is routed to `SEG` or teleoperation-data quality, while any gain that remains necessary online must be interpreted as evidence for a shared-autonomy-assisted deployment route rather than fully autonomous control.

### 3.14 Semantic Relation State as Disturbance-Disambiguating Support

The local D07 reading queue also suggests that relation-centric state may be useful not because it adds more semantics in the abstract, but because it helps the controller disambiguate different failure causes under coupled disturbance. KG-M3PO [REF: 2603.24083] indicates that online scene-graph or relation-aware state can provide compact task structure under partial observability. For aerial-arm control, this structure is especially useful when visually similar observations may correspond to distinct control failures: viewpoint drift from base motion, target misalignment before grasp, obstacle conflict during approach, or contact-stage instability after closure.

We therefore introduce a semantic relation support layer that augments the local state with a small set of task-relevant relations, such as `end-effector ↔ target`, `target ↔ container`, and `arm ↔ obstacle`. This layer is not presented as a standalone reasoning contribution. Instead, it serves as a support interface for relation-aware local compensation, and any gain obtained from it is conservatively attributed to `RLG` unless it also survives deployment-oriented evaluation together with the rest of the stack. In this way, D07 can test semantic structure without allowing it to inflate the paper into a generic knowledge-guided manipulation narrative.

### 3.15 Window-Localized Gain Attribution Contract

To prevent first-round evidence from being over-upgraded, we define three execution windows for gain attribution: **W1 pre-contact alignment**, **W2 contact establishment**, and **W3 post-contact stabilization**. Warm-start and perception-bridge modules are expected to help mostly in W1 and, at most, the W1→W2 transition, whereas relation-aware local compensation and semantic support should matter primarily in W2 and W3 under D2/D3 disturbance. Hybrid stabilization only earns a deployment-facing interpretation if its gains remain positive across all three windows together with acceptable transfer and maintenance characteristics.

Formally, each baseline is summarized not only by `(SEG, PBG, RLG, DHG)` but also by a localized tuple `Δloc = (Δalign, Δcontact, Δstabilize)`. A result with large `Δalign` but weak `Δcontact` and `Δstabilize` is interpreted as acceleration or transfer support rather than robust manipulation control. A result with strong `Δcontact` and `Δstabilize` under D2/D3 but weak hybrid promotion is interpreted as relation-aware local compensation. This contract is essential for D07 because it forces every positive result to declare where in the aerial manipulation pipeline the gain actually occurs, instead of letting pre-contact improvements masquerade as deployable whole-task robustness.

### 3.16 Disturbance-Human Coupling Budget and Intervention Retention

We further introduce a disturbance-human coupling budget to determine when shared-autonomy signals should remain in the deployed loop and when they should be distilled away after data collection. Let `ι_t` denote bounded human or copilot intervention intensity over a short horizon, and let `b_t = (d_t, p_t, delay_t, drift_t)` summarize the current disturbance-phase context. We only allow online intervention retention when the system repeatedly enters high-risk slices such as delayed W2 contact establishment or W3 post-contact stabilization under D3 disturbance, where autonomous compensation alone fails to keep the execution thread stable.

This budget matters because D07 should not quietly keep human assistance online while still claiming a fully autonomous controller. If intervention improves only demonstration quality or early convergence, its effect is routed to `SEG` or demonstration utility. If bounded intervention remains necessary in high-disturbance contact windows, the paper must explicitly narrow toward a shared-autonomy-assisted deployment route. In this way, D07 can separate "human help as data shaping" from "human help as persistent control dependency" without blurring the two into a single HITL narrative.

### 3.17 BC-to-Q Warm Start and Gated Online Recovery

The recent Q2RL evidence [REF: 2605.05172] suggests that D07 should not treat behavior-cloning warm start as a binary choice between “keep BC fixed” and “discard BC for online RL.” Instead, a more deployment-relevant route is to extract a lightweight Q-function from the BC policy and use it as a *gated bridge* between stable initialization and bounded online improvement. For aerial-arm control, this matters because catastrophic forgetting under disturbance can erase a usable warm start long before a pure RL fine-tuning loop learns robust contact recovery.

We therefore introduce a **BC-to-Q warm-start interface** for D07. Let `π_BC` denote the demonstration-initialized policy and `Q_BC` denote a Q-estimator distilled from the same behavior manifold. During online fine-tuning, the controller samples between the BC action and the RL action under a gated selection rule
\[
a_t = \begin{cases}
a_t^{\text{BC}}, & Q_{BC}(s_t, a_t^{\text{BC}}) \ge Q_{RL}(s_t, a_t^{\text{RL}}) - \epsilon_t \\
a_t^{\text{RL}}, & \text{otherwise},
\end{cases}
\]
where `ε_t` is a disturbance- and phase-aware tolerance. In low-risk windows or when the RL branch has already established stable gains, the gate may favor `a_t^{RL}`; in high-risk W2/W3 slices under D2/D3 disturbance, the gate is allowed to fall back to BC-consistent actions when the learned online branch becomes unstable.

This interface is intentionally narrow in narrative scope. If the gain appears mainly as faster early recovery after warm start and reduced catastrophic forgetting, it is routed to `SEG` plus online-retention support rather than being promoted to a deployable hybrid-control claim. Only when BC-to-Q gating improves W2/W3 contact robustness under disturbance, *and* those gains survive the hybrid and transfer gates, may it support a stronger deployment-facing story. In other words, Q-guided gating is first treated as an **honest anti-forgetting bridge** between offline skill and online improvement, not as automatic proof of full aerial-arm autonomy.

### 3.18 Hierarchical RL Planning with Analytic Safety Projection

The newly added reactive dexterous grasping evidence [REF: 2605.03363] sharpens another D07 design choice: a high-dimensional manipulation stack may benefit from explicitly separating **task-space intent generation** from **joint-space safety enforcement**. Rather than asking a single policy to jointly discover fast response, collision avoidance, joint-limit satisfaction, and disturbance recovery, D07 can instantiate a two-stage interface in which the learned policy proposes short-horizon task-space motion and an analytic controller projects that proposal into a safe executable action.

Concretely, we let the high-level policy output a task-space velocity proposal `u_t^{task}` conditioned on phase-aligned disturbance state, while a low-level projection layer solves
\[
a_t^* = \arg\min_{a_t} \; \|J(q_t)a_t - u_t^{task}\|_2^2 + \lambda_{safe} \mathcal{C}(a_t; q_t, o_t, c_t),
\]
where `J(q_t)` is the manipulator Jacobian and `\mathcal{C}` encodes joint-limit, self-collision, obstacle-clearance, and contact-safety constraints. The goal is not to turn D07 into a QP-control paper, but to create a clean experimental boundary between *RL planning gain* and *analytic safety-shell gain*.

This decomposition is valuable for D07 because it gives the hybrid line a stronger mechanistic interpretation. If the analytic projection mainly prevents unsafe local actions without improving disturbance-window robustness, its gain belongs to a safety-shell baseline. If, however, the planner-plus-projection stack materially improves W2/W3 stability, reduces catastrophic contact failures, and survives sim-to-real transfer, then D07 can legitimately argue for a **deployable hybrid control route** rather than a pure warm-start or local-compensation paper. This section therefore strengthens the method-side distinction between `RLG` and `DHG`: relation-aware priors explain *where* to compensate, while analytic projection layers explain *how* to keep that compensation controller-consumable and safe.

### 3.19 Disturbance-Localized Credit Ledger for First-Round D07 Claims

The recent D07 local evidence base now spans six distinct support families that can all produce superficially positive results: **reward-origin recovery credit** from ARM-style shaping, **retention credit** from Q2RL-style BC-to-Q gating, **analytic projection credit** from reactive task-space-to-joint-space safety projection, **exploration credit** from TMRL-style diffusion-timestep modulation, **dynamics-set robustness credit** from dynamics-randomized reactive training, and **sequence-copilot credit** from DiSCo-style bounded action refinement. The main methodological risk is that these routes can all improve some part of the first real bundle while still corresponding to weaker claims than a true deployable hybrid aerial-arm interface.

We therefore introduce a unified disturbance-localized credit ledger `\Xi_{D07}` that records, for each candidate route, **which execution window improved first**, **which disturbance boundary it survived**, and **which weaker family explanations remain undefeated**. Concretely, each row is summarized as
\[
\Xi_{D07}(r)=\big(w_r^{+},\; w_r^{\dagger},\; s_r^{\dagger},\; \kappa_r^{src},\; \kappa_r^{dep},\; \kappa_r^{undefeated}\big),
\]
where `w_r^{+}` is the earliest positive window, `w_r^{\dagger}` is the latest surviving window, `s_r^{\dagger}` is the highest disturbance source boundary reached, `\kappa_r^{src}` tags the dominant source family (reward / retention / projection / exploration / dynamics-set / copilot), `\kappa_r^{dep}` indicates whether the gain survives into deployment-oriented slices, and `\kappa_r^{undefeated}` lists the still-plausible weaker explanations that were not yet ruled out.

This ledger is intentionally conservative. A route whose gain stops at W1 or only survives to pre-contact disturbance cannot be promoted beyond acceleration or preparation support. A route that reaches W2/W3 but still leaves a weaker support family undefeated must also remain frozen at the weaker title route. Only rows that survive into late windows, cross payload-bearing or post-contact disturbance boundaries, and defeat matched weaker-family explanations are allowed to support a deployable-interface claim. In this way, D07 no longer asks only whether a method helped, but whether it helped *late enough*, under *hard enough disturbance*, and after *enough weaker explanations were explicitly removed*.


## 4. Experiments

### 4.1 Setup

We evaluate on an aerial manipulator consisting of a multirotor base with a lightweight robotic arm in Isaac Lab. Tasks are organized by increasing coupling difficulty: **(i)** static target reach, **(ii)** reach-and-grasp under base disturbance, and **(iii)** grasp-and-lift with payload-induced perturbation. The evaluation emphasis is not on long-horizon task diversity, but on high-frequency local control, disturbance rejection, and transfer viability.

Baselines are grouped into three families: **pure RL from scratch**, **warm-started RL**, and **hybrid control** with a stabilizing inner loop. We additionally include ablations for relation-aware local compensation and perception-bridge transfer. Metrics include task success rate, recovery success under injected disturbance, sample efficiency, sim-to-real transfer delta, and deployment-oriented maintenance cost. This experimental structure is chosen to answer a more precise question than “what gets the highest score”: which layer produces the dominant, reproducible gain that deserves to define the paper.

### 4.2 Evaluation Protocol and Decision Gates

To keep first-round results interpretable, we organize experiments into four serial gates. **G0 (environment validity)** checks reset consistency, disturbance injection correctness, reward boundedness, and observation-action logging integrity before any long training run is considered valid. **G1 (sample-efficiency gate)** compares warm-started RL against scratch RL under matched compute budgets and records whether early learning acceleration persists beyond the first convergence window. **G2 (control-structure gate)** compares fixed-flight local compensation, pure RL, and hybrid inner-loop stabilization under identical disturbance profiles. **G3 (transfer-and-deployment gate)** evaluates sim-to-real delta, risk coverage, and maintenance overhead when perception bridging and hybrid stabilization are introduced together.

We use this gate structure because D07 is especially vulnerable to narrative inflation: a method may train faster without being more deployable, or transfer better visually without stabilizing contact-rich control. Therefore, every claim in the paper must be tied to the gate where it is earned. A gain that appears only in G1 is counted as optimization acceleration, not as deployment readiness. A gain that appears in G2 but collapses in G3 is counted as a simulation-side control advantage, not a deployable sim-to-real result.

### 4.3 Local Evidence Anchors for the First Round

The first-round experiment plan is explicitly anchored to three local evidence sources that already exist in the D07 knowledge base. **RL-Based Sim-Real Co-Training for VLA** [REF: 2602.12628] anchors the claim that sim training, limited real-data anchoring, and transfer evaluation should be treated as a connected protocol rather than as isolated phases. **Squint** [REF: 2602.21203] anchors the interpretation of warm-start gains as training-efficiency evidence rather than automatic controller superiority. **RoTri-Diff** [REF: 2603.07165] anchors the hypothesis that geometry-aware local structure should mostly improve disturbance-heavy contact phases instead of headline nominal success.

We use these anchors to define what counts as a meaningful positive signal. A result is not considered deployment evidence simply because it improves average success rate; it must improve the correct metric family under the correct gate. For example, a Squint-like advantage is meaningful only if it survives matched-compute evaluation, and a RoTri-Diff-like advantage is meaningful only if it reduces D2/D3 contact instability rather than merely smoothing trajectories in D0/D1. This anchor-based interpretation keeps the experiment section tightly coupled to the strongest local evidence already collected for D07.

### 4.4 Main Metrics

We report five primary metrics. **Success Rate (SR)** measures task completion under coupled base-arm disturbance. **Sample-Efficiency Gain (SEG)** measures how much warm-start initialization reduces the wall-clock or environment-step cost needed to reach a fixed success threshold. **Sim-to-Real Transfer Gap (STG)** measures the performance delta between Isaac-Lab validation and real or deployment-like evaluation settings. **Stabilized Recovery under Aerial Risk (SARR)** measures recovery quality after injected disturbance during approach, grasp, and lift. **Normalized Maintenance Cost (NMC)** measures the extra engineering burden introduced by hybrid controllers, bridge modules, and calibration procedures.

These metrics are mapped directly to the narrative decision protocol. A method with high SEG but weak SARR cannot be described as deployable control. A method with lower STG but large NMC must be discussed as a qualified transfer recipe rather than a universally superior design. This mapping ensures that D07 reports which type of gain it achieved, instead of letting one best-case success number dominate the paper.

### 4.4 First-Round Baseline Matrix

We explicitly structure the first experimental round around four baseline families so that different gain sources cannot hide inside one aggregate success rate. **B1 Scratch RL** trains directly in Isaac Lab with no demonstration warm start and no structured bridge modules. **B2 Warm-Started RL** adds demonstrations or behavior-cloning initialization and is used primarily to measure `SEG`, following the fast-adaptation logic evidenced by Squint [REF: 2602.21203]. **B3 Relation-Aware + Perception Bridge** augments the controller with triadic relative-pose structure and deployment-facing visual transfer support, so that `RLG` and `PBG` can be evaluated without conflating them with hybrid stabilization. **B4 Deployable Hybrid** adds the stabilizing inner loop and risk-aware deployment evaluation, and is the only baseline family eligible to support a `DHG` claim.

For each family, we report not only average success rate but also the gain-dominance vector `(SEG, PBG, RLG, DHG)`, together with `STG`, `SARR`, and `NMC`. This matrix is important because D07 is not merely comparing algorithms; it is comparing explanations of where the gain comes from. A strong B2 result with weak B4 transfer means the paper should be routed to an acceleration narrative. A strong B3 result with only partial B4 support means the paper should be written as relation-aware compensation with perception bridging. Only a strong B4 result under acceptable `NMC` can justify a deployable-control abstract.

### 4.5 Disturbance-Stratified Evaluation

To reduce ambiguity in first-round interpretation, we stratify evaluation by disturbance regime rather than reporting only a single aggregate success number. We separate **D0 nominal hover**, **D1 low-amplitude platform drift**, **D2 sustained lateral disturbance**, and **D3 contact-phase compounded disturbance**. This regime split is necessary because optimization gains usually appear first in D0/D1, whereas geometry-aware compensation and deployable hybrid stabilization should reveal themselves more clearly in D2/D3.

Each disturbance regime is paired with a minimal decision gate. `SEG` is credited only if warm start reaches the target threshold faster under D0/D1 without regressing post-convergence stability. `RLG` is credited only if relation-aware structure reduces contact-stage failure, collision, or pose-loss under D2/D3. `DHG` is credited only if the hybrid stack remains positive across the full D0→D3 ladder while keeping maintenance overhead acceptable. This disturbance-stratified view prevents D07 from mistaking easier optimization or cleaner nominal trajectories for evidence of deployable aerial-arm control.

### 4.6 Minimal Verification Questions

Before any deployability claim is made, the first round must answer three concrete questions. **Q1:** does warm-start initialization still help after matched compute and seed averaging, or is the advantage only an early optimization transient? **Q2:** do relation-aware local priors improve final-approach and grasp stability under D2/D3 disturbance, instead of merely smoothing trajectories in simulation? **Q3:** does the hybrid controller retain its advantage after sim-to-real bridge cost, risk coverage checks, and maintenance overhead are all accounted for?

These questions define the default routing of the paper after the first experimental round. If only Q1 is positive, D07 should be written as a **data-gated acceleration recipe**. If Q2 is positive and Q3 remains inconclusive, the draft should narrow to **relation-aware local compensation under coupled disturbance**, optionally with perception bridging as a supporting module. Only when Q3 is also positive, together with acceptable `SARR`, `STG`, and `NMC`, may D07 be promoted to a **deployable hybrid aerial-arm control** narrative.

### 4.7 Human-in-the-Loop Data Quality Ablation

Because D07 may rely on teleoperation or shared-autonomy demonstrations for warm start, we include a compact ablation on demonstration quality rather than assuming all demonstrations are equally useful. We compare **raw teleoperation warm start**, **offline-refined teleoperation warm start**, and **shared-autonomy-assisted warm start**, where the last condition uses a sequence-level copilot to denoise or complete unstable action chunks before policy initialization. The core question is whether better human-guided data primarily improves convergence speed, disturbance robustness, or online recoverability.

This ablation is interpreted conservatively. If shared-autonomy assistance only improves warm-start efficiency, its effect is counted as a stronger data-shaping recipe rather than a controller innovation. If it materially improves D2/D3 contact robustness or reduces catastrophic recovery failures when kept online, then it becomes evidence that D07 may need a bounded copilot layer at deployment time. This experiment is important because it prevents D07 from conflating three distinct claims: having better demonstrations, having a better autonomous controller, and having a better human-machine shared control interface.

### 4.9 Semantic-State Ablation for Relation-Aware Compensation

To test whether semantic relation state provides more than cosmetic structure, we compare three variants: **pixel+proprioception only**, **pixel+proprioception+local geometric relations**, and **pixel+proprioception+local geometric relations+semantic relation state**. The key question is not whether more state always improves average score, but whether semantic relation tags help the controller disambiguate base-drift viewpoint shift, target misalignment, and contact-stage instability under D2/D3 conditions. We therefore evaluate this ablation primarily on W2/W3 slices rather than on whole-episode nominal averages.

### 4.10 Disturbance-Localized Credit Ledger and Non-Promotion Audit

The first real D07 bundle should not be read as a generic benchmark table. Instead, every row must be accompanied by the disturbance-localized credit ledger `\Xi_{D07}` defined in Section 3.19 so that the reader can see not only whether a row improved, but also **where the gain first appeared**, **how far it survived**, and **which weaker explanations remain undefeated**. Concretely, each candidate row reports `(w^{+}, w^{\dagger}, s^{\dagger}, \kappa^{src}, \kappa^{dep}, \kappa^{undefeated})` alongside task score and recovery metrics.

To make the ledger executable rather than rhetorical, the first bundle must log a small set of route-facing fields for every run: `(seed, family, earliest_positive_window, latest_surviving_window, highest_disturbance_source, dominant_credit, deployment_survival, undefeated_weaker_family, sim2sim_consistency, fixed_to_coupled_promotion)`. These fields are intentionally lightweight but sufficient to determine whether an observed gain belongs to acceleration, retention, safety-shell support, relation-aware compensation, or deployable hybrid control.

This logging contract turns D07's current reviewer-facing honesty rules into a reproducible experiment protocol.

### 4.10 BC-to-Q Retention Bridge Evaluation

Motivated by Q2RL [REF: 2605.05172], we evaluate whether a BC-to-Q bridge should be interpreted as an honest anti-forgetting mechanism rather than a new control interface. We compare **warm-start PPO**, **warm-start PPO + BC action fallback**, and **warm-start PPO + BC-to-Q gated online recovery** under matched compute, matched disturbance schedules, and identical reset semantics. The central question is whether the bridge merely preserves early demonstration competence or actually improves W2/W3 disturbance robustness beyond what a weaker retention-only family can explain.

For this purpose, we log a retention tuple `R_ret = (WSR, PRS, CGU)`, where `WSR` denotes warm-start survival rate after online updates, `PRS` denotes policy-retention stability under D2/D3 perturbation, and `CGU` denotes contact-gap utilization during late-window recovery. A row is only allowed to support a stronger route if its gain survives `matched-family defeat`: if BC-to-Q only prevents catastrophic forgetting while failing to dominate simpler retention baselines on W2/W3 contact recovery, then it must be frozen as an **anti-forgetting bridge** and may not be promoted to deployable hybrid control.

### 4.11 Sequence-Copilot Retention Boundary

Motivated by DiSCo [REF: 2603.22787], we further evaluate whether sequence-level copilot assistance should remain a data-shaping aid or be treated as a persistent online dependency. We compare **raw teleoperation warm start**, **offline sequence-refined warm start**, and **bounded online sequence-copilot assistance**. The purpose is not to show that more human support always helps, but to determine whether sequence-level refinement changes only initialization quality or also materially improves disturbance-heavy W2/W3 execution when held online.

We therefore define a route-freeze boundary `\Omega_{cop}^{D07}`: DiSCo-style sequence copilots are counted by default as **sequence-level data shaping / bounded assistance support**. They are not allowed to upgrade into retention robustness, analytic projection, or deployable hybrid interface claims unless the same row also (i) preserves gains through W2/W3 windows, (ii) survives payload-bearing disturbance slices, and (iii) defeats a matched weaker family that lacks sequence-level assistance. This boundary is important because D07 otherwise risks letting cleaner demonstrations or bounded online smoothing masquerade as controller-interface innovation.

### 4.12 Platform-vs-Controller Separation Template

A positive result in this ablation is interpreted narrowly. Only if the same benefit survives deployment-oriented evaluation together with the rest of the stack may it contribute to a stronger deployment-facing claim. This keeps D07 from inflating a useful state-design choice into an oversized semantic-manipulation narrative.

Because Isaac Lab [REF: 2511.04831] standardizes reset semantics, disturbance injection, actuator wrapping, logging, and replay structure, D07 must explicitly prevent infrastructure hygiene from being over-credited as controller innovation. This rule has a direct reviewer-facing consequence. In practice, D07 must therefore report whether a gain survives after infrastructure is locked and only the learning/control interface changes. This section is important because aerial-arm experiments are unusually vulnerable to hidden platform variance, and without an infrastructure-locked interpretation rule, the paper could accidentally confuse better experimental hygiene with better control design.

After infrastructure is locked, we interpret first-round gains through a two-stage filter. This routing step therefore turns infrastructure discipline from a background engineering detail into an explicit guardrail against overclaiming.

### 4.10 First-Round Training Schedule and Honest Go/No-Go Rules

The first round follows a three-stage schedule. **S1 smoke test** runs short training jobs to validate environment reset integrity, reward boundedness, disturbance injection, logging, and whether warm-start checkpoints are actually consumed. **S2 matched-compute comparison** evaluates scratch RL, warm-start RL, and BC-to-Q-style gated warm start under identical budgets to decide whether sample-efficiency gains persist after matched compute. **S3 disturbance-promotion evaluation** introduces D2/D3 disturbance, relation-aware local compensation, and hybrid stabilization to determine whether any gain deserves promotion beyond an acceleration story.

We write three honest go/no-go rules directly into the paper draft. **GNG-1**: if warm start only reduces time-to-threshold but does not preserve `WSR/PRS` or improve late-window stability, it must be reported as a training acceleration recipe. **GNG-2**: if BC-to-Q or retention mechanisms mainly prevent catastrophic forgetting without improving `Δcontact` or `Δstabilize`, they remain anti-forgetting bridges rather than controller innovations. **GNG-3**: if hybrid control raises average success but fails to improve post-contact stabilization, disturbance survival, or deployment cost, it must be downgraded to a research-only hybrid baseline.

### 4.11 Reward-Origin Accountability for Long-Horizon Manipulation

Recent local evidence around advantage-style reward modeling suggests that D07 must explicitly separate **reward-side recovery shaping** from **controller-side disturbance survival**. We therefore define a reward-origin accountability tuple `R_rew = (AER, RCR, LRS, w^+_rew, s^\dagger_rew)`, where `AER` denotes advantage-explained recovery, `RCR` denotes recovery-credit ratio, `LRS` denotes late-recovery share, `w^+_rew` is the earliest window in which reward shaping produces measurable gain, and `s^\dagger_rew` records the highest disturbance source at which the reward-side gain remains visible. This tuple is reported alongside the controller attribution metrics instead of being folded into a single success score.

The purpose is reviewer-facing honesty. If a late-window improvement arises mainly because the reward design better credits recovery behavior, that is a meaningful result—but it should be written as a reward-accountability contribution, not silently upgraded into a control-interface contribution. D07 therefore treats reward shaping, retention bridges, relation-aware priors, and hybrid stabilization as separate explanatory axes, and requires the experiment section to state which axis actually produced the gain.

We therefore evaluate BC-to-Q gating with a retention tuple `R_ret=(WSR, PRS, CGU, w^+_ret, s^\dagger_ret)`, where `WSR` measures warm-start survival after online fine-tuning begins, `PRS` measures policy-retention stability across matched-compute checkpoints, `CGU` measures contact-window gain utilization in W2/W3, `w^+_ret` is the earliest window where retention benefit becomes visible, and `s^\dagger_ret` records the strongest disturbance source that still preserves BC-consistent gains. This accountability rule matters because Q-guided fine-tuning can look deceptively strong when it merely avoids catastrophic forgetting. ### 4.70 Infrastructure-Compatible Retention-versus-Projection Readout for the First Real Bundle

The recent local D07 anchors sharpen a practical ambiguity that the current draft still needs to resolve more explicitly: a late-window gain can look similar whether it comes from **BC-to-Q retention support** or from **analytic safety projection**, yet the paper should not let these two families co-own the same first-round claim. \[
\mathcal{R}_{proj}^{D07}=(UARR,\;CPCR,\;SAPG,\;\eta_{plat}^{proj},\;\tau_{proj}),

This paired readout matters because D07 is no longer short on plausible mechanisms; it is short on clean separations between them. By requiring `\mathcal{R}_{ret}^{D07}` and `\mathcal{R}_{proj}^{D07}` to be reported side by side with platform-compatibility terms, the first real bundle can distinguish whether D07 has merely preserved safer behavior, merely projected unsafe actions away, or genuinely moved late-window coupled-disturbance robustness on a common validated backbone.

The latest local evidence queue also suggests that D07 needs one more reviewer-facing rule before the first trustworthy table arrives: **a row is not promoted by looking strong in isolation, but by defeating the nearest weaker family on the same infrastructure and disturbance slice**. ### 4.70 Exploration-Bridge Freeze for Diffusion-Timestep-Modulated Warm Starts

This freeze matters because timestep-modulated diffusion exploration can easily look stronger than plain BC warm start while still leaving the core D07 question unresolved: does the controller survive coupled disturbance through contact and payload-bearing post-contact execution? In other words, D07 may absorb diffusion-timestep exploration, but only as a bounded bridge between imitation and robust online control—not as automatic evidence for a new interface claim.

### 4.81 Boundary-Movement Requirement beyond Reward-Shaped Late Recovery

The recent local re-read of **Bounded Extremum Seeking for RL under Distribution Shift** [REF: 2604.01142] adds one more necessary guardrail for D07: not every late-window improvement under disturbance should be credited to a stronger controller interface, even when it clearly improves robustness. The paper shows that a learned manipulation policy can be substantially stabilized at deployment time by attaching a lightweight **inference-time corrective feedback layer** that adapts online under distribution shift. For D07, this route is highly relevant because aerial-arm control will almost certainly face payload variation, contact mismatch, aerodynamic disturbance, and simulator-to-deployment residual shift. However, its most honest first interpretation is still **deployment-time correction support** rather than immediate evidence of coupled-base hybrid interface superiority.

We therefore freeze a boundary-movement requirement for any row whose strongest visible improvement could be explained by deployment-time correction. Let
\[
\Omega_{corr}^{D07}=(\delta_{ood},\;\delta_{adapt},\;\delta_{late},\;w^+_{corr},\;s^\dagger_{corr}),
\]
where `\delta_{ood}` measures robustness gain under shifted task or dynamics conditions, `\delta_{adapt}` measures whether the gain comes from explicit online corrective adaptation rather than stronger nominal control, `\delta_{late}` measures whether the improvement survives into W2/W3 instead of only stabilizing early approach, and `(w^+_{corr}, s^\dagger_{corr})` record the earliest positive window and the highest surviving disturbance-source rung. By default, gains dominated by `\Omega_{corr}^{D07}` are routed to **deployment-time correction support** and may not be promoted to retention-robustness, projection-led safety-shell superiority, or deployable hybrid control unless the same row also defeats the matched weaker family on the same substrate and clearly moves the disturbance frontier rather than merely rescuing failure locally.

This rule matters because D07 is now close to the stage where a row with stronger online correction could look deceptively similar to a true interface-improving hybrid controller. If the main win is that inference-time correction patches distribution shift or contact drift while the underlying W2/W3 payload-bearing frontier remains unchanged, then the honest narrative is still that D07 found a useful **test-time robustness bridge**. Only rows that turn this correction gain into genuine boundary movement—late-window continuity, stronger disturbance survival, and matched-family defeat—are allowed to compete for the stronger deployment-facing route.

## 5. Conclusion

D07 now has a much sharper paper contract than a generic "Isaac Lab + RL for aerial manipulation" draft. The current evidence base suggests that the real contribution is not simply to compare more controllers, but to separate **acceleration**, **retention**, **projection**, **reward-accountable recovery**, **base-locked local compensation**, and **coupled-base deployable hybrid control** into distinct claim routes with explicit promotion boundaries. In particular, recent local anchors such as ARM, Q2RL, Reactive Dexterous Grasping, and the Isaac-Lab-native infrastructure line show that late-window gains can easily come from better reward credit, safer projection, stronger BC preservation, or cleaner platform discipline rather than from a fundamentally stronger aerial-arm interface.

For that reason, the most important D07 advance at the current stage is methodological honesty. The paper now defines a minimal first real bundle, binds it to executable log schemas, distinguishes fixed-flight from reopened coupled-base evaluation, and requires ONNX Isaac→MuJoCo sim2sim consistency to be reported before any strong deployment language is used. This means the next real experiments can no longer drift into vague success storytelling: once the first trustworthy table exists, D07 must freeze to one of a small set of honest routes—**acceleration**, **retention bridge**, **safety-shell support**, **reward-shaped recovery**, **base-locked local compensation**, or **deployable hybrid control**—according to the strongest surviving evidence.
