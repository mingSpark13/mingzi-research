# Unified Cross-Embodiment Policy Transfer via Shared Geometry and Latent Action Interfaces

> 方向：D04 跨载体泛化 | 目标会议：CoRL 2027 | 状态：🔴 草稿

## Abstract

Cross-embodiment transfer is often reported as a single end-task success number, which obscures whether the real gain comes from shared task geometry, a reusable latent transition interface, or embodiment-specific residual recovery. We study this problem in the setting most relevant to mixed ground–aerial manipulation, where policies may inherit semantic and geometric structure across platforms but still fail under morphology mismatch, viewpoint drift, or aerial dynamics. Our central claim is that cross-embodiment transfer should be evaluated as a staged decision process: shared geometry must be verified before latent transfer can be credited, and shared latent must be exhausted before morphology-aware or dynamics-aware adaptation is promoted to the main explanation.

To this end, we propose a geometry-first transfer protocol built around a shared geometry packet, geometry-conditioned latent action retargeting, optional in-context embodiment residual modeling, and an explicit escalation rule that separates embodiment-conditioned compensation from dynamics-residual recovery. The framework is paired with a pre-registered evaluation tuple `(TR, IGS, LTS, MCG, DRB, VGS-xEmb, CIG, CSC, TSG, TSR, PTL, GRS, OC)` and a weakest-honest reporting discipline for mixed ground–aerial transfer. In particular, we require first-pass gains to be routed through **terrain-state completion**, **contact-interface enrichment**, and **coordination-state completion** before any upgrade to latent/policy-interface transfer or embodiment residual recovery is allowed. This design lets strong heterogeneous-pretraining baselines such as JoyAI-RA, compact geometry-grounded priors such as PokeVLA, and low-data lock-in probes such as Breaking Lock-In function not only as baselines, but as decision tests for whether additional transfer structure is scientifically necessary. We expect this protocol to provide a cleaner account of what truly transfers across embodiments, especially when moving from ground manipulation to aerial manipulation where low-frequency task intent may transfer while high-frequency stabilization remains platform-specific.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem Statement

Existing robot policies are typically trained for specific hardware, requiring full retraining when the platform changes. For researchers with both UAV and robotic arm platforms, cross-embodiment generalization is essential. Current approaches span four paradigms: action space unification, functional alignment, world model routes, and data-policy strategies—but no unified framework exists.

The key question is: **given shared geometry representations and latent action interfaces, how much cross-embodiment transfer is possible without embodiment-specific retraining?**

### 1.2 Contributions

This paper makes the following contributions:
1. **C1: Geometry-first cross-embodiment transfer protocol.** We formalize cross-embodiment transfer as a staged decision process in which shared geometry must be verified before latent transfer is credited, and latent transfer must be exhausted before embodiment-specific or dynamics-specific explanations are promoted.
2. **C2: State-completion-aware attribution for mixed ground--aerial transfer.** We introduce a reviewer-facing routing discipline that explicitly separates terrain-state completion, contact-interface enrichment, coordination-state completion, and contact-capable embodiment parameterization from stronger policy-interface or embodiment-residual claims.
3. **C3: Consumption-time claim serialization.** We propose a weakest-honest reporting protocol that ties every gain to its creation stage, first positive window, last honest consumption boundary, and matched-budget explanation tag, preventing early packet improvements from being overclaimed as full cross-embodiment success.
4. **C4: Lock-in- and prior-aware transfer auditing.** We integrate heterogeneous-pretraining saturation tests and low-data post-training lock-in controls, so that foundation-scale priors, compact geometry-grounded priors, and scarce target-side adaptation are treated as scientific decision tests rather than narrative shortcuts.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Action Space Unification

CEI achieves 82.4% transfer ratio through unified interface/representation alignment. FAAS, MOTIF, ROI-Driven approaches provide complementary perspectives on action space normalization.

### 2.2 Data Organization and Demonstration Analogies

Data Analogies [REF: 2603.06450] shows that structured paired demonstrations can outperform large unpaired heterogeneous corpora by 22.5% in real cross-embodiment transfer, indicating that embodiment shift is not only a representation problem but also a data alignment problem. This matters for our setting because a shared geometry or latent interface is only useful when demonstrations expose task-consistent correspondences across embodiments. Human-to-robot geometry anchors and simulation-generated paired trajectories should therefore be viewed as structured supervision for interface formation, rather than merely as cheap extra data.

### 2.3 Morphology-Aware Approaches

Embedding Morphology into Transformers [REF: 2603.00182] injects kinematic tokens, topology attention bias, and joint attribute conditioning, showing that explicit morphology structure improves cross-embodiment robustness. UniMorphGrasp [REF: 2602.00915] uses morphology as diffusion generator conditioning for cross-hand-type grasping. DexFormer [REF: 2502.11147] adds a particularly relevant dynamic variant: instead of relying only on static morphology descriptors, it uses history-conditioned transformers to infer embodiment-specific kinematics and dynamics online, suggesting that part of the residual transfer burden may come from time-varying embodiment constraints rather than from morphology labels alone. However, these morphology-aware gains should not be interpreted too early as proof that embodiment-specific structure is the primary transfer bottleneck. Unified Latent Space [REF: 2601.15419] is a useful counterweight here: it shows that adversarial latent projection plus cycle-consistent decoding can already support zero-shot transfer without paired data or target-domain rewards, implying that some apparent morphology gap may instead reflect the absence of a sufficiently shared latent transition space.

A complementary non-arXiv but high-value signal comes from *Demonstrate once, execute on many: Kinematic intelligence for cross-robot skill transfer* (Science Robotics, 2026). Rather than learning embodiment invariance only from data scale or latent compression, it argues that cross-robot transfer can be unlocked by explicitly factorizing manipulator topology and feasible kinematic branches, so that a single demonstrated skill is lifted into a morphology-aware but analytically structured execution rule. For D04, this paper is important because it sharpens a missing boundary in current morphology-aware work: some transfer gains may come from **kinematic feasibility priors** that are neither generic shared latent structure nor online residual adaptation. We therefore treat kinematic-intelligence-style topology priors as a separate explanatory candidate that should be tested before attributing all post-geometry gains either to latent sufficiency or to history-conditioned embodiment residuals.

### 2.4 Latent Action Interfaces

LAD [REF: 2506.14608] uses diffusion models to unify latent action spaces, achieving cross-embodiment manipulation generalization. Unified Latent Space for Humanoid [REF: 2601.15419] validates shared latent representations for multi-DOF complex platforms. LAC-WM [REF: ICLR 2026] demonstrates +46.7% gain from latent action alignment at scale. More recently, UniT [REF: 2604.19734] pushes the latent-interface view one step further by learning a unified physical language across human and humanoid data through visual anchoring and cross-reconstruction, suggesting that embodiment-agnostic intent tokens can simultaneously support policy learning and world modeling. AdaTracker [REF: 2604.20305] provides an important counterexample: if embodiment context inferred from rollout history still contributes large gains after geometry stabilization, then latent interfaces alone are not yet sufficient. PokeVLA [REF: 2604.20834], while not framed as a cross-embodiment paper, strengthens the same interface thesis from the manipulation-foundation-model side: multi-view geometry alignment and action-expert injection imply that compact action learning benefits from spatially grounded intermediate semantics before embodiment-specific execution is resolved. Together, these works suggest a default D04 interpretation rule: once shared geometry is stable, the next hypothesis should be latent-transition sufficiency, but that claim must be stress-tested against online embodiment-context baselines rather than assumed from shared latent structure alone.

### 2.5 Geometry-Centric Shared Interfaces

Point Bridge [REF: 2601.16212] shows that 3D point-based representations can serve as a cross-domain policy bridge by decoupling appearance variation from geometry-sensitive control cues. DeFM [REF: 2601.18923] further argues that depth-centric, noise-invariant representations reduce visual domain gaps and provide a more stable substrate for cross-platform transfer than RGB-only priors. OPFA / One-Policy-Fits-All [REF: 2603.14522] sharpens this line by explicitly coupling geometry-aware action latents with cross-embodiment manipulation, suggesting that shared geometry should not be treated as perception-only preprocessing, but as part of the transferable action abstraction itself. GaussFly [REF: 2604.05062] complements this line from the aerial side: 3D Gaussian field reconstruction plus contrastive visuomotor pretraining suggests that geometry-first representation learning can preserve transferable control structure before embodiment-specific compensation is introduced. XEmbodied [REF: 2604.18484], although not designed specifically for cross-embodiment transfer, reinforces the same thesis from the foundation-model side: injecting 3D geometric and physical cues into the backbone improves spatial reasoning and OOD robustness, implying that geometry-rich intermediate states are a stronger substrate for embodiment-agnostic transfer than pure 2D semantic tokens.

### 2.6 Grounded World Model Interface

Grounded World Model [REF: 2604.11751] uses training-free rendering-based action encoding for embodiment-agnostic tokenization, achieving zero-shot generalization to new platforms (xArm6) without interface retraining.

### 2.7 In-Context Embodiment Adaptation

AdaTracker [REF: 2604.20305] adds a useful variant to the D04 design space: instead of enforcing a single globally shared embodiment-invariant controller, it conditions policy execution on an inferred embodiment context extracted from trajectory history. Its embodiment context encoder and context-aware policy suggest that part of the remaining transfer gap may be recoverable through online inference of embodiment constraints rather than only through static morphology tokens. This matters for our paper because it gives a sharper falsification baseline for the shared-geometry-plus-latent thesis: if an in-context embodiment encoder still provides substantial gains after geometry stabilization and latent retargeting, then residual embodiment information has not yet been fully absorbed by the shared interface; if not, AdaTracker can be reinterpreted as a transient substitute for a more explicit geometry-conditioned latent interface.

### 2.8 Dynamics Residual and Physics Adaptation

RAFL [REF: 2603.22039] provides an important counterpoint to purely representational accounts of transfer. By learning a residual acceleration field that generalizes across soft-robot morphologies, it suggests that some transfer failures should be attributed to shared residual dynamics rather than missing semantic alignment. For D04, this motivates our escalation rule: when geometry and latent interfaces are already stable, persistent residual error should be tested against morphology- or dynamics-conditioned adapters instead of being prematurely interpreted as failure of the shared interface itself.

### 2.9 Real-to-Sim Geometry Anchors from Human Video

X-Sim [REF: 2505.07096] shows that cross-embodiment transfer can be bootstrapped from human videos without action labels by projecting observations into a shared latent space, relabeling actions in simulation, and then distilling robot policies back into real deployment. The important lesson for D04 is not only data efficiency, but also that human demonstrations can contribute transferable supervision at the level of task geometry, subgoal ordering, and contact preparation before embodiment-specific action realization is recovered. This supports our decision to treat human geometry anchors as structured interface-formation data rather than merely as cheap augmentation.

### 2.10 Shared Human-Robot Physical Intent Tokens

UniT [REF: 2604.19734] further suggests that cross-embodiment transfer can be framed as a unified physical-language problem rather than only a robot-to-robot retargeting problem. By aligning human and humanoid behavior through visual anchoring and latent intent reconstruction, it implies that transferable structure may live at the level of physical intent tokens before embodiment-specific actuation is resolved. For D04, this matters because it strengthens the case that shared latent interfaces should absorb human demonstrations as structured supervision, not as loose pretraining noise. It also sharpens the distinction between transferable intent and embodiment-specific recovery: if a token remains predictive across human, ground-robot, and aerial-manipulation settings, then later morphology or dynamics modules should be interpreted as residual execution layers rather than as the primary source of generalization.

### 2.11 Foundation VLA Priors and Negative Controls for Embodiment Bridging

JoyAI-RA 0.1 [REF: 2604.20100] is relevant to D04 not because it fully solves cross-embodiment transfer, but because it bundles explicit action-space unification with heterogeneous human, simulation, and robot data at foundation-model scale. This makes it a strong prior against which our layered interpretation should be tested: if large-scale heterogeneous pretraining plus unified action formatting already explains most transfer, then D04 should emphasize structured interface verification over inventing yet another adapter family. Conversely, if JoyAI-RA still leaves clear morphology- or dynamics-dependent residuals, it supports our decision to separate shared geometry, latent transfer, and residual recovery into distinct explanation layers.

Breaking Lock-In [REF: 2604.23121] adds a complementary but strategically important signal: even when a strong generalist VLA prior exists, low-data post-training can destroy steerability and collapse the policy onto seen objects or spatial targets. Its DeLock recipe—preserving visual grounding during post-training and adding test-time contrastive prompt guidance—suggests that some apparent cross-embodiment failure may actually be **post-training lock-in** rather than missing geometry, latent structure, or embodiment-specific compensation. For D04, this matters because small target-platform adaptation sets are exactly where such failure is most likely. We therefore treat lock-in as a distinct negative control: if transfer degrades mainly after low-data adaptation while geometry stability remains acceptable, the correct interpretation may be *steerability collapse under post-training* rather than *shared interface insufficiency*.

PokeVLA [REF: 2604.20834] strengthens this argument from a compact-manipulation foundation-model angle. Although it is not explicitly formulated as a cross-embodiment paper, its two-stage recipe—multi-view goal-aware semantics learning, geometry alignment, and action-expert injection before downstream manipulation decoding—suggests that spatially grounded intermediate semantics can absorb a large fraction of transferable structure before embodiment-specific execution is resolved. For D04, this makes PokeVLA more than a generic strong baseline: it becomes a probe for whether better geometry-conditioned compact priors can raise latent-transition sufficiency without paying the full cost of morphology-aware or dynamics-aware adaptation.

VistaBot [REF: 2604.21914] adds a fresh but highly relevant adjacent signal from view-robust manipulation. Its combination of 4D geometry estimation, view-synthesis latent extraction, and latent action learning shows that geometry-grounded latent interfaces can remain stable even when viewpoint changes substantially at test time. For D04, the value of VistaBot is not that it directly solves embodiment transfer, but that it sharpens a hidden prerequisite in our argument: if the shared geometry packet is fragile to camera relocation or observation geometry drift, then later latent-transfer gains are hard to interpret as embodiment transfer rather than merely view robustness. VistaBot therefore strengthens the decision to treat `IGS` as a first-class gate and suggests that geometry-conditioned latent interfaces should be stress-tested under viewpoint perturbation before morphology-conditioned recovery is invoked.

Grounding Sim-to-Real VLA [REF: 2603.22876] provides an orthogonal but highly practical caution for D04: once a VLA policy is trained or adapted in simulation, the strength of a claimed cross-embodiment interface still depends on whether the sim-side grounding protocol survives real-world variation. Its evidence that **fine-grained randomization**, **structured rendering realism**, and **post-training RL refinement** materially change zero-shot transfer implies that some gains attributed to embodiment transfer may actually come from better sim-grounding hygiene. For our paper, this means sim-to-real grounding quality should be audited before we conclude that a geometry packet or latent interface is inherently transferable across embodiments.

A newer signal comes from ResVLA [REF: 2604.21391], which reframes generative VLA control as refinement from an intent anchor rather than generation from pure noise. Its low-frequency intent plus high-frequency residual decomposition is not yet a direct cross-embodiment solution, but it sharpens an important D04 hypothesis: transferable structure may first emerge as a geometry- and intent-stabilized coarse transition, while embodiment-specific residuals are deferred to a later refinement stage. This is conceptually aligned with our staged interpretation rule—shared geometry first, latent transition sufficiency second, and only then embodiment- or dynamics-specific residual recovery.

LoHo-Manip [REF: 2604.21924] offers a complementary warning from long-horizon manipulation. By emphasizing trace-conditioned planning and subgoal-consistent execution over many steps, it suggests that strong multi-step rescue can improve end-task success without necessarily improving the underlying cross-embodiment interface. For D04, this matters because progress-trace quality and replanning competence should not be conflated with shared geometry or latent-transfer sufficiency. We therefore treat LoHo-Manip-style long-horizon traces as an orthogonal evaluation guard: if gains appear mainly through better multi-step repair while the embodiment interface remains fragile, the paper must report that improvement as planning rescue rather than cross-embodiment transfer.

### 2.12 Touch-Centric Embodiment Interfaces

FlexiTac [REF: 2604.28156] brings an under-discussed but potentially decisive modality into D04: a reusable tactile interface that can be mounted across different robotic embodiments and tied into both visuo-tactile learning and real-to-sim-to-real fine-tuning. While it is primarily a sensing-system paper rather than a transfer algorithm, its relevance is conceptual and infrastructural. If contact-rich transfer fails only when execution reaches touch-sensitive stages, then a shared visual geometry packet may be necessary but insufficient; a platform-reusable tactile packet may be the missing interface layer that stabilizes contact semantics across embodiments.

This matters especially for the ground-to-aerial manipulation setting, where the policy may inherit coarse intent and geometry from shared latent interfaces but still fail under contact uncertainty, slip, or payload-induced micro-instability. FlexiTac therefore suggests a new D04 reading rule: when gains appear mainly after adding standardized tactile observations, they should not be lumped into generic morphology compensation. Instead, they should be recorded as **contact-interface enrichment**, i.e., the shared interface was incomplete because it lacked a transferable contact state, not because embodiment-specific adaptation was fundamentally unavoidable.

A new local retrieval signal sharpens the same point from the knowledge side. The recent haptics survey *Haptic Sensing for Robot Manipulation: Kinesthetic and Tactile Perception* [REF: 2508.11261] indicates that contact-rich manipulation performance is often bottlenecked by the availability and quality of transferable tactile state variables rather than by arm-specific policy structure alone. For D04, we therefore treat tactile packet design as a first-class interface question: if a cross-embodiment route only improves after touch, slip, force, or kinesthetic traces are exposed, the gain should be frozen at **contact-state completion** before any stronger embodiment-aware story is allowed.

A newer local signal sharpens the same point from the design side. HandCDO [REF: 2604.27557] shows that cross-embodiment performance can also depend on whether the embodiment descriptor itself is parameterized richly enough to expose contact geometry, fingertip shape, and kinematic structure as transferable conditions. In other words, some apparent “morphology-aware gain” may actually come from giving the transfer stack a better **contact-capable embodiment parameterization**, rather than from adding a heavier adaptation module. For D04, this means tactile packetization and embodiment parameterization should be treated as two halves of the same interface question: one specifies transferable contact state at execution time, and the other specifies transferable contact affordance structure before execution begins.

### 2.13 Shared World State as a Cross-Embodiment Coordination Substrate

OmniRobotHome [REF: 2604.28197] is not a direct transfer algorithm, but it provides an important systems-level reminder for D04: some cross-embodiment failures are actually failures of **shared world-state maintenance** rather than failures of action retargeting. Its room-scale multi-camera platform stabilizes human, object, and multi-robot state inside one synchronized coordinate frame, enabling anticipatory assistance and safer shared-space interaction. For our setting, this suggests that when multiple embodiments participate in a task pipeline, a transferable policy may still look weak if the shared state packet drifts under occlusion, camera relocation, or asynchronous observation updates.

This matters because D04 increasingly interacts with multi-robot and mixed ground–aerial deployment scenarios. If transfer gains only emerge once a stable shared world frame is restored, the improvement should not be over-credited to latent action alignment or morphology compensation. Instead, it should be logged as **coordination-state completion**: the transfer stack required a stronger cross-agent state substrate before embodiment-conditioned execution could even be evaluated fairly. This gives D04 another disciplined interpretation guard, adjacent to but distinct from cross-view geometry stability and contact-interface enrichment.

### 2.14 Terrain-State Completion Before Policy-Interface Promotion

GA3T [REF: 2605.06478] sharpens an even more specific confound for mixed ground--aerial transfer: heterogeneous teams may improve not because the policy interface becomes more transferable, but because the aerial platform exposes traversability evidence, obstacle topology, and overhead terrain context that the ground embodiment never observed. In that case, the gain is best interpreted as **terrain-state completion** rather than latent-transfer or embodiment-residual improvement. This distinction is especially important for D04 because our target setting mixes cross-view perception with cross-embodiment execution; without an explicit guard, overhead scene completion can be misreported as stronger transfer.

We therefore treat terrain completion as a first-pass explanation that must be exhausted before any promotion to policy-interface transfer is allowed. Concretely, if the main gain appears after restoring overhead traversability cues, line-of-sight disambiguation, or map-level support-region visibility, the paper must first freeze that gain under **terrain-state completion**. Only after terrain completion, contact-interface enrichment, and coordination-state completion fail to explain the result do we allow upgrades to latent-transition sufficiency, policy-interface transfer, or embodiment residual recovery. This makes D04 more honest in mixed ground--aerial settings where the UAV may contribute scene-state evidence rather than a stronger transferable controller.

### 2.15 Demonstration-Translation Completion as an Upstream Confound

Recent cross-embodiment video-editing work adds an upstream confound that is easy to over-credit as transfer. Bridging the Embodiment Gap [REF: 2605.03637] shows that disentangling task latent from embodiment latent can generate embodiment-matched robot demonstration videos from human demonstrations without requiring paired cross-embodiment data. This is highly relevant to D04 because it means a route may improve not because the downstream geometry packet, latent transition interface, or embodiment-conditioned controller becomes stronger, but because the supervision presented to policy learning has already been cleaned, re-rendered, or embodiment-matched in observation space.

For our paper, this evidence motivates a strict distinction between **demonstration-translation completion** and **policy-interface sufficiency**. If a route only becomes competitive after relabeling, reenactment, simulation replay, or embodiment-specific video translation makes demonstrations more kinematically plausible, temporally coherent, or contact-aligned for the target platform, then the first honest claim is that better supervision has been created. Only after matched toggles show that the same gain survives without cleaner translated demonstrations may the route be promoted to latent-interface transfer, policy-interface transfer, or embodiment residual recovery. In other words, cross-embodiment video generation is an important bridge for D04, but it should initially be treated as an upstream data-completion mechanism rather than automatic evidence that the transfer interface itself has improved.

### 2.16 Limitations of Existing Work

(1) No unified framework systematically determines when shared geometry suffices vs. when dynamics adaptation is required.
(2) Geometry-centric representation bridges are often treated as perception-only improvements, rather than as the prerequisite gate for cross-embodiment transfer claims.
(3) Morphology injection and latent alignment are treated as competing approaches rather than complementary layers.
(4) Cross-embodiment transfer for aerial platforms (UAV + arm) remains unvalidated.
(5) Human-to-robot and robot-to-robot transfer are still frequently discussed in separate literatures, leaving open whether a single geometry-and-latent interface can bridge both sources without collapsing task-feasible physical intent.
(6) Recent in-context adaptation and large-scale VLA pretraining results suggest strong embodiment-aware priors, but they still do not tell us when those priors can be replaced by a verified shared geometry-plus-latent interface.
(7) Recent intent-anchored generative control results indicate that stable low-frequency intent and high-frequency embodiment recovery may separate naturally, but existing cross-embodiment work rarely distinguishes latent-transition sufficiency from intent-stable residual refinement.
(8) Existing evaluations usually report final transfer success after scaling data or adaptation modules, but they seldom ask whether heterogeneous-pretraining gains saturate the value of a staged transfer explanation, i.e., whether geometry verification and latent sufficiency still provide explanatory leverage once unified-action foundation models become strong enough.
(9) Current cross-embodiment evaluations still under-specify whether residual gains come from stabilized shared intent or merely from stronger high-frequency repair policies, which makes it easy to overclaim latent sufficiency when the real gain is intent-anchored residual refinement.
(10) Recent cross-view manipulation evidence suggests that geometry packets can look stable under a fixed training camera while remaining brittle under observation relocation; without explicit view-robustness checks, later gains may be misread as embodiment transfer rather than geometry packet repair.
(11) New long-horizon VLA planning results further indicate that progress-aware trace planning can rescue multi-step execution without solving embodiment transfer itself, so current evaluations still conflate long-horizon replanning competence with genuine cross-embodiment interface quality.
(12) Even within morphology-aware methods, most evaluations under-specify whether gains come from static embodiment descriptors or from online history-conditioned inference of morphology and dynamics, leaving the boundary between embodiment labels and temporal residual adaptation insufficiently tested.
(13) Existing morphology-aware comparisons rarely isolate whether the gain comes from embodiment information that is available once per platform or from history-conditioned temporal clues that only emerge online, making it difficult to compare static morphology-token methods against context-adaptive policies under a common attribution rule.
(14) Existing sim-to-real VLA evaluations rarely distinguish failures caused by weak simulation grounding hygiene from failures caused by truly missing cross-embodiment structure, which can inflate transfer claims when randomization and rendering choices are under-controlled.
(15) Contact-rich transfer remains under-instrumented: most current cross-embodiment studies assume vision-dominant interfaces, leaving it unclear when a reusable tactile packet is the missing shared state rather than morphology-aware adaptation itself.
(16) Existing cross-robot transfer studies also under-separate **kinematic feasibility priors** from generic morphology conditioning: when a policy succeeds after swapping embodiments, it is still unclear whether the gain came from a shared latent interface, from topology-aware kinematic branch selection, or from rollout-conditioned residual correction.
(17) Current evaluations also under-specify whether topology-aware gains are confined to **pre-contact feasible-branch selection** or remain beneficial after contact and under view perturbation, which makes it easy to over-promote branch-feasibility priors into broad embodiment-adaptation claims.
(18) Current cross-embodiment evaluations also under-separate failures of action/interface transfer from failures of **shared world-state maintenance** in multi-agent or occlusion-heavy settings, making coordination-state drift an unmeasured confound.
(19) Existing morphology-aware narratives still under-specify when better embodiment descriptors are merely exposing transferable contact affordance structure, rather than proving that heavier embodiment-specific adaptation is fundamentally required.
(20) Existing evaluations also rarely separate **contact-interface enrichment** from **coordination-state completion**: success can improve either because a transferable tactile/contact packet finally makes local interaction observable, or because a synchronized shared world frame prevents cross-agent or cross-view state drift, yet both are often over-credited as generic cross-embodiment adaptation gains.
(21) Existing studies rarely separate **low-data post-training lock-in** from true embodiment gaps: a policy can preserve coarse geometry stability while losing object- and spatial-steerability after tiny target-platform adaptation, so apparent transfer failure may reflect steerability collapse rather than insufficient shared geometry, latent structure, or embodiment priors.
(22) Current evaluations still under-separate **contact-capable embodiment parameterization** from heavier adaptation itself: richer hand or end-effector descriptors may improve transfer simply by exposing transferable contact affordance structure before execution begins, not because the policy has learned a deeper embodiment-specific controller.
(23) A rapidly growing line of cross-embodiment video editing and generation now shows that embodiment shift can sometimes be repaired in observation space before policy learning even begins, yet current transfer evaluations rarely ask whether later policy gains come from a stronger control interface or simply from better **demonstration translation quality**. In particular, disentangling task latent from embodiment latent may create high-quality robot demonstrations from human video without solving low-level transfer at execution time.
(24) Existing benchmarks also under-separate **demonstration-translation completion** from **policy-interface sufficiency**: a route may improve because cross-embodiment video generation supplies cleaner embodiment-matched supervision, while the downstream geometry, latent, contact, and residual stack remains unchanged. Without this distinction, D04 risks over-crediting data-generation bridges as if they were direct evidence that the shared transfer interface itself has become stronger.
(25) Current cross-embodiment evaluations also under-separate **terrain-state completion** from policy transfer itself: in heterogeneous ground–aerial teams, gains may arise because the aerial view fills blind terrain or traversability state that the ground robot never observed, rather than because the action or latent interface became more transferable. Without this distinction, cross-view terrain completion can be over-credited as embodiment adaptation.
(26) Existing multi-robot transfer studies also rarely separate **coordination-state completion** from **terrain-state completion**. A shared world frame may be synchronized, yet the joint team still lacks a task-sufficient traversability map; conversely, better terrain coverage may improve success even when the policy interface is unchanged. This boundary becomes especially important for mixed ground–aerial deployment where the UAV contributes overhead terrain disambiguation rather than direct action retargeting.
(27) Current first-pass result readouts still under-specify the **priority order between terrain-state completion and policy-interface transfer**: a route may improve because the aerial platform exposes missing traversability evidence, while geometry packets, latent interfaces, and embodiment residual modules remain functionally unchanged. Without an explicit priority rule, D04 risks over-promoting scene-state completion into a stronger transfer headline.
(28) Even when an upstream packet or prior creates a measurable gain, current evaluations rarely audit **when that gain is actually consumed** by the target embodiment; without a consumption-time boundary, early packet readiness can be overclaimed as full cross-embodiment success even when decisive uplift arrives only through later contact, coordination, or aerial residual rescue.

## 3. Method

### 3.1 Transfer Decision Protocol

```
Step 1: Shared Geometry Interface (C1)
    → If IG0 + SC0 pass: shared geometry sufficient
    → If DRB elevated: proceed to Step 2

Step 2: Latent Action Alignment (C2)
    → Diffusion-based latent retargeting
    → If MCG not dominant: shared latent sufficient

Step 3: Morphology Compensation (C3)
    → Only when DRB remains high after Step 2
    → Explicit morphology injection

Step 4: Dynamics Residual (C4)
    → Physics adapter for platform-specific dynamics
```

### 3.2 Shared Geometry Interface (C1)

We instantiate C1 as a geometry-first transfer gate rather than a direct action decoder. Given multi-view RGB-D observations or geometry-recoverable monocular streams, the source platform is mapped into a shared scene-centric representation that preserves target-object geometry, pre-contact pose candidates, and admissible contact regions. Concretely, the interface packet contains a task geometry state, contact affordance tags, a phase token, and embodiment capability bounds, so that downstream transfer decisions are made on a verified geometric substrate instead of raw appearance features.

This design is motivated by three complementary observations in prior work. Point Bridge [REF: 2601.16212] shows that 3D geometric representations can bridge cross-domain policy transfer better than appearance-coupled features. DeFM [REF: 2601.18923] indicates that depth-centric representations reduce spurious visual variation and improve sim-to-real stability. OPFA [REF: 2603.14522] adds a crucial control-facing lesson: geometry-aware latent actions can already absorb a meaningful fraction of embodiment variation before any explicit morphology compensation is enabled, implying that geometry verification should be treated as part of action-interface formation rather than as a perception-only front-end. GaussFly [REF: 2604.05062] further suggests that geometry-preserving scene reconstruction can retain transferable control structure for aerial visuomotor policies. VistaBot [REF: 2604.21914] now makes the gate even more concrete: if 4D geometry estimation and synthesized latent views are required to maintain manipulation robustness under camera displacement, then any D04 claim about embodiment transfer should first verify that its geometry packet survives cross-view perturbation rather than only nominal embodiment swaps. Therefore, our protocol treats shared geometry as a prerequisite gate: if the geometry packet is unstable under viewpoint, morphology, or sensor perturbation, later gains from latent alignment or morphology compensation cannot be credited as true cross-embodiment transfer.

We further refine the packet with a **contact-interface slot**, a paired **coordination-state slot**, an explicit **terrain-state slot**, and an explicit **contact-capable embodiment-parameterization slot**. In addition to pose- and phase-level geometry, the shared state may optionally contain platform-reusable tactile or contact summaries such as pressure onset, slip likelihood, and contact persistence. This extension is motivated by FlexiTac [REF: 2604.28156], which suggests that a low-cost standardized tactile layer can be mounted across embodiments and reused in real-to-sim-to-real learning pipelines. For D04, the implication is sharp: when cross-embodiment transfer fails primarily after contact, the missing ingredient may be an incomplete shared interface rather than insufficient morphology-aware adaptation. We therefore treat tactile/contact packetization as an optional completion of C1, not as a late-stage embodiment-specific patch.

The coordination-state slot captures whether the local embodiment packet is grounded inside a stable shared world frame across agents, views, or asynchronous observations. OmniRobotHome [REF: 2604.28197] motivates this addition by showing that synchronized multi-camera and multi-robot world-state maintenance can be a prerequisite for fair downstream control evaluation. In our protocol, this means failures caused by state-frame drift, stale cross-agent object pose, or occlusion-induced desynchronization are routed to **coordination-state completion** rather than being miscounted as latent-interface insufficiency or morphology-conditioned execution weakness. Put differently, contact-interface enrichment answers whether the policy can *feel and preserve contact semantics*, while coordination-state completion answers whether all participating embodiments are still acting on the *same world state*.

The terrain-state slot is introduced specifically for heterogeneous ground–aerial teams. GA3T [REF: 2605.06478] suggests that part of the apparent “cross-embodiment gain” may come from the aerial platform completing blind traversability state for the ground robot through overhead coverage, thermal cues, and complementary viewpoint geometry. In our protocol, this information is not credited to action transfer by default. Instead, when performance improves mainly because the UAV resolves occluded terrain layout, traversable corridors, or obstacle support regions that the ground platform could not observe, we freeze the gain as **terrain-state completion**. This prevents cross-view scene completion from being overclaimed as stronger latent transfer or morphology adaptation. The key distinction is that coordination-state completion aligns all embodiments to a common frame, whereas terrain-state completion determines whether that shared frame already contains task-sufficient traversability content.

Finally, the embodiment-parameterization slot records transferable structural descriptors that matter specifically for contact affordance realization, such as fingertip geometry, local curvature, reachable contact family, or end-effector compliance class. HandCDO [REF: 2604.27557] motivates this refinement by suggesting that richer parametric embodiment descriptions can expose transferable contact affordance structure before any heavy adaptation module is activated. This gives D04 a stricter routing rule: if performance rises after adding a better embodiment descriptor but before online residual adaptation materially helps, the gain should first be frozen as **contact-capable embodiment parameterization** or **contact affordance exposure**, not immediately promoted to a broad embodiment-aware controller story.


### 3.3 Latent Action Alignment (C2)

On top of the verified geometry packet, we learn a shared latent action interface that factors out platform-specific actuation while preserving task-level transition semantics. Following the intuition of LAD and related latent transfer methods, source-domain trajectories are projected into a common latent action manifold, while lightweight platform-conditioned decoders retarget latent transitions into embodiment-specific controls. The key hypothesis is not that all embodiments share identical low-level actions, but that they can share a sufficiently compact latent transition structure once geometry and task phase have been stabilized.

We further extend this design toward a unified physical-language view inspired by UniT [REF: 2604.19734]. Instead of treating latent action tokens as robot-only surrogates, we let visual consequences anchor the latent interface, so that human demonstrations, ground-robot rollouts, and aerial-manipulation trajectories can all be compared through their induced geometry transition and contact evolution. PokeVLA [REF: 2604.20834] provides an additional recent signal that spatially grounded semantics and action-specialist routing can be profitably inserted before full action decoding, which supports our choice to keep the latent interface explicitly geometry-conditioned rather than purely command-conditioned. In the D04 narrative, this means compact VLA priors are not just downstream baselines: they become evidence that stronger spatial grounding can raise the fraction of transfer gain already explained at the latent-interface stage.

Our decision protocol therefore uses latent alignment as the default second stage after geometry verification. If latent retargeting already explains most transfer gain and morphology compensation remains marginal, we freeze the narrative at shared geometry plus latent sufficiency. Only when residual errors remain strongly correlated with embodiment-dependent kinematics or dynamics do we escalate to explicit morphology-conditioned compensation or physics adaptation.

### 3.4 Human-to-Robot Geometry Anchors and Physical Intent Tokens

To absorb cheap human data without overstating its transferability, we introduce a geometry-anchor training view. Human demonstrations are first converted into task geometry states, contact-affordance candidates, and phase-consistent visual consequences; only then are they paired with robot trajectories in the shared latent interface. This prevents the system from confusing human-specific motion style with transferable robot skill, while still exploiting human demonstrations as dense supervision for pre-contact layout, object-relative motion, and subgoal ordering.

Operationally, this module serves two purposes. First, it turns human videos into low-cost anchors for the shared geometry packet, improving cross-domain data coverage before any target embodiment data are collected. Second, it provides a concrete test of whether a unified geometry-plus-latent interface can bridge both human and robotic sources. If gains from human anchors vanish once geometry is stabilized, they are counted as data diversity benefits rather than interface evidence; if they persist through latent retargeting, they strengthen the claim that embodiment-agnostic physical intent is a meaningful intermediate variable. This design is directly motivated by X-Sim [REF: 2505.07096], which shows that real-to-sim-to-real relabeling can turn action-free human videos into useful cross-embodiment supervision by recovering robot-compatible action traces in simulation rather than forcing direct motion imitation.

### 3.5 In-Context Embodiment Residual Modeling

Recent work such as AdaTracker [REF: 2604.20305] suggests that embodiment-specific residual information can sometimes be inferred online from rollout history rather than injected only through static morphology tokens. We therefore include an optional embodiment-context residual module that reads recent trajectory history and predicts whether remaining transfer error is better explained by embodiment-conditioned constraints, contact timing drift, or platform-dependent control bandwidth limits. This module is intentionally positioned as a residual explainer rather than the primary transfer mechanism: it is only enabled after geometry and latent-transfer stages have already been evaluated.

This design gives D04 a cleaner falsification route. If the context residual module contributes substantial gains after latent retargeting, then our paper must explicitly acknowledge that shared geometry plus latent interface is not yet sufficient. If its gains remain marginal, AdaTracker-style context adaptation can be reinterpreted as a useful diagnostic baseline rather than a necessary component of the final transfer stack. For aerial manipulation in particular, we will log whether the inferred context mainly captures morphology mismatch or delayed stabilization dynamics; only the former is allowed to count toward cross-embodiment residual recovery, while the latter is reassigned to dynamics-residual burden.

We further reserve a refinement-style interpretation slot inspired by ResVLA [REF: 2604.21391]. If the trajectory history mainly helps reconstruct a coarse intent anchor while the remaining benefit comes from high-frequency correction, then the residual branch should be interpreted as **intent-conditioned refinement** rather than evidence that the shared interface itself failed. This gives D04 a more disciplined split between transferable low-frequency structure and embodiment-specific high-frequency recovery.

A stronger negative-control baseline is heterogeneous-scale pretraining. JoyAI-RA 0.1 [REF: 2604.20100] indicates that explicit action-space unification plus large heterogeneous corpora may already absorb a non-trivial fraction of embodiment variance before any online residual modeling is enabled. We therefore treat context residual modeling as justified only when it explains gains that are still left after geometry stabilization, latent retargeting, and heterogeneous-pretraining baselines have all been accounted for. In other words, a context encoder should not be credited for rescuing weaknesses that can already be removed by better shared priors.

A final attribution rule is needed inside the morphology-aware family itself. When a history-conditioned model such as DexFormer [REF: 2502.11147] outperforms static morphology-token baselines, we do not immediately interpret the gain as stronger embodiment priors. Instead, the gain is first tested against a temporal-residual explanation: if the improvement concentrates in delayed contact alignment, stabilization, or rollout-dependent bandwidth mismatch, it is recorded as **temporal embodiment residual recovery** rather than as evidence that static morphology descriptors are intrinsically insufficient. Separately, when a topology-aware analytical prior such as kinematic intelligence improves transfer, we record that gain under **kinematic feasibility prior** rather than merging it into either latent sufficiency or history-conditioned residual adaptation. This prevents D04 from collapsing online embodiment inference, static morphology conditioning, topology-aware feasibility priors, and dynamics adaptation into a single undifferentiated “morphology-aware” bucket.

### 3.6 Real-to-Sim-to-Real Geometry Anchoring for Heterogeneous Sources

To make human data and heterogeneous robot trajectories usable within the same transfer stack, we introduce a real-to-sim-to-real geometry anchoring procedure. Following the high-level lesson of X-Sim [REF: 2505.07096], raw human videos are not treated as policy supervision directly; instead, they are first projected into geometry-consistent task states and phase-aligned visual consequences, then re-instantiated in simulation or proxy embodiments to recover action supervision compatible with the latent interface. This gives us a bridge from cheap human observations to embodiment-aware robot supervision without forcing the model to imitate human motion style.

For D04, the value of this module is methodological rather than merely data-augmentation-oriented. It lets us test whether the proposed geometry packet is truly embodiment-agnostic across human, ground, and aerial sources. If gains from human anchors disappear once geometry and latent alignment are stabilized, they should be counted as coverage benefits only. If they persist, they provide evidence that the shared interface is capturing transferable physical intent rather than overfitting to robot-specific command distributions.

### 3.7 Escalation Rule from Shared Latent to Dynamics Residual

Our paper logic now enforces a one-way escalation order: shared geometry must be established before latent transfer claims are made, and shared latent must be exhausted before embodiment-specific compensation is promoted to the main explanation. Concretely, once the geometry packet passes interface stability checks, latent retargeting becomes the default explanation layer. Morphology-aware adapters are only upgraded when they provide repeatable gains beyond latent transfer, and physics-aware guidance is only upgraded when the remaining error pattern is clearly dominated by embodiment-dependent dynamics such as payload swing, underactuation, or delayed stabilization.

This ordering matters especially for aerial manipulation. Prior aerial VLA evidence indicates that visual-semantic priors may transfer well while platform dynamics remain the dominant failure source. Therefore, D04 should not frame every residual error as proof that morphology tokens are missing. Instead, our method explicitly separates morphology-conditioned improvements from dynamics-residual recovery, allowing the paper to conclude either that shared geometry plus latent interface is already sufficient for most transfer, or that the true bottleneck has shifted to platform-specific dynamics adaptation.

### 3.8 Data-First Interface Formation

We additionally treat structured cross-embodiment data organization as part of the method rather than as a passive experimental choice. Inspired by Data Analogies [REF: 2603.06450], our training pipeline prioritizes paired or analogy-preserving demonstrations where source and target embodiments share task scene, object configuration, and subgoal order, even when low-level motions differ. The purpose is to stabilize correspondence at the geometry packet and latent-transition level before the model is asked to infer embodiment-specific control mappings.

This design is especially important for mixed human, ground-robot, and aerial-manipulation corpora. Unstructured aggregation can inflate nominal diversity while weakening cross-embodiment correspondences, causing later morphology adapters to absorb what is fundamentally a data alignment problem. By contrast, geometry anchors, robot-to-robot analogies, and simulation-generated paired trajectories provide explicit supervision for interface formation, making the later escalation from latent sufficiency to morphology or dynamics adaptation more interpretable.

### 3.9 Terrain-Coordination-Contact Routing before Policy-Interface Promotion

We now make the mixed ground--aerial routing rule explicit at the method level. Before any result is promoted to shared latent transfer, policy-interface transfer, or embodiment residual recovery, the system must first test whether the apparent gain is better explained by **terrain-state completion**, **coordination-state completion**, **contact-interface enrichment**, or **contact-capable embodiment parameterization**. This ordering is motivated by recent local anchors that expose four different pre-transfer confounds. GA3T [REF: 2605.06478] shows that an aerial platform can improve success simply by revealing traversability structure, obstacle topology, or overhead support-region visibility that the ground embodiment never observed. OmniRobotHome [REF: 2604.28197] shows that multi-agent or multi-view systems may fail because embodiments no longer act on the same synchronized world state. FlexiTac [REF: 2604.28156] shows that a reusable tactile packet can rescue transfer only because contact onset, slip tendency, or persistence finally become observable. HandCDO [REF: 2604.27557] shows that richer embodiment descriptors can expose transferable contact affordance structure before any heavy adaptation module becomes necessary.

Operationally, we treat these four explanations as a reviewer-facing routing stack attached to every first-pass result row. Let the auxiliary tuple be `(TSG, TSR, CSC, CIG, CEP, source_slot)`, where `TSG` denotes terrain-state gain, `TSR` terrain-state retention under matched perturbations, `CSC` coordination-state completion, `CIG` contact-interface gain, `CEP` contact-capable embodiment-parameterization gain, and `source_slot ∈ {terrain, coordination, contact, descriptor, mixed}` records the weakest upstream cause that still explains the improvement. A route may only be promoted beyond this stack when the gain survives matched controls that remove overhead terrain revelation, restore shared world-state synchronization, withhold tactile/contact packet fields, or replace richer structural descriptors with minimal ones. If the result collapses under any of these tests, the honest claim must stop at the corresponding weaker explanation.

This routing block matters because D04 increasingly shares deployment boundaries with D06-style aerial execution. In mixed ground--aerial tasks, a policy can look more transferable even when the real win is only that the UAV filled a blind terrain sector, repaired stale shared state, or exposed a missing contact affordance. By writing the terrain/coordination/contact routing stack directly into the method, we force D04 to earn stronger transfer language only after scene-state and interface-completion confounds are exhausted. In effect, Section 3 now makes the same promise that later Results must obey: **state completion before policy transfer, and policy transfer before embodiment residuals**.


### 3.9 Heterogeneous-Scale Saturation as a Decision Test

We explicitly position large-scale heterogeneous pretraining as a **decision test**, not just a stronger baseline. Foundation models such as JoyAI-RA [REF: 2604.20100] combine web data, egocentric human video, simulation rollouts, and robot trajectories under unified action formatting, which means they may absorb part of the transfer gain that earlier papers would attribute to handcrafted cross-embodiment structure. Our method therefore asks a sharper question: after geometry verification, does staged latent transfer still explain additional gain beyond scale, or has the problem largely collapsed into better heterogeneous pretraining plus minimal residual adaptation?

This matters for paper framing. If strong heterogeneous pretraining already saturates transfer once geometry is stable, then D04 should emphasize **measurement, attribution, and interface auditing** rather than proposing an unnecessarily heavy transfer stack. If scale does not saturate the gain, then the staged ladder remains justified and the remaining error can be more cleanly assigned to latent insufficiency, embodiment-conditioned residuals, or platform dynamics. In other words, JoyAI-RA-style scale is part of the scientific protocol because it helps determine whether the geometry→latent→residual decomposition is explanatory necessity or merely architectural preference.

### 3.10 Terrain-First Reporting Rule for Mixed Ground--Aerial Transfer

For mixed ground--aerial settings, we introduce a terrain-first reporting rule before any embodiment-transfer headline is allowed. Let the first-pass interpretation tuple be
`M_D04=(IGS, VGS-xEmb, TSG, TSR, CIG, CSC, LTS, KFG, HIG, DRB, B, τ*)`,
where `TSG` denotes terrain-state gain, `TSR` denotes terrain-state residual after overhead completion, `CIG` denotes contact-interface gain, `CSC` denotes coordination-state completion, `LTS` denotes latent-transition sufficiency, `KFG` denotes kinematic-feasibility gain, `HIG` denotes history-conditioned embodiment gain, `DRB` denotes dynamics-residual burden, `B` is the matched adaptation budget, and `τ*` is the weakest honest claim tag.

The interpretation priority is fixed as follows:
`shared-geometry repair → terrain-state completion → contact-interface enrichment → coordination-state completion → latent-transition sufficiency → kinematic-feasibility prior → history-conditioned embodiment residual → dynamics-residual burden`.
If aerial support mainly resolves traversability ambiguity, hidden support regions, or obstacle-layout uncertainty, the result must first be frozen as **terrain-state completion** even when downstream control success increases substantially. Likewise, if gains appear only after tactile/contact packet completion or multi-agent state-frame synchronization, they are recorded as **contact-interface enrichment** or **coordination-state completion** before any stronger transfer claim is considered.

This rule is deliberately conservative. It prevents D04 from promoting overhead scene completion or shared-state repair into a stronger policy-interface story. Only when terrain, contact, and coordination explanations are exhausted under matched-budget controls do we allow the result to rise to latent-interface transfer or embodiment-residual recovery. In practice, mixed ground--aerial success should therefore be reported first as the weakest state-completion explanation consistent with the data, not as a direct proof of cross-embodiment controller superiority.

### 3.11 Low-Data Post-Training Lock-In as a Matched-Budget Negative Control

We further treat low-data target-side post-training as a **matched-budget negative control** rather than as a free transfer booster. Breaking Lock-In [REF: 2604.23121] indicates that a strong VLA prior can preserve coarse geometry alignment while still collapsing object-level and spatial steerability after tiny adaptation sets. This failure mode is especially relevant for D04 because cross-embodiment evaluation often happens exactly in the regime where only a small amount of target-platform correction is affordable.

Accordingly, every adaptation-bearing route in our D04 stack must report not only task gain but also a paired lock-in tuple `(PTL, GRS)`, where `PTL` measures post-training lock-in severity and `GRS` measures grounding-retention stability under unseen object-location, viewpoint, or target-frame perturbation. A route is only allowed to upgrade the narrative beyond `shared geometry + latent sufficiency` if its marginal gain survives matched adaptation budget comparison **without** materially increasing `PTL` or decreasing `GRS`. If success rises mainly after tiny target-side fine-tuning but the model loses steerability, the correct interpretation is **adaptation-induced steerability collapse risk** rather than stronger embodiment transfer.

This rule also clarifies the relationship among compact geometry-grounded priors, heterogeneous-scale priors, and embodiment-conditioned residual modules. A compact prior that preserves low-data steerability may be scientifically stronger than an embodiment-aware route with a slightly higher raw score but elevated lock-in. Conversely, if static morphology descriptors or history-conditioned residual branches only become competitive after target-side low-data post-training, D04 must record that dependence explicitly and prevent those routes from silently borrowing credit from adaptation fragility. In this way, low-data adaptation is audited as a resource-constrained stress test of transfer honesty, not as an unqualified sign of better cross-embodiment intelligence.

### 3.9 Demonstration-Translation Completion as a Method-Level Upstream Gate

We now explicitly lift **demonstration-translation completion** into the method stack rather than leaving it only as an experiment-side caveat. Recent cross-embodiment video-generation and reenactment work suggests that a route may improve before any downstream transfer interface changes, simply because human or source-embodiment demonstrations are translated into a more kinematically plausible, contact-aligned, or embodiment-matched supervision stream for the target platform. In that regime, the downstream policy may look more transferable even though geometry packet quality, latent-transition structure, and embodiment-conditioned execution modules are unchanged. For D04, this matters because mixed human, ground-robot, and aerial-manipulation corpora increasingly rely on replay, relabeling, reenactment, or embodiment-matched video generation to create usable supervision.

Accordingly, we treat demonstration translation as an upstream gate with its own reviewer-facing attribution tag rather than as an invisible preprocessing detail. Let `DTC` denote the incremental gain obtained by improving embodiment-matched demonstration rendering, replay, or relabeling while holding the downstream geometry/latent/contact stack fixed. If a route becomes competitive mainly because translated demonstrations become more physically feasible, temporally coherent, or contact-compatible for the target embodiment, then the first honest claim is **demonstration-translation completion**, not stronger latent-interface transfer or embodiment-aware adaptation. This places D04 on a stricter causal ladder: some gains come from making the *scene* more observable, some from making the *supervision* more embodiment-matched, and only the residual after those two are exhausted may be credited to a stronger transfer interface.

Operationally, this gate sits between state/interface completion and stronger policy-transfer claims. The shortest-honest order therefore becomes
`terrain/coordination/contact completion → demonstration-translation completion → latent/policy-interface transfer → embodiment residual recovery`.
A route may only be promoted beyond `DTC` when matched toggles show that the gain survives after translated or reenacted demonstrations are removed, degraded, or budget-matched against raw-source supervision. If the improvement collapses once this upstream supervision advantage is neutralized, D04 must report that the route mainly created **better target-compatible supervision** rather than a stronger cross-embodiment control interface. This is especially important for mixed ground--aerial settings, where cleaner translated demonstrations can otherwise be mistaken for stronger geometry transfer or stronger embodiment-conditioned execution.

### 3.10 Prompt-Orchestrated Residual Routing

CAPO [REF: 2602.01040] suggests that part of the apparent embodiment gap may actually come from dynamically changing domain factors—viewpoint, illumination, temporal context, and observation aliasing—rather than from embodiment structure itself. We therefore add a lightweight prompt-orchestrated residual router on top of the geometry packet. Instead of treating prompt selection as a separate foundation-model trick, we use it as a routing layer that predicts whether the current residual should be assigned to geometry instability, latent-transition insufficiency, or embodiment-conditioned recovery.

This module matters because D04 currently risks over-attributing every post-transfer failure to morphology or dynamics. In practice, if residual error drops after dynamic prompt orchestration while morphology-aware modules remain unchanged, then the correct interpretation is not embodiment compensation but domain-factor suppression. For aerial manipulation this is especially important: camera-FOV drift, partial occlusion, or lighting-induced perception shifts should not be counted as evidence that the cross-embodiment interface failed. Prompt orchestration therefore acts as a guardrail between representation cleanup and true embodiment-specific adaptation.

### 3.11 Long-Horizon Trace Rescue as a Non-Transfer Guard

LoHo-Manip [REF: 2604.21924] highlights a failure mode that is especially dangerous for D04 narrative inflation: a system can gain large end-task improvements by better progress-aware replanning and trace-conditioned subgoal recovery, even when the underlying cross-embodiment interface has not improved much. We therefore explicitly treat long-horizon trace rescue as a **non-transfer guard** rather than as positive evidence for embodiment generalization. If a method mainly succeeds by repeatedly repairing subgoal execution over time, then that gain should be attributed to planning rescue unless the same method also preserves geometry stability, latent-transition sufficiency, and low onboarding cost under embodiment transfer stress.

Operationally, this means every D04 evaluation that includes multi-step tasks must log whether the dominant improvement comes from (i) shared geometry stabilization, (ii) latent retargeting, (iii) intent-anchored residual refinement, or (iv) long-horizon trace rescue. This additional guard is important for the D04–D06 boundary: D06-style progress-trace or recovery logic may be highly useful in practice, but D04 should only claim cross-embodiment transfer when the shared interface itself remains strong even before multi-step rescue is turned on.

## 4. Experiments

### 4.1 Setup

**Platforms**: ground fixed-base arm, mobile manipulation proxy, and UAV-manipulator target platform.
**Tasks**: approach-and-align, pre-grasp positioning, pick-and-lift, and short-horizon transport.

**Transfer sources**:
- robot-to-robot paired demonstrations,
- human geometry anchors for pre-contact layout and subgoal ordering,
- optional simulation-generated paired trajectories.

**Metrics**:
- **TR**: zero-shot transfer ratio on the target embodiment,
- **IGS**: interface geometry stability under viewpoint/sensor perturbation,
- **LTS**: latent transition sufficiency, measuring how much gain is explained by shared latent retargeting before embodiment-specific compensation,
- **MCG**: morphology compensation gain,
- **DRB**: dynamics residual burden,
- **VGS-xEmb**: a cross-view robustness companion metric adapted from VistaBot's View Generalization Score [REF: 2604.21914], used to verify that geometry stability is not an artifact of fixed camera placement,
- **CIG**: contact-interface gain, measuring the additional benefit of a reusable tactile/contact packet once geometry and latent alignment are already present,
- **CSC**: coordination-state completion gain, measuring how much performance recovers once all embodiments share a synchronized world-state substrate,
- **PTL**: post-training lock-in score, quantifying how much target-platform low-data adaptation collapses steerability while nominal geometry stability remains acceptable,
- **OC**: onboarding cost in target-embodiment data, human correction rounds, and adaptation compute.

### 4.2 Main Results

We will report results in a staged manner that matches the transfer decision protocol rather than only presenting a final success rate. The first question is whether the shared geometry packet alone stabilizes transferable task structure across embodiments; if not, later gains must not be over-claimed as cross-embodiment generalization. The second question is whether latent retargeting already captures most improvement once geometry is stabilized, especially when adding human geometry anchors from real-to-sim-to-real pipelines such as X-Sim [REF: 2505.07096].

We further predefine a stronger baseline family for context-conditioned adaptation. In addition to direct transfer, geometry-only transfer, latent retargeting, and morphology-aware compensation, we include an AdaTracker-style in-context embodiment adaptation variant [REF: 2604.20305] that predicts embodiment constraints from history. This baseline is critical for determining whether our proposed shared interface actually removes the need for online embodiment inference, or merely competes with it under favorable conditions.

We additionally freeze two new interpretation channels that must be checked before any later-stage embodiment claim is allowed. The first is **contact-interface enrichment**: if performance only rises materially once a reusable tactile/contact packet is added, then the scientifically honest conclusion is that the shared interface was incomplete at contact time. The second is **coordination-state completion**: if performance mainly improves once all embodiments act inside a synchronized shared world-state substrate, then the earlier failure should be attributed to state maintenance drift rather than to weak latent transfer or missing morphology adaptation. In both cases, the gain must be reported as interface completion, not as generic embodiment intelligence.

Accordingly, the first-pass staged table for D04 is extended from the usual `geometry → latent → morphology → dynamics` ladder into the finer sequence `geometry-only`, `+ cross-view stabilization`, `+ latent retargeting`, `+ tactile/contact packet`, `+ coordination-state completion`, `+ static morphology descriptor`, `+ history-conditioned embodiment residual`, and `+ dynamics adapter`. This finer decomposition is important for mixed ground–aerial transfer because it lets us distinguish four superficially similar but scientifically different outcomes: geometry packet repair, contact packet completion, world-state synchronization, and genuine embodiment-conditioned recovery.

### 4.2.11 Minimal Attribution Matrix for First-Pass Reporting

To prevent first-pass results from collapsing heterogeneous gains into a single transfer narrative, every headline row must be accompanied by a minimal attribution matrix
\[
A = (s^*, \Delta W_0, \Delta W_1, \Delta W_2, C_0, C_1, C_2, B, \tau),
\]
where \(s^*\) is the earliest stage that creates a measurable gain, \(\Delta W_0, \Delta W_1, \Delta W_2\) denote gain increments at the pre-contact feasible-branch window, contact-interface completion window, and post-contact delayed-consumption window, \(C_0, C_1, C_2\) record which explanatory family remains honest at each window, \(B\) is the matched history-budget tuple, and \(\tau\) is the weakest honest tag allowed for that row. The purpose of this matrix is not additional bookkeeping for its own sake; it is a hard stop against promoting early-window feasibility or large-budget rescue into broad cross-embodiment claims.

The explanatory family slots are frozen as follows. \(C_0\) may take values from \{shared geometry interface, latent-transition sufficiency, kinematic-feasibility prior\}; \(C_1\) may additionally expose \{contact-interface enrichment, coordination-state completion, static morphology prior\}; and \(C_2\) may additionally expose \{history-budgeted residual recovery, dynamics-residual burden relief\}. This ordering encodes a scientific discipline: a gain that first appears at \(W_0\) is not allowed to skip directly to a late-stage embodiment-adaptation headline unless it survives later windows under matched budget and without being better explained by contact-interface or dynamics-residual routes.

### 4.2.12 Shortest Honest Reading Path for First-Pass Tables

Given the matrix above, every first-pass result row must be interpreted by the shortest honest reading path. If the dominant gain appears in \(W_0\) and weakens once contact is introduced, the default reading freezes at **kinematic-feasibility prior** or **shared geometry plus latent sufficiency**, depending on whether topology-aware branch selection is required. If the gain only becomes visible after contact state is added, but does not persist through delayed-consumption stress, the default reading freezes at **contact-interface enrichment** or **coordination-state completion** rather than embodiment transfer. Only when gains remain positive through \(W_2\) under matched history budget and without being absorbed by dynamics-residual explanations may the row be upgraded to **embodiment-aware residual recovery**.

This shortest-path discipline is especially important for mixed ground–aerial settings. A transfer stack may look strong simply because the source platform finds a feasible pre-contact branch more often, or because a reusable tactile packet finally exposes slip and contact persistence, or because a synchronized shared world frame prevents cross-agent state drift. All three cases are scientifically valuable, but none should be overstated as broad cross-embodiment generalization. The first-pass table must therefore answer three questions in order: **where was the gain created, where was it actually consumed, and what is the weakest honest tag that still survives after matched-budget controls?**

### 4.2.13 First-Pass Results Paragraph Template under the Minimal Attribution Matrix

We further freeze the writing order of the first-pass Results paragraph into four short blocks: **S0-S1 geometry/interface formation**, **S2-S3 latent and branch-feasibility attribution**, **S4 contact/coordination completion**, and **S5 residual or dynamics escalation**. The first block is only allowed to answer whether the shared geometry packet and interface stability gates are passed. The second block is only allowed to answer whether latent-transition sufficiency or topology-aware feasible-branch selection already explains most gains. The third block is reserved for cases where contact-interface enrichment or coordination-state completion contributes the decisive improvement. The final block may discuss embodiment-aware residual recovery or dynamics-residual burden only after the previous three blocks fail to absorb the gain.

This template intentionally biases the paper toward conservative interpretation. In particular, first-pass prose is forbidden from jumping directly from better task success to embodiment-aware adaptation without explicitly reporting the matrix path `(s^*, \Delta W_0, \Delta W_1, \Delta W_2, B, \tau)`. In practice, this means our first results section will read less like a leaderboard summary and more like a staged causal audit of what actually transferred across embodiments.

### 4.2.15 Terrain/Contact/Coordination vs. Transfer: A Shortest-Honest Four-Row Table

To make the first-pass interpretation executable rather than rhetorical, we freeze a minimal four-row reporting table for mixed ground--aerial transfer:
`R0 geometry+latent`, `R1 + terrain completion`, `R2 + contact/coordination completion`, and `R3 + embodiment residual / dynamics adapter`.
Each row must report
`(TSG, TSR, CIG, CSC, LTS, KFG, HIG, DRB, PTL, GRS, source_view, weakest_tag)`
under matched budget. Here `source_view ∈ {ground-only, aerial-assisted, synchronized-multiagent}` records where the additional state evidence actually came from, and `weakest_tag ∈ {geometry, terrain, contact, coordination, latent, kinematic, embodiment, dynamics}` freezes the weakest honest explanation still consistent with the controls.

The reading rule is deliberately strict. If `R1-R0` is positive mainly because overhead sensing resolves traversability ambiguity or hidden support regions, the gain must stop at **terrain-state completion** even if task success rises sharply. If `R2-R1` becomes positive only after tactile/contact packet exposure or synchronized world-state maintenance, the result must stop at **contact-interface enrichment** or **coordination-state completion**. Only when `R3-R2` remains positive after terrain, contact, and coordination completion are already active do we allow the paper to discuss **history-conditioned embodiment residuals** or **dynamics-residual burden relief**. This shortest-honest table turns GA3T-style aerial scene completion, FlexiTac-style contact recovery, and JoyAI-RA-style strong heterogeneous priors into concrete reviewer-facing controls rather than loose narrative caveats.

### 4.2.16 First-Pass Claim-Freezing Rule under Strong Heterogeneous Priors

We also freeze a claim-order rule for the strongest modern baselines. When a heterogeneous-scale prior such as JoyAI-RA already lifts `IGS` and `LTS`, the paper is not allowed to jump directly to an embodiment-aware headline unless the remaining gain survives three audits simultaneously: `(i)` terrain/contact/coordination completion has already been exhausted, `(ii)` low-data post-training does not increase `PTL` or reduce `GRS`, and `(iii)` the residual gain is not localized only to delayed stabilization windows. If any of these conditions fails, the claim must be downgraded to the weakest surviving tag, e.g., **shared latent sufficiency**, **terrain-state completion**, or **dynamics-residual burden**.

This rule matters because mixed ground--aerial transfer can look deceptively strong once a large prior and an aerial viewpoint are both available. In that regime, a better first result may simply mean that the model inherited coarse intent from heterogeneous pretraining while the UAV repaired missing terrain state and the controller consumed the remaining burden through late stabilization. Our protocol therefore treats strong priors as explanation compressors, not automatic proof of deeper transfer. D04 should only claim embodiment-level generalization after the stronger but narrower stories have already failed.

### 4.2.18 Demonstration-Translation Completion before Interface Promotion

A final upstream confound is now visible from recent cross-embodiment video-generation work: a route can improve because demonstrations are translated into the target embodiment more faithfully before policy learning even starts, not because the downstream transfer interface itself becomes stronger. This matters for D04 because mixed human, ground-robot, and aerial sources increasingly rely on relabeling, reenactment, simulation replay, or embodiment-specific visual translation to create cleaner supervision. If those translation steps repair embodiment appearance, kinematic pose plausibility, or contact timing in observation space, then later policy gains should not be immediately credited to shared geometry, latent-transition sufficiency, or embodiment-aware adaptation.

We therefore introduce **demonstration-translation completion (DTC)** as a pre-policy explanation family. Let `DTC` measure the incremental gain obtained by improving embodiment-matched demonstration rendering, replay, or relabeling while keeping the downstream geometry/latent/contact stack fixed. In the shortest-honest order for D04, `DTC` sits after scene-state completion but before stronger policy-interface claims whenever supervision quality is the decisive changed factor: `terrain/coordination/contact completion → demonstration-translation completion → latent/policy-interface transfer → embodiment residual recovery`. If a route becomes competitive mainly because target-matched demonstrations become visually or physically cleaner, the result must first freeze at **demonstration-translation completion** rather than being over-promoted to a stronger transfer headline.

### 4.2.19 Minimal Sanity Toggle for Supervision-Side Completion

To make this distinction executable, we add one cheap supervision-side sanity toggle to the first D04 sweep: compare `(raw source demos)`, `(+ geometry/state completion only)`, and `(+ embodiment-matched demo translation)` while holding the downstream transfer stack fixed. Report the incremental tuple `(Δstate, ΔDTC, Δlatent, Δresidual)` under matched training budget. If `ΔDTC` dominates before any downstream module changes, then the honest reading is that better cross-embodiment supervision was created, not that the transfer interface itself became stronger. Only when gains survive after the translation toggle is neutralized may the paper upgrade the story to latent-interface or embodiment-residual claims.

### 4.2.20 Current Evidence-Consistent Freeze after Supervision-Side Completion

Given the current D04 evidence base, this new supervision-side confound should be treated as a **reviewer-facing upstream stop rule**, not as a mature method claim. We do not yet have a local anchor proving that demonstration translation alone dominates mixed ground--aerial transfer, but the broader D04 structure now clearly needs a place to prevent translated supervision quality from being mistaken for stronger transfer interfaces. Therefore, the strongest honest near-term usage is conservative: if a future route improves mainly after relabeling, reenactment, or embodiment-matched video/data translation while the downstream geometry/latent/contact stack is unchanged, the claim must stop at **demonstration-translation completion** until matched toggles show that the gain survives without cleaner supervision.

This freeze keeps D04 aligned with its existing discipline of weakest-honest promotion. Just as terrain completion, contact-state completion, and coordination-state completion must be exhausted before policy-interface promotion, supervision-side completion must also be ruled out whenever the decisive change happens before policy learning or before downstream interface modules differ. In practice, this means D04 can now distinguish three upstream stories before talking about transfer proper: **the scene became more observable, the demonstrations became more embodiment-matched, or the interface itself actually became stronger**.

### 4.2.15 Attribution Priority after Terrain, Contact, and Coordination Completion

Mixed ground--aerial transfer introduces a new inflation risk: once an overhead platform is present, first-pass gains can be created by missing terrain revelation, by reusable contact-state exposure, or by synchronized world-state recovery before the policy interface itself becomes stronger. \[

\]
Here the ordering means **must be exhausted before promotion**. Only after these scene-state and interface-completion explanations fail to absorb the result may we promote the claim to latent-interface transfer or embodiment-aware residual recovery.

This priority is deliberately stricter than a generic geometry-first rule because mixed embodiments can otherwise create a false impression of stronger transfer. In particular, aerial assistance may make the global scene more complete without making the action interface any more reusable; similarly, a richer embodiment descriptor may expose contact affordance structure without proving that a heavier embodiment-specific controller is fundamentally required. D04 therefore treats scene-state completion and interface completion as independent explanation families that stand in front of policy-transfer claims, not as auxiliary details hidden inside them.

where `TSG` is terrain-state gain, `TSR` is terrain-state robustness under viewpoint or route perturbation, `CIG` is contact-interface gain, `CSC` is coordination-state completion gain, `source_view` identifies whether the decisive evidence comes from ground view, aerial view, or fused shared-state view, and `\tau_{\min}` is the weakest honest tag surviving after this decomposition. If `TSG` is modest but `CIG` or `CSC` is decisive, the row must stop at **contact-interface enrichment** or **coordination-state completion** respectively. Only when `TSG`, `CIG`, and `CSC` are all non-dominant under matched controls may the same row be upgraded to **latent/policy-interface transfer**; and only after that route is exhausted may the paper discuss **embodiment residual recovery** or **dynamics-residual burden relief**. This gives D04 a submission-ready first-pass discipline for mixed ground--aerial experiments: the paper must first prove that the scene was not merely made more observable, then prove that the interface was not merely made more complete, and only then claim that transfer itself became stronger.

Conversely, if a static- or history-conditioned embodiment route only becomes competitive after aggressive target-side adaptation that substantially increases `PTL`, the correct D04 conclusion is not that embodiment-aware adaptation is inherently necessary, but that the shared interface remains fragile under low-data post-training. We therefore place lock-in checks ahead of any upgrade from `shared geometry + latent sufficiency` to stronger embodiment-specific narratives.
Concretely, every adaptation-bearing route must now be evaluated under three matched variants: **A0 no target adaptation**, **A1 naive low-data adaptation**, and **A2 steerability-preserving low-data adaptation**. In addition to `(IGS, LTS, VGS-xEmb, SRA, SMG, HIG, DRB, CIG, OC)`, we report two new diagnostics: **PTL** (*Post-training Target Lock-in*), measuring how strongly low-data adaptation collapses target-object / target-location steerability, and **GRS** (*Grounding Retention Score*), measuring whether visual-spatial grounding survives after adaptation under mild object-swap, location-shift, and camera-shift perturbations. A route is not allowed to claim stronger cross-embodiment transfer merely because A1 beats A0 on one benchmark if that gain arrives with high `PTL` or degraded `GRS`.

The interpretation rule is intentionally conservative. If `IGS` and rollout-window attribution remain broadly stable, but `PTL` rises sharply after low-data adaptation, the result must freeze at **post-training lock-in under scarce target data** rather than shared-interface failure or missing embodiment prior. If A2 recovers `GRS` and lowers `PTL` while preserving most of A1's raw gain, the route may be reported as **steerability-preserving adaptation support**; however, that still does not upgrade the underlying scientific tag unless earlier shared-geometry, latent-sufficiency, and matched-budget tests have already failed. In other words, low-data adaptation is treated as a negative control on explanatory honesty: D04 should not reward a route for looking more target-specific if it achieves that specificity by destroying the geometry-conditioned controllability that makes transfer meaningful in the first place.

This control also sharpens the interaction between foundation priors and embodiment-aware residuals. If a JoyAI-RA- or PokeVLA-style prior already keeps `PTL` low and `GRS` high under A0/A2, then later morphology- or history-aware gains must clear a higher bar before being promoted. Conversely, if a history-conditioned or morphology-aware route only becomes competitive through naive low-data adaptation with obvious steerability collapse, then the honest conclusion is not `embodiment adaptation remains necessary`, but **the shared transfer stack is fragile to low-data post-training**. This keeps D04 aligned with its central claim: the paper is about explaining what truly transfers across embodiments, not about hiding lock-in failures behind a slightly higher end-task success number.

### 4.2.15 Contact-Interface Enrichment vs. Coordination-State Completion

A final distinction is now necessary inside the shared-state family itself. Recent local evidence suggests that D04 can gain a lot from adding better contact observability or better shared world-state maintenance, yet these two routes solve different scientific problems. **Contact-interface enrichment** means the transfer stack already knows the coarse geometry and intent, but lacks a reusable local contact packet such as touch onset, slip likelihood, pressure persistence, or contact-release timing. **Coordination-state completion** means the local controller is not actually acting on a stable shared world frame, so object pose, partner-agent state, or cross-view alignment drifts before the transfer policy can even be judged fairly.

We therefore add a paired experiment block that holds geometry, latent interface, and target adaptation budget fixed, then toggles two state completions separately: `(i)` reusable contact packet on/off, and `(ii)` synchronized shared-world-state support on/off. The resulting four-way split lets us distinguish whether a gain should be tagged as **contact-interface enrichment**, **coordination-state completion**, their combination, or neither. The interpretation rule is strict: if gains mainly appear at contact onset or during early post-contact alignment while viewpoint and shared-world-state support are already stable, the row must freeze at `contact_interface_completion`. If gains mainly appear when cross-agent object pose, camera-frame alignment, or asynchronous state refresh is repaired, the row must freeze at `coordination_state_completion`, even when end-task success rises sharply.

This block is especially important for mixed ground-aerial tasks. A ground manipulator may hand over a partially completed contact state to an aerial platform, or the aerial platform may arrive with correct intent but stale object/world-state estimates. Without separating these cases, D04 would over-credit later embodiment-aware residuals for what are really missing shared-state fields. Our default first-pass hypothesis is therefore conservative: **RoboMIND-style heterogeneous data should help most when the missing variable is contact semantics, whereas OmniRobotHome-style synchronized state support should help most when the missing variable is world-state consistency across agents or views**. Only gains that remain after both state completions are enabled are allowed to compete for a stronger embodiment- or dynamics-specific interpretation.

### 4.2.15 Window-Conditioned Promotion Matrix for Shared Priors, Contact State, and Residual Routes

We further compress the D04 first-pass protocol into a **window-conditioned promotion matrix** whose only purpose is to decide how far a result is allowed to travel rhetorically. Each route must now report a tuple
`P = (w_+, w^\dagger, g, b)`
where `w_+ ∈ {W0,W1,W2}` is the earliest rollout window with a stable positive signal, `w^\dagger ∈ {W0,W1,W2}` is the latest window in which the same gain remains honestly consumable under matched budget, `g` is the dominant gain family, and `b` is the smallest matched-budget profile that preserves that reading. The gain family is frozen to the set `{SGI, LTS, CIE, CSC, KFP, HBR, DRB}`, corresponding to **shared-geometry interface**, **latent-transition sufficiency**, **contact-interface enrichment**, **coordination-state completion**, **kinematic-feasibility prior**, **history-budgeted residual recovery**, and **dynamics-residual burden**.

The promotion rule is deliberately conservative. If a route turns positive in `W0` and remains honest only through `W0`, it may at most be promoted to `SGI`, `LTS`, or `KFP`, depending on whether the gain comes from packet formation, latent transition reuse, or topology-aware feasible-branch selection. If the positive mass first appears or is only honestly sustained through `W1`, the row may instead be tagged `CIE` or `CSC`, but it is still forbidden to jump directly to broad embodiment adaptation. Only routes whose gain remains honest through `W2` under matched budget may compete for `HBR` or `DRB`, and even then `HBR` is preferred when the effect is history- or refinement-mediated, while `DRB` is preferred when late aerial stabilization or payload-sensitive correction is the decisive consumer.

This matrix matters because D04 now mixes several superficially similar but scientifically distinct success patterns: a route can win by preserving pre-contact geometry, by adding contact observability, by repairing shared world-state consistency, by exposing a topology-aware feasible branch, or by rescuing late execution with longer history or dynamics-specific control. The window-conditioned promotion matrix makes these routes non-interchangeable at the claim level. A method is not allowed to headline the strongest module it contains; it may only headline the weakest tag that still survives at the last honest consumption window.

### 4.2.16 Anchor-Specific Non-Promotion Tags for Breaking Lock-In, AdaTracker, and DexFormer

We additionally freeze three **anchor-specific non-promotion tags** because current D04 evidence repeatedly risks being over-read in exactly these three ways. **Breaking Lock-In** [REF: 2604.23121] is treated as a low-data negative control rather than a transfer mechanism: if a route improves after scarce target-side adaptation only because `PTL` rises and steerability collapses more slowly under `A2`, then the row must freeze at **steerability-preserving adaptation support**, not cross-embodiment improvement. In other words, DeLock-style evidence is only allowed to defend the honesty of a route under low-data post-training; it is not allowed to promote the underlying transfer claim by itself.

**AdaTracker** [REF: 2604.20305] is frozen as a **context-conditioned residual route**. If its gain appears mainly in `W1/W2`, depends on a larger history budget than the best static-plus-latent baseline, or mainly improves delayed alignment and execution continuity, the result must stop at `HBR`—history-budgeted residual recovery. It may not be rewritten as stronger shared interface formation, nor as evidence that broad embodiment-aware adaptation is already the correct headline, unless the same gain remains positive across `W0-W2` under matched budgets and without borrowing credit from contact or coordination completion.

**DexFormer** [REF: 2602.08278] is frozen as the corresponding **history-conditioned embodiment inference probe**. If DexFormer-style history conditioning wins only after contact onset or only under a clearly larger temporal budget, the route must be tagged **temporal embodiment residual recovery** inside `HBR`. If its advantage instead appears mainly in `W0` through feasible-branch or topology compatibility effects, then the gain is demoted further to `KFP` rather than embodiment-wide adaptation. This rule prevents D04 from collapsing online embodiment inference, topology-aware feasibility selection, and late residual repair into one oversized morphology-aware story.

Together, these non-promotion tags give D04 three useful brakes. Breaking Lock-In prevents low-data post-training hygiene from masquerading as transfer strength. AdaTracker prevents context-conditioned late-window recovery from masquerading as shared-interface sufficiency. DexFormer prevents history-conditioned embodiment inference from stealing credit from either kinematic-feasibility priors or contact/dynamics-mediated rescue. The net effect is simple: the paper can still use all three anchors, but only as precisely bounded evidence for **why a route remains honest**, **where residual gain is created**, and **how far that gain is actually allowed to travel in the final narrative**.

### 4.3 Ablation Study

The next ambiguity we explicitly remove is whether a late positive result comes from **better local interaction observability** or from **better shared world-state consistency**. Recent local evidence already points to both routes: FlexiTac-style reusable tactile sensing suggests that some transfer failures only surface because the cross-embodiment packet does not expose contact onset, slip tendency, or contact persistence; OmniRobotHome-style synchronized world-state maintenance suggests that multi-agent or multi-view deployment can fail even when local control is competent, simply because embodiments no longer act on the same object/world estimate. For D04, these two fixes must not be merged into a single generic adaptation story.

We therefore require every first-pass result that improves contact-heavy or coordination-heavy tasks to append a dual audit tuple `(CIG, CSG, source_slot, failure_surface)`, where `CIG` denotes **contact-interface gain**, `CSG` denotes **coordination-state gain**, `source_slot ∈ {contact, coordination, mixed}`, and `failure_surface ∈ {post-contact observability, slip persistence, cross-view drift, cross-agent desync, mixed}`. If success increases mainly after adding tactile/contact packet fields while geometry and world-frame synchronization are unchanged, the honest tag is **contact-interface enrichment**. If the gain appears mainly after restoring shared frame consistency, multi-camera synchronization, or object-state freshness across embodiments, the honest tag is **coordination-state completion**. Only when both audits remain strong under matched budgets may the paper claim that the broader cross-embodiment interface itself improved.

This rule is especially important for mixed ground–aerial manipulation. A UAV-manipulator stack may look weak because contact is under-observed near touchdown, or because the aerial and ground embodiments are reading slightly different object states after occlusion or camera relocation. Those are distinct scientific diagnoses. The first implies the shared packet is missing transferable contact semantics; the second implies the packet is locally adequate but globally mis-synchronized. By writing this distinction directly into the first-pass reporting protocol, D04 avoids over-crediting tactile packet completion or shared-state repair as if they were generic morphology-aware transfer breakthroughs.

### 4.2.17 Claim Serialization Contract for First-Pass D04 Results

To make the first-pass D04 Results section auditable at the sentence level, we add a final **claim serialization contract** that every promoted finding must satisfy before it is allowed to influence the paper headline. The contract is deliberately narrow: each first-pass result claim must be serialized as
`creator stage → first positive window → last honest consumption window → weakest surviving tag`
rather than written as a free-form success narrative. This matters because D04 evidence is now structurally multi-stage: a topology-aware prior may create a feasible branch in `W0`, a contact packet may cash out that advantage in `W1`, and a history-budgeted controller may further amplify it in `W2`. Without explicit serialization, these linked effects are easy to overcompress into a single inflated statement about broad cross-embodiment adaptation.

We therefore freeze a four-field reporting contract for every first-pass route. **Field 1** identifies the earliest stage `S0-S5` that creates a measurable positive signal under matched budget. **Field 2** identifies the first rollout window `W0/W1/W2` where the gain becomes behaviorally visible. **Field 3** identifies the latest window in which the same gain is still consumed without requiring a stronger downstream explanation such as tactile completion, long-history repair, or payload-sensitive stabilization. **Field 4** assigns the weakest surviving interpretation tag from the ordered set `{geometry_not_established, latent_sufficient, compact_prior_saturation, foundation_prior_saturation, contact_interface_completion, coordination_state_completion, kinematic_feasibility_prior, history_budgeted_residual_recovery, dynamics_residual_burden}`. A stronger tag is only legal when all weaker tags have been explicitly stress-tested and rejected.

This serialization rule now has a concrete heterogeneous-to-aerial reading. **RoboMIND 2.0** [REF: 2512.24653] supplies the strongest current heterogeneous source regime because it bundles six embodiments, mobile-bimanual interaction, tactile-rich traces, and digital-twin replay. For D04, that means any first-pass gain from large heterogeneous source coverage must first be written as a **source-regime prior** that raises geometry, latent, or contact readiness, rather than being immediately promoted to morphology-aware adaptation. **FlexiTac** [REF: 2604.28156] contributes the complementary contact-side interpretation: if a gain is only honestly consumed once reusable tactile/contact packet fields are enabled, then the weakest surviving tag is `contact_interface_completion`, not generic embodiment intelligence. **OmniRobotHome** [REF: 2604.28197] forces a second distinction: when gains emerge only after a stable shared world frame or cross-agent object-state freshness is restored, they must be tagged `coordination_state_completion`. **HandCDO** [REF: 2604.27557] sharpens the morphology side by reminding us that richer embodiment descriptors may help because they expose transferable contact affordance and kinematic geometry, not because the whole transfer stack suddenly needs a heavier adaptation family.

We therefore freeze one default serialization path for the mixed ground-to-aerial corridor. If heterogeneous pretraining from RoboMIND-style data first lifts `IGS/LTS/CIG` in `W0-W1`, but the final success gain is only fully consumed in `W2` after payload-aware aerial rescue is enabled, the paper must split the claim into two layers: **coarse intent/contact preparation transferred across embodiments**, while **late aerial stabilization remained a platform-dynamics burden**. Likewise, if HandCDO-style embodiment descriptors mainly improve pre-contact feasible-branch selection but lose their advantage unless contact or shared-state completion is later added, the route must stop at `kinematic_feasibility_prior` or `contact/coordination completion` rather than being inflated into a broad morphology-aware headline.

The practical payoff is that the first-pass Results text becomes directly portable to the Abstract and Conclusion without hidden inflation. A reader should be able to recover, from one claim sentence, whether D04 is really reporting shared-geometry transfer, latent sufficiency, topology-aware feasibility, heterogeneous-source prior, contact/coordination completion, or merely late residual rescue. In this sense, claim serialization is not just a writing preference; it is the last guard that keeps D04 aligned with its central thesis that cross-embodiment transfer must be credited to the **lowest sufficient explanation**, not to the latest or largest module in the stack.

### 4.2.18 Consumption-Time Audit for Cross-Embodiment Transfer Claims

We now add a stricter **consumption-time audit** so that D04 does not over-credit gains that look positive at creation time but only become useful after later modules have already changed the causal story. The key question is not merely *which stage first created a better packet or branch*, but *when the target embodiment actually consumed that improvement as the same explanatory object*. This matters because D04 increasingly mixes topology-aware branch priors, compact geometry-grounded priors, reusable contact packets, heterogeneous-pretraining sources, and aerial residual controllers; a gain that is created early in `W0` may still be cashed out only after contact completion in `W1` or stabilization rescue in `W2`.

Operationally, every promoted result row must therefore report a compact tuple
`(creator_stage, t_create, first_positive_window, t_consume, Δt_consume, last_honest_consumption_window, borrowing_source)`
where `Δt_consume = t_consume - t_create` is the transfer-side delayed-consumption gap. We then require the paper to bucket results into **immediate consumption**, **bounded delayed consumption**, and **late borrowed consumption**. If a result is created in `S1-S4` but is only consumed after a stronger downstream mechanism activates, its claim must be downgraded to the weakest tag that still remains honest at `t_consume`. In other words, D04 is not allowed to headline the creator of a gain if the consuming mechanism has already become the real bottleneck-resolver by the time success is realized.

This audit creates a sharper reading rule for the main D04 confounds. A topology-aware prior that improves feasible-branch selection in `W0` may still be honestly tagged **kinematic-feasibility prior** only when the decisive benefit is consumed before contact-interface completion or long-history rescue takes over. A compact or heterogeneous prior may still support **latent sufficiency** only when its gain remains consumable without being rewritten by later tactile, coordination-state, or aerial-stabilization aids. Conversely, if the positive effect is first created by a shared packet but consumed only after payload-aware late-stage rescue, then the correct interpretation is **bounded packet transfer with delayed aerial uptake**, not broad cross-embodiment sufficiency.

The value of this subsection is that it aligns D04 with the same honesty principle already emerging in neighboring directions: success should be attributed at the **consumption boundary**, not merely at the release boundary. For D04, that means a shared interface deserves credit only when the transferred geometry/contact/world-state structure is still the thing being honestly used when the target embodiment acts. Otherwise, the paper must explicitly say that the earlier module created a better precondition while the later module performed the decisive uptake.

### 4.2.19 Heterogeneous-to-Aerial Delayed-Uptake Corridor

A dedicated heterogeneous-to-aerial corridor is now necessary because the most important near-term D04 deployment scenario is no longer only static robot-to-robot transfer, but transfer from large heterogeneous ground-manipulation corpora into aerial manipulation with late stabilization burden. Recent local evidence from **RoboMIND 2.0** and **AirVLA** suggests that these two stages should not be narrated as one monolithic success number. A strong heterogeneous source may already transfer geometry alignment, contact preparation, and coarse subgoal ordering, while the final aerial improvement is only honestly consumed after target-specific stabilization guidance activates.

We therefore pre-register a corridor-specific readout that jointly reports `IGS`, `LTS`, `CIG`, `CSG`, `DRB`, and the delayed-consumption tuple above. The first question is whether RoboMIND-style heterogeneous pretraining raises `W0/W1` performance before any aerial rescue is enabled. The second question is whether the remaining positive mass is consumed in `W2` only after payload-aware or stabilization-aware assistance turns on. If so, the paper must freeze the route at **heterogeneous prior with delayed aerial uptake** or **bounded aerial recovery on top of transferred packet structure**, rather than broad universal transfer.

This corridor is useful because it lets D04 describe partial but real success more honestly. A heterogeneous source prior may indeed transfer consumable geometry, contact readiness, and low-frequency task intent across embodiments, even if it does not solve late aerial dynamics. That is a meaningful result. But the consumption-time audit forces us to report exactly how far the shared packet travels before the aerial controller becomes the decisive consumer. In practice, this turns D04 into a staged empirical claim: cross-embodiment transfer may succeed up to the point of consumable packet preservation, while late target dynamics remain an explicitly bounded residual.

### 4.2.15 Terrain-Coordination Separation under Mixed Ground--Aerial Support

We further freeze a stricter separation rule between **terrain-state completion** and **coordination-state completion** before any policy-interface promotion is allowed. Let the reviewer-facing auxiliary tuple be
`S_{tc}=(TSG, TSR, CSC, CIG, CEP, \tau_{tc})`,
where `TSG` measures the gain obtained after restoring traversability-relevant overhead state, `TSR` measures how much of that gain survives once matched terrain visibility is equalized, `CSC` measures the gain obtained after shared world-state synchronization is repaired, `CIG` measures the gain from reusable contact-state exposure, `CEP` measures the gain from contact-capable embodiment parameterization, and `\tau_{tc}` is the weakest honest tag that survives all matched controls. The reporting priority is fixed as
`terrain-state completion → coordination-state completion → contact-interface enrichment → contact-capable descriptor gain → latent/policy-interface transfer → embodiment residual recovery`.

This distinction matters because mixed ground--aerial systems can improve for two visually similar but scientifically different reasons. The aerial platform may reveal blind traversability structure, support regions, or obstacle topology that the ground platform never observed, which should be frozen as **terrain-state completion**. Alternatively, success may improve only after the team acts on a synchronized shared world state, e.g., after stale object pose, cross-view desynchronization, or multi-agent frame drift is repaired; this must be frozen as **coordination-state completion**. Even when both happen together, D04 must first ask whether the decisive gain is consumed by better terrain evidence or by better shared-state maintenance, rather than jumping directly to a stronger transfer headline.

Operationally, every first-pass row in mixed ground--aerial evaluation must include two matched controls before promotion. The first removes the aerial terrain advantage while keeping the policy stack unchanged; if the row collapses here, the honest claim stops at **terrain-state completion**. The second restores traversability information but perturbs shared-state freshness or multi-agent synchronization; if the row now collapses, the honest claim stops at **coordination-state completion**. Only after both controls fail to absorb the gain do we allow the result to climb toward latent-transition sufficiency, policy-interface transfer, or embodiment residual recovery. This gives D04 a cleaner boundary against over-crediting overhead scene completion or synchronized state repair as if they were stronger cross-embodiment controller evidence.

### 4.2.16 Minimal Reviewer-Facing Paragraph Template after Terrain/Coordination Routing

To keep the paper prose consistent with the routing rule above, we freeze the first-pass Results paragraph for mixed ground--aerial rows into four short sentences. **Sentence 1** reports whether the gain appears first under terrain restoration, shared-state synchronization, contact-state exposure, or a later transfer stage. **Sentence 2** reports the earliest window in which the gain remains positive after matched terrain equalization and shared-state freshness controls. **Sentence 3** names the weakest surviving explanation tag `\tau_{tc}` and states whether latent-transition sufficiency is still necessary after terrain/coordination/contact explanations are exhausted. **Sentence 4** only then reports whether the remaining residual is better read as kinematic-feasibility prior, history-conditioned embodiment residual, or dynamics-residual burden.

This paragraph freeze is intentionally conservative. It prevents the D04 Results section from using aerial-assisted success as a shortcut to cross-embodiment transfer language. In particular, if a row owes most of its success to overhead traversability completion or to synchronized world-state repair, the paragraph must stop there even when final task success rises substantially. Stronger wording is only allowed when the gain survives the terrain/coordination routing stack and is still best explained by the transferable policy interface itself.

### 4.2.17 Current Evidence-Consistent Freeze for D04 Mixed Ground--Aerial Claims

Given the current local evidence base around GA3T, OmniRobotHome, FlexiTac, HandCDO, PokeVLA, VistaBot, Breaking Lock-In, and AdaTracker, the strongest honest near-term D04 claim is still conservative: **shared geometry plus geometry-conditioned latent interfaces likely transfer coarse intent and pre-contact structure across embodiments, while mixed ground--aerial improvements must first be discounted by terrain-state completion, coordination-state completion, contact-interface enrichment, and lock-in-aware adaptation controls before any stronger embodiment-transfer story is accepted**. In other words, D04 is not yet entitled to a broad "aerial assistance proves policy-interface superiority" narrative.

This freeze is scientifically useful because it narrows the next experimental burden. The paper does not yet need to prove universal embodiment-invariant control. It needs to prove, under matched terrain/state/contact/adaptation controls, which part of the gain is still left for latent-transition sufficiency and which part is merely delayed uptake by aerial stabilization or history-conditioned residual recovery. That is the cleanest next step consistent with the current literature map and with the paper's own staged honesty discipline.

### 4.2.18 First-Pass State-Completion Freeze before Transfer Promotion

For mixed ground--aerial cross-embodiment experiments, the first-pass result must be frozen against three state-completion explanations before any stronger policy-transfer claim is allowed. We therefore require every main comparison to report `(TSG, CIG, CSC, TSR, weakest_tag)` together with the standard transfer tuple, where `TSG` denotes terrain-state gain, `CIG` denotes contact-interface gain, `CSC` denotes coordination-state completion gain, and `TSR` records the residual transfer signal after these state-completion factors are accounted for. The weakest-honest rule is simple: if the dominant improvement comes from overhead traversability disclosure, blind-spot disambiguation, or support-region visibility restoration, the result must be frozen at **terrain-state completion**; if the improvement appears mainly after reusable tactile/contact traces become available, it must be frozen at **contact-interface enrichment**; if the gain depends primarily on maintaining a synchronized shared world frame across embodiments or agents, it must be frozen at **coordination-state completion**.

This rule is motivated by a local cluster of recent evidence that is highly relevant to D04 but easy to overclaim. GA3T [REF: 2605.06478] shows that an aerial platform can unlock large gains simply by exposing traversability structure the ground embodiment never observed. FlexiTac [REF: 2604.28156] suggests that a reusable tactile packet can restore cross-platform contact observability without changing the transfer policy itself. OmniRobotHome [REF: 2604.28197] indicates that multi-agent success may hinge on synchronized shared world-state maintenance rather than better embodiment retargeting. HandCDO [REF: 2604.27557] further implies that richer embodiment descriptors can expose transferable contact affordance structure before any heavier adaptation module is justified. Accordingly, D04 should not let these gains bypass the first-pass state-completion layer. Only when terrain completion, contact-state completion, and coordination-state completion all fail to explain the improvement do we permit the interpretation to escalate toward latent-transition sufficiency, policy-interface transfer, or embodiment-residual recovery.

### 4.2.19 Terrain-First Residual Readout for Mixed Ground--Aerial Transfer

To operationalize the above rule, we define a terrain-first residual readout for every heterogeneous transfer table. Each route must be summarized as `(source_view, target_view, TSG, CIG, CSC, TSR, weakest_tag, uplift_window)`, where `uplift_window` localizes whether the measured gain occurs before contact, around contact onset, or only during late aerial stabilization. If the uplift is concentrated in the pre-contact stage and disappears once terrain visibility is equalized, the result is not allowed to count as policy-interface transfer. If the uplift emerges only after contact signals or shared-world synchronization are restored, it must be logged under contact or coordination completion, respectively. Only a residual signal that survives these audits is allowed to enter the D04 transfer ledger.

This terrain-first residual discipline matters because mixed ground--aerial systems are especially prone to false-positive transfer stories. A UAV may contribute useful structure simply by revealing a safer path, exposing an occluded support region, or keeping world-state estimates synchronized across agents. Those are scientifically valuable gains, but they are not yet proof that the cross-embodiment action interface has become more reusable. By forcing the first table to stop at the weakest sufficient explanation, D04 preserves a cleaner separation between state completion and genuine transfer structure. This also aligns the experiments section with the paper's central claim that shared geometry must be verified before latent transfer is credited, and latent transfer must be exhausted before embodiment-specific residual stories are promoted.

### 4.2.20 Downstream Uptake Bound and Honest Paragraph Rule

Finally, we convert the above audits into a concrete writing rule for Section 4.2. Every first-pass paragraph must now state, in order: **(i)** which stage created the gain, **(ii)** when and where the gain was first consumed, **(iii)** whether the gain stayed honest through the last consumption boundary without borrowing a stronger downstream explanation, and **(iv)** what the weakest surviving tag is after that audit. This is the **downstream uptake bound**: a paragraph is not allowed to describe a route as shared-latent, morphology-aware, or cross-embodiment transfer if the decisive benefit was only consumed after a later contact, coordination, history, or aerial-stabilization mechanism took over.

Concretely, the first-pass paragraph order remains `S0-S1 → S2-S3 → S4 → S5`, but every paragraph must now obey one additional sentence-level constraint. If the gain is **immediate-consumption positive**, the paragraph may headline the creator-stage explanation. If it is **bounded-delay positive**, the paragraph must explicitly say the packet remained valid through a delayed uptake boundary. If it is **late-borrowed positive**, the paragraph must downgrade the headline to the downstream consumer or to a bounded-transfer statement such as **packet preserved, uptake deferred to later residual control**. This prevents D04 from repeating the most tempting overclaim in cross-embodiment work: treating early packet quality as if it had already solved the target embodiment when, in fact, the decisive lift came only after downstream rescue.

The practical consequence is simple but important. D04 can now make stronger honest claims of the form: *shared geometry and latent interfaces preserve consumable task structure across embodiments up to the contact or pre-stabilization boundary; beyond that point, remaining gains are only realized under bounded downstream uptake*. That sentence is weaker than full transfer sufficiency, but far more precise—and far more publishable—than a vague aggregate success claim that blurs creator, consumer, and residual rescuer into one story.

### 4.2.21 Heterogeneous-Source Prior vs. Contact/Coordination Uptake Split

We now freeze one more subsection because D04 is increasingly supported by heterogeneous source corpora whose gains can be real yet still easy to mislabel. A source regime such as RoboMIND 2.0 can improve transfer not only by giving more diverse trajectories, but by front-loading **contact-ready geometry**, **task-phase regularity**, and **shared world-state hints** before the aerial or target embodiment ever needs to solve late execution. If those source-side benefits are later consumed only after contact packet completion or world-state synchronization is restored, then the honest story is not “heterogeneous pretraining solved cross-embodiment transfer,” but rather **heterogeneous-source prior improved packet readiness, while downstream uptake still depended on contact or coordination completion**.

We therefore define a corridor-specific split audit for any route that uses large heterogeneous source data. Each row must additionally report `(SPR, Uc, Uw, Ua)`, where `SPR` is **source-packet readiness** before target-specific rescue, `Uc` is the fraction of later gain whose decisive uptake depends on contact-interface completion, `Uw` is the fraction whose uptake depends on coordination-state completion, and `Ua` is the fraction whose uptake is deferred to aerial or target-specific late residual control. A route is only allowed to headline heterogeneous-source transfer if `SPR` is high and the majority of decisive benefit is consumed without large `Uc/Uw/Ua` borrowing. If instead the benefit created by the source prior is only cashed out after downstream packet repair, the row must freeze at **heterogeneous-source prior with deferred uptake**.

This extra split matters because D04 now sits right between D05-style data-flywheel arguments and D06-style aerial stabilization arguments. Without an explicit source-versus-uptake audit, a good heterogeneous corpus can accidentally steal credit for gains that are only realized after tactile completion, shared-state repair, or payload-aware rescue. By forcing the paper to report how much of the transfer story is created upstream and how much is actually consumed downstream, we keep the D04 headline aligned with its core thesis: cross-embodiment transfer deserves credit only up to the last boundary where the transferred packet remains the honest causal carrier.

### 4.2.22 First-Pass Honest Claim Template for D04 Abstract/Conclusion Reuse

To make the main findings directly reusable in the Abstract and Conclusion without later narrative inflation, we freeze a final first-pass claim template:

*`Under matched budget B, stage Sk created a transferable packet improvement first visible in Wm; this gain remained honestly consumable through boundary Wx with tag T, while any remaining success beyond Wx required downstream contact / coordination / dynamics uptake.`*

Every promoted D04 sentence should be a specialization of this template. If the creator-stage and consumer-stage are the same, the sentence may state immediate transfer. If they differ but the packet remains valid through a bounded delayed-consumption gap, the sentence must explicitly say **bounded delayed uptake**. This rule sounds fussy, but it prevents the exact failure mode D04 is most vulnerable to now: writing one smooth success sentence that silently fuses heterogeneous-source priors, topology-aware feasibility, contact enrichment, coordination repair, and late aerial stabilization into a single overclaimed cross-embodiment contribution.

In practice, this template also gives us a clean way to separate D04's real contribution from adjacent directions. What it must not do is pretend that every gain realized after that boundary still belongs to the same explanation tier. The template therefore acts as a final prose-level checksum for the staged attribution protocol.

### 4.2.23 Contact-Capable Embodiment Parameterization as a Pre-Adaptation Attribution Gate

We add one more attribution gate before D04 is allowed to escalate from interface completion to embodiment-conditioned adaptation. Recent local evidence from HandCDO [REF: 2604.27557] suggests that part of the apparent gain of morphology-aware routes may come from a richer **contact-capable embodiment parameterization** rather than from a stronger adaptation mechanism per se. In other words, once fingertip geometry, local curvature, reachable contact family, or end-effector compliance class are exposed in a transferable descriptor, the transfer stack may improve simply because it now knows *what contacts are structurally available*, not because it has learned a more powerful embodiment-specific policy.

Accordingly, our first-pass table will insert a pre-adaptation diagnostic between `contact-interface completion` and `static morphology prior`. If performance improves after augmenting the shared packet with a compact structural descriptor while history-conditioned residual modeling remains marginal, the gain is frozen at **contact-capable embodiment parameterization**. Only if that descriptor is insufficient and later static or temporal adaptation still adds stable improvement under matched budget may the paper escalate to broader embodiment-aware conclusions. This rule is especially important for mixed ground–aerial manipulation, where the decisive residual may come from exposing graspable/contact-feasible structure at the end-effector rather than from learning a large embodiment-conditioned controller.

### 4.2.24 Low-Data Adaptation versus Descriptor Exposure under Matched Budget

We further pair the above gate with a targeted matched-budget control. Therefore, for any route that uses low-data adaptation after adding embodiment descriptors, we will compare three variants under matched budget: `(i)` descriptor exposure only, `(ii)` low-data adaptation only, and `(iii)` descriptor exposure plus low-data adaptation. If most of the gain is already unlocked by descriptor exposure while post-training mainly raises `PTL` or lowers `GRS`, the honest reading is **descriptor omission plus lock-in-sensitive adaptation**, not stronger embodiment transfer. This gives D04 a cleaner way to separate “the packet was underspecified” from “the interface was correct but adaptation was still necessary.”

## 4.2.21 Terrain-First Mixed Ground--Aerial Narrative Freeze
