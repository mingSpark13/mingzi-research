## Abstract

Aerial Vision-Language Navigation (VLN) requires unmanned aerial vehicles to follow natural-language instructions in open 3D environments, yet existing VLN methods remain poorly matched to aerial deployment because they inherit 2D frontier assumptions, weak dynamics awareness, and brittle planner-controller handoff. We propose a packet-centric aerial VLN framework that couples **3D Semantic Frontier Mapping**, **dynamics-aware frontier selection**, and a **Navigate-then-Manipulate (NtM)** execution interface. The core representation is a **Semantic Waypoint Packet**, which serializes target pose, yaw prior, altitude band, stage tag, runtime budget, and guarded execution prefix into a verifier-compatible object that can be refreshed online. This framing allows us to jointly evaluate semantic planning quality, directional instruction adherence, runtime continuity, and downstream controllability rather than collapsing them into a single navigation score. Compared with prior aerial systems such as OnFly, HTNav, LookasideVLN, and LiveVLN, our method is designed to preserve packet validity through the full `planner → verifier → controller` chain while retaining the ability to hand off from search-time navigation to manipulation-time local interaction. We further formalize packet-facing metrics, including verifier pass rate, packet staleness, packet handoff failure, context retention, and progress-trace consistency, to expose failure modes that standard success-rate reporting obscures. The resulting framework positions aerial VLN as a deployable interface problem rather than only a waypoint prediction problem.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Problem Statement

Vision-Language Navigation (VLN) enables robots to navigate to language-described targets in unknown environments, a core capability for embodied intelligence. While ground VLN has seen substantial progress—VLFM [REF: ], NaVILA [REF: ], ApexNav [REF: ], SG-Nav [REF: ]—**aerial VLN remains severely underexplored**. Existing methods assume 2D planar motion, low speed, and stable viewpoints, fundamentally incompatible with the 6-DoF motion space, high-speed flight, dramatic pitch-angle changes, and dynamics constraints of unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs).

The gap is not merely one of scale. Aerial platforms introduce three qualitatively distinct challenges absent from ground VLN: (1) **3D spatial exploration**: targets may be above, below, or at arbitrary elevations, requiring volumetric frontier representations rather than 2D maps; (2) **dynamics-aware planning**: energy budgets, velocity limits, and attitude constraints must be incorporated into exploration cost functions to avoid infeasible paths; and (3) **navigation-to-manipulation continuity**: aerial VLN naturally extends to Navigate-then-Manipulate (NtM) tasks where the robot must transition from search to precise end-effector operations upon target localization.

Recent surveys [REF: 2604.13654, 2604.07705] confirm that 3D spatial representation and dynamics-aware planning are the two core unsolved problems in aerial VLN. This paper directly addresses both.

### 1.2 Contributions

This paper makes the following contributions:

1. **C1: Aerial VL-Frontier Map (AVLFM)**: We extend 2D vision-language frontier maps to 3D volumetric representations, incorporating VLM semantic relevance scoring to guide exploration in the full 3D aerial workspace, including vertical dimension exploration and viewpoint-change robustness.

2. **C2: Dynamics-Aware Navigation Policy**: We explicitly model UAV energy, velocity, and safety constraints within the frontier selection and path planning process, treating dynamics constraints as part of the exploration cost function to eliminate infeasible candidate paths.

3. **C3: Navigate-then-Manipulate (NtM) Architecture**: We design a unified architecture that seamlessly transitions from navigation mode to manipulation mode when the target enters the operational range, with closed-loop recovery enabling re-navigation upon manipulation failure.

4. **C4: Aerial VLN Benchmark**: We construct an evaluation benchmark covering indoor and outdoor aerial VLN scenarios with dynamics-aware metrics, providing the first systematic evaluation of aerial VLN with 3D spatial reasoning.

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Ground Vision-Language Navigation

Ground VLN has established strong baselines for language-guided exploration. VLFM [REF: ] uses VLM semantic relevance to guide 2D frontier exploration, demonstrating that language-conditioned frontier selection significantly outperforms geometric-only exploration. NaVILA [REF: ] adopts a hierarchical VLA design with high-level VLM reasoning and low-level action execution. ApexNav [REF: ] introduces adaptive semantic-geometric exploration balancing exploration and exploitation. SG-Nav [REF: ] uses scene graph representations for structured navigation reasoning. DRIVE-Nav [REF: ] enhances spatial reasoning through directional inference. These methods collectively demonstrate the value of semantic frontier maps but are fundamentally limited to 2D planar motion and cannot handle aerial dynamics.

### 2.2 Aerial Navigation Systems

OnFly [REF: ] introduces a dual-agent UAV navigation system with decoupled perception and control frequencies, the closest existing work to aerial VLN. OpenFly [REF: 2502.18041] provides a comprehensive aerial VLN platform combining UE/GTA V/Google Earth/3DGS rendering sources, automatic trajectory and instruction generation, and a keyframe-aware VLA model, serving as our primary data infrastructure reference. AirNav [REF: 2601.03707] presents the first real-world urban aerial VLN benchmark with AirVLN-R1 (SFT + RLFT training recipe), directly addressing the real-data evaluation gap. APEX [REF: 2602.00551] proposes Dynamic Spatio-Semantic Mapping with RL-based action decisions and target-guided exploration, providing an interpretable modular explorer suitable as an engineering reference for our GoalSearch component. Fly0 [REF: 2602.15875] explicitly decouples semantic grounding from geometric trajectory planning in three stages, demonstrating zero-shot, low-latency navigation with geometric robustness after visual loss. FineCog-Nav [REF: 2604.16298] introduces fine-grained cognitive modularization for zero-shot UAV VLN, decomposing language processing, perception, attention, memory, imagination, reasoning, and decision into role-specific modules with the AerialVLN-Fine benchmark for sentence-level instruction-trajectory alignment. SpatialFly [REF: 2603.21046] represents a complementary geometry-first line: rather than constructing an explicit 3D frontier map, it injects geometric priors into 2D semantic tokens and aligns them with 3D structural cues, showing that representation-level geometry alignment can improve unseen-scene path alignment and motion smoothness even without full volumetric reconstruction.

### 2.3 Aerial Manipulation

π-Make-It-Fly [REF: ] demonstrates that visual representations transfer to aerial platforms but control dynamics do not, requiring payload-aware guidance at inference time. Unified Aerial Grasping [REF: ] provides an end-to-end language-conditioned aerial grasping system with active exploration and 6-DoF grasping. AeroPlace-Flow [REF: ] addresses language-guided aerial object placement using Visual Foresight and Object Flow. These manipulation works assume known approximate target locations, lacking a systematic navigation front-end—the gap our NtM architecture addresses.

### 2.4 Direction-Aware Spatial Language Planning and Live Runtime Execution

LookasideVLN [REF: 2604.17190] highlights a previously under-modeled source of aerial VLN gain: explicit directional cues in language instructions. Instead of relying only on landmark mentions or deeper lookahead search, it introduces an Egocentric Lookaside Graph and a Spatial Landmark Knowledge Base to encode landmark-direction relations for multimodal path planning. Its strongest value for our setting is not a replacement of frontier exploration, but a lightweight planner frontend that can improve instruction adherence, waypoint proposal quality, and heading selection before the packet-verifier-controller chain. This suggests that direction-aware language parsing should be treated as an augmenting module for waypoint proposal quality, not as a substitute for 3D semantic frontier mapping or dynamics-aware execution.

OnFly [REF: 2603.10682] further strengthens the systems side of this argument from an aerial deployment perspective. Its onboard dual-agent design, hybrid keyframe/latest-frame memory, and semantic-geometric verification pipeline show that aerial VLN performance depends not only on semantic planning quality, but also on whether the runtime can maintain safe progress monitoring under onboard latency constraints. In our framing, this means that packet quality should be judged together with *verifiability* and *runtime continuity*, rather than by semantic relevance alone.

LiveVLN [REF: 2604.19536] further pushes the field toward temporally grounded, online navigation by exposing the deployment inefficiency of stop-and-go execution. It proposes a training-free runtime that splits an action sequence into an executed prefix, a guard buffer, and a revisable tail, enabling guarded handoff and real-time adaptation while preserving execution continuity. For our setting, its main value is not replacing aerial exploration itself, but motivating a **live packet refresh** mechanism that keeps frontier proposals updateable under streaming observations without forcing full replanning at every step. Together, these works motivate us to evaluate planner frontend quality not only by semantic relevance, but also by direction-conditioned adherence, verifier compatibility, and runtime continuity under packetized execution.

### 2.5 Packet-Centric Framing and the Limits of Existing Aerial VLN Pipelines

A common limitation across current aerial VLN systems is that planning quality, execution continuity, and downstream controllability are still entangled. Landmark-centric methods often produce semantically plausible targets without explicitly representing directional priors. Runtime-oriented systems improve continuity, but usually treat the planner output as a transient action list rather than a reusable interface object. Modular onboard systems add semantic-geometric verification, yet the intermediate representation passed from planner to controller is still weakly formalized. As a result, it remains difficult to diagnose whether failure comes from poor waypoint semantics, stale action handoff, verifier mismatch, or low-level control drift.

This motivates our packet-centric formulation: instead of asking only whether the planner predicts the right next move, we ask whether it emits a **semantic waypoint packet** that is direction-aware, budget-bounded, verifier-compatible, and refreshable online. Under this framing, the key unit of evaluation is not just task success, but whether the packet can survive the full `planner → verifier → controller` chain with low staleness, low heading drift, and low handoff failure. This reframes aerial VLN from pure navigation prediction toward a deployment-ready interface problem.

Recent pipeline-level evidence reinforces this viewpoint. QuadAgent [REF: 2604.02786] shows that low-latency responsive flight can be improved through asynchronous agent orchestration and sparse memory updates, but its planner-controller interface remains weakly verifiable, making it difficult to cleanly separate semantic planning gains from execution-shell gains. FineCog-Nav [REF: 2604.16298] further reveals that planner quality itself is internally structured: instruction adherence, memory use, and module handoff quality can improve under fine-grained cognitive decomposition, but these gains only matter for deployment if they remain compatible with packet-level verification and execution continuity. This suggests that aerial VLN systems need packet-level bookkeeping not only for semantic correctness, but also for *interface accountability* across perception, planning, verification, and control. In our formulation, packetization is therefore not a software convenience—it is the minimal abstraction required to make planner quality, verifier compatibility, and execution continuity jointly diagnosable.

A second recent line of evidence points to *deployability-aware planner structure* rather than only better semantic scoring. A Deployable Embodied Vision-Language Navigation System with Hierarchical Cognition and Context-Aware Exploration [REF: 2604.21363] shows that asynchronous perception, memory integration, and high-level reasoning can be paired with a context-aware viewpoint routing objective on real-world robots under tight compute constraints. Although it is not an aerial system, its main lesson is directly relevant to our formulation: when planner outputs must remain valid under real-time deployment limits, viewpoint selection quality should be evaluated jointly with memory integration quality and execution latency, not in isolation. This strengthens our claim that packetization should expose not just *where to go next*, but also *what contextual support and runtime budget make that proposal worth executing*.

This additional evidence also sharpens the role of hierarchical planning in our aerial setting. HTNav [REF: 2604.08883] suggests that planner quality in continuous UAV navigation should be decomposed into macro waypoint selection and local reactive stabilization, while FineCog-Nav [REF: 2604.16298] suggests that even the high-level planner itself benefits from explicit memory and reasoning modularization. Taken together, these results imply that a deployable aerial VLN stack should not evaluate planner quality only at the final action level. Instead, it should ask whether packet fields preserve clause-level intent, directional priors, memory-conditioned context, and runtime budget information in a form that downstream verifier and controller modules can still consume.

OpenVLN [REF: 2511.06182] contributes an orthogonal but important pressure test for this framing. Its value-model-guided verifiable reward and VLN-CE replanner indicate that long-horizon open-world aerial navigation may require an explicit *replanning-value* signal in addition to semantic grounding and runtime continuity. For our paper, this does not replace packetization; instead, it sharpens a missing distinction inside current packet-centric systems. A packet may be semantically valid and locally executable yet still be a poor continuation because it weakens future recoverability or long-horizon search value. This suggests that packet quality should ultimately be judged not only by immediate verifier compatibility, but also by whether the packet preserves a high-value replanning corridor under partial observability.

We therefore treat packetization and value-guided replanning as complementary rather than competing views. Packetization defines the deployable interface contract, while value-guided replanning asks whether that contract preserves long-horizon recoverability. In other words, D06 should not collapse into either “better waypoint packets” or “better replanning rewards” alone. A deployable aerial VLN system needs both a controller-consumable packet schema and a way to judge whether accepted packets are still compatible with future correction under open-world uncertainty.

A final lesson from these local baselines is that packet validity should be interpreted as a **compound preservation problem** rather than a binary pass/fail state. LookasideVLN [REF: 2604.17190] implies that a packet can fail by dropping clause-level directional intent even when landmark grounding looks correct; LiveVLN [REF: 2604.19536] shows that a packet can fail by becoming stale under stop-and-go execution even when the original proposal was reasonable; and the deployability-oriented hierarchical system of Xu et al. [REF: 2604.21363] suggests that context integration itself can fail if memory-backed rationale is not preserved under runtime constraints. These observations motivate our next methodological move: packet evaluation should explicitly ask whether a proposal remains **clause-preserving, context-preserving, refresh-consistent, and controller-consumable** across the full execution chain, rather than only whether it initially points toward the right region.

### 2.6 Baseline Families and Attribution-Consistent Reading Rules

To avoid inflating the contribution of a packet-centric aerial VLN system, we organize recent baselines into four *attribution families* and constrain what each family is allowed to explain. This is not merely a writing convenience; it is a deployment-facing accounting rule. If a baseline improves one failure surface but does not alter the others, its gain should stay attached to that surface instead of being retroactively merged into a larger "better aerial planner" narrative.

The first family is **clause- and direction-preserving planner frontends**, represented by FineCog-Nav [REF: 2604.16298] and LookasideVLN [REF: 2604.17190]. These methods are most informative when the dominant question is whether language-side structure is preserved before execution: clause-level intent, egocentric directional relation, memory-backed landmark anchoring, and sentence-to-waypoint consistency. In our paper, gains from this family are therefore only allowed to explain improvements in *direction-conditioned instruction adherence*, *pre-verifier semantic mismatch reduction*, and *packet-facing clause retention*. They are not sufficient evidence for runtime survivability, controller compatibility, or cross-stage recovery on their own.

The second family is **runtime-survivability and live-execution systems**, represented by OnFly [REF: 2603.10682] and LiveVLN [REF: 2604.19536]. Their primary value lies in maintaining executable continuity under latency, monitoring, and refresh constraints. In our attribution rules, gains from this family are allowed to explain improvements in *verifier pass rate under onboard latency*, *packet staleness reduction*, *guarded execution continuity*, and *handoff survivability*. They should not be over-read as evidence that the underlying semantic planner is intrinsically better at clause interpretation or frontier ranking.

The third family is **representation-level geometry alignment**, represented by SpatialFly [REF: 2603.21046] and related geometry-guided aerial grounding methods. These methods matter when the dominant failure comes from 2D-3D mismatch, viewpoint distortion, or weak structural alignment between semantic tokens and spatial geometry. In our framework, gains from this family are only allowed to explain *representation-level grounding robustness*, *cross-view alignment stability*, and *geometry-aware path smoothness*. They are not sufficient evidence that the system has solved packet serialization, verifier compatibility, or long-horizon recovery.

The fourth family is **recoverability- and replanning-aware systems**, represented by OpenVLN [REF: 2511.06182] and other value-guided aerial replanning pipelines. Their value is that they ask whether a locally executable step preserves a future correction corridor under uncertainty. In our reading rules, gains from this family are only allowed to explain *recovery-corridor preservation*, *replanning-value retention*, and *long-horizon correction friendliness*. They should not be over-read as proof of better clause parsing, lower-latency execution, or cleaner packet handoff.

These attribution-consistent reading rules directly shape how we evaluate our own system. If our packet-centric framework outperforms a direction-aware frontend, we only claim superiority on packet-facing clause preservation if the evidence actually lives there. If we outperform runtime-survivability systems, we only claim gains in live refresh and handoff stability when the evidence isolates those axes. This prevents D06 from collapsing semantically distinct gains—language adherence, runtime survivability, geometry robustness, and recoverability—into one oversized packet story. Instead, the paper argues that a deployable aerial VLN system should be judged by whether these four gain surfaces are jointly preserved through a common packet interface, not by whether one of them alone happens to improve.

### 2.8 Controller-Consumption Accountability and the Limits of Planner-Time Correctness

A growing deployment gap in aerial VLN is that packet quality is usually judged at *planner emission time* rather than at the moment the downstream controller actually consumes the packet. OnFly [REF: 2603.10682] already implies this gap in practice: its dual-agent onboard design, hybrid keyframe/latest-frame memory, and semantic-geometric verifier improve runtime safety and progress monitoring, but the main measured gains are still closest to planner-verifier-time correctness and continuity. HTNav [REF: 2604.08883] sharpens a second part of the problem from the planner side: hierarchical macro-planning and local reactive control can improve waypoint quality and execution stability, yet they do not by themselves specify whether a semantically valid macro waypoint remains clause-preserving, context-preserving, and manipulation-ready when the low-level controller finally consumes it under delayed refresh and stage transition.

AerialVLA [REF: 2603.14363] exposes a complementary boundary from the opposite design extreme. Its minimalist end-to-end visual-language-to-control pipeline avoids explicit packet serialization and achieves strong autonomous navigation behavior with dual-view perception and fuzzy directional prompting, showing that planner-time semantic grounding can indeed translate into competitive continuous-control outputs. However, precisely because the intermediate interface is implicit, AerialVLA leaves under-specified whether the directional prior, target identity, and stage-conditioned execution assumptions remain *inspectable* and *honest* at controller-consumption time. In other words, end-to-end control can reduce orchestration overhead, but it also weakens our ability to distinguish semantic correctness at emission time from handoff-value preservation at actual consume time.

This distinction matters because many aerial VLN packets degrade *after* they were locally reasonable. A packet can be semantically correct at planning time, verifier-compatible at handoff time, and still fail at controller-consumption time because heading priors, local anchor identity, guarded-prefix assumptions, or manipulation-ready shell constraints have silently shifted. Existing systems rarely separate these horizons. As a result, planner-side gains, verifier-side gains, and true controller-consumption gains are often reported under a shared success narrative.

Our paper therefore treats controller-consumption accountability as a first-class deployment boundary. Related systems such as OnFly, HTNav, LookasideVLN, LiveVLN, and AerialVLA each illuminate one face of the problem—planner quality, directional adherence, runtime continuity, or end-to-end control sufficiency—but none fully define a common rule for when a packet may still be credited as preserving handoff value at the instant of downstream consumption. This motivates our later D06 reading rule: a packet that is only planner-correct or verifier-correct must not be over-promoted to a full NtM handoff claim unless it remains honest through controller consumption.

### 2.9 Implicit End-to-End Interfaces as a Strong but Non-Inspectable Baseline

Minimalist end-to-end systems should not be treated only as missing-interface baselines; they are also the strongest counterexample against any claim that explicit packetization is automatically necessary. AerialVLA [REF: 2603.14363] is especially important here because it shows that a compact latent interface can still yield competitive UAV navigation with low orchestration overhead and good responsiveness. For D06, this means we must argue against a *strong baseline*: if an implicit controller-facing latent already works well, explicit packet interfaces are only justified when they unlock a capability that latent end-to-end control cannot honestly expose.

We therefore frame AerialVLA-style systems as **strong-but-non-inspectable baselines**. Their strongest evidence is about *continuous-control sufficiency*: semantic grounding can survive long enough to produce useful online actions. Their weakest point is not navigation competence itself, but the absence of an explicit accounting layer for when directional priors, target identity, stage tags, and guarded execution assumptions are still valid at the exact consume instant. This is the key contrast we need for D06: the paper should not claim that packetization is better because it is more structured, but because it makes controller-consumption honesty, delayed-consumption audit, and NtM handoff attribution measurable.

Under this reading, the main comparison between packet-centric D06 and end-to-end AerialVLA is not simply modular versus end-to-end. It is **inspectable delayed-consumption accountability versus latent execution sufficiency**. If a future result only shows higher success without improving consume-time honesty, then D06 should not over-claim superiority over AerialVLA; at best it would have shown a different path to strong navigation. Conversely, if packetization allows us to preserve or recover handoff value specifically at the `approach → manipulate-ready` boundary, then the explicit interface earns its complexity by exposing and protecting a deployment property that implicit latent control does not directly certify.

### 2.10 Consume-Time Packet Contracts and Boundary-Specific Evidence

A remaining weakness in current D06 framing is that controller-consumption accountability is still mostly argued as a qualitative principle rather than as a field-level contract. To make delayed-consumption honesty operational, we define the packet-facing evidence boundary around four consume-time fields: **directional prior survival** `A^{dir}`, **context-anchor survival** `A^{ctx}`, **manipulation-shell survival** `A^{shell}`, and **guarded-prefix survival** `A^{guard}`. Intuitively, these fields ask whether the packet still preserves the instruction-conditioned heading relation, the target identity and local anchor chain, the pose/budget assumptions needed for manipulation-ready entry, and the short-horizon execution prefix that justified safe continuation in the first place.

This contract-oriented view clarifies why planner-time correctness and controller-consumption honesty are not interchangeable. A planner may emit a semantically plausible packet and a verifier may still accept it under a stale but bounded trust region, yet the packet can already have lost one of the four consume-time fields by the time the downstream controller actually commits. In that case, the result should not be reported as packet-interface superiority; at most it supports a weaker explanation such as planner-side semantic gain, runtime continuity gain, or low-level rescue under a surviving safety shell.

The same contract also sharpens the comparison against AerialVLA, OnFly, HTNav, and LiveVLN. AerialVLA is strongest when latent control remains behaviorally sufficient, but it leaves `A^{dir}/A^{ctx}/A^{shell}/A^{guard}` largely implicit. OnFly and LiveVLN preserve continuity and guarded execution better, yet they still stop short of reporting which consume-time packet fields remain honest at the `approach → manipulate-ready` boundary. HTNav helps separate macro planning from reactive execution, but does not by itself certify that the serialized target identity and stage-conditioned assumptions remain valid when the controller consumes them. This suggests that D06 should compare methods not only by task success or verifier pass rate, but by **which consume-time fields survive, and in which boundary window they survive**.

A unified-model line further reinforces this boundary. Aerial Vision-Language Navigation with a Unified Framework for Spatial, Temporal and Embodied Reasoning [REF: 2512.08639] shows that a lightweight monocular next-token formulation can close much of the gap to heavier aerial VLN stacks under tight sensing constraints. Its main value for D06 is not that it solves packet accountability, but that it establishes another strong planner-time baseline: better tokenized spatial-temporal reasoning can improve navigation even when explicit packet contracts are absent. This means D06 must be careful not to over-credit any gain that only appears at planner time or immediate verifier bind; such a gain may simply reflect stronger unified aerial reasoning rather than stronger consume-time handoff honesty.

Under this reading, the main reviewer-facing question becomes: *when a packet succeeds late, which fields were still alive at consume time?* If only `A^{guard}` survives, the evidence ceiling is continuity or bounded rescue. If `A^{dir}` and `A^{ctx}` survive but `A^{shell}` collapses at the manipulation boundary, the result supports planner-side semantic alignment but not full NtM handoff preservation. Only when the packet survives with all four fields still honest through the `approach → manipulate-ready` transition should D06 promote the gain to a full packet-contract or handoff-accountability claim.

### 2.11 Limitations of Existing Work

Existing aerial navigation and VLN approaches share critical gaps:
(1) No existing method provides a complete aerial VLN framework with 3D semantic frontier exploration.
(2) Ground VLN methods cannot transfer to aerial platforms due to 2D map assumptions and absence of dynamics modeling.
(3) Navigation and manipulation are treated as separate problems; no unified architecture handles the full search-to-grasp pipeline.
(4) Dynamics constraints are either ignored or handled as post-hoc filters rather than integrated into the exploration cost function.
(5) Existing aerial navigation benchmarks lack dynamics-aware metrics and 3D spatial reasoning evaluation.
(6) Directional cues and egocentric spatial relations in natural language remain under-modeled; most current methods emphasize landmarks, memory, or lookahead depth, but do not explicitly convert direction words into structured waypoint priors.
(7) Temporally updated online observations are still weakly integrated into planner-verifier-controller pipelines, making live execution drift and packet staleness underexplored failure modes.
(8) The planner output is rarely formalized as a reusable packet-level interface, so semantic planning quality, verifier compatibility, and controller handoff quality are hard to disentangle during deployment.
(9) Existing responsive aerial systems improve latency but still provide limited evidence on whether packetized interfaces remain stable under downstream controller consumption and cross-stage handoff.
(10) Even recent long-horizon VLA planning systems such as LoHo-Manip [REF: 2604.21924] emphasize progress-aware traces and receding-horizon continuation, but they still leave open how aerial navigation should serialize directional priors, flight-envelope budgets, and verifier-ready execution state into a shared packet object.
(11) Geometry-guided representation methods such as SpatialFly [REF: 2603.21046] reduce 2D–3D mismatch at the feature level, but they still stop short of exposing a verifier-ready packet abstraction, making it difficult to diagnose whether geometry-aware gains survive runtime refresh, stage handoff, and downstream controller consumption.
(12) Existing onboard systems such as OnFly [REF: 2603.10682] and runtime frameworks such as LiveVLN [REF: 2604.19536] clearly improve either verification-aware monitoring or stop-and-go latency, yet they still stop short of defining a single packet contract that jointly preserves semantic intent, directional priors, guarded execution state, and manipulation-ready handoff information across the full aerial deployment stack.
(13) Even strong direction-aware or cognitively decomposed planners such as LookasideVLN [REF: 2604.17190] and FineCog-Nav [REF: 2604.16298] mainly improve *planner-side* clause adherence, direction parsing, or memory-supported reasoning, but they rarely measure whether these gains survive **packet retention** across `search → approach → manipulate-ready` transitions.
(14) Existing online aerial stacks still under-specify whether a packet that is semantically correct at planning time remains **manipulation-ready** at handoff time; current evaluations seldom separate *planner correctness*, *refresh-time context retention*, and *navigation-to-manipulation handoff stability* as three distinct deployment variables.
(15) Current evaluations rarely distinguish a packet that is **navigation-correct** from one that is **manipulation-ready**: a proposal may preserve search-time clause satisfaction yet still lose the pose precision, contextual anchor, or guarded execution state required at the `approach → manipulate-ready` boundary.
(16) Existing packet-refresh pipelines also lack an explicit retention-aware gate: refresh is often treated as uniformly beneficial, but in aerial deployment an aggressive refresh schedule can silently trade planner-side semantic improvement for worse downstream handoff stability and manipulation readiness.
(17) Recent system accelerators such as FreqCache [REF: 2604.24391] reduce inference latency for embodied VLN, but they do not explain whether cached tokens preserve packet freshness at the **controller-consumption instant** rather than only at the planner emission instant.
(18) Strong local safe-flight planners such as SAGA [REF: 2605.02301] improve obstacle-aware execution quality and action-anchor selection, yet they still operate below the packet semantics layer and therefore cannot determine whether a semantically valid navigation packet remains clause-preserving, context-preserving, and handoff-stable after being consumed by the downstream controller.
(19) Existing aerial VLN evaluations still rarely distinguish **planner-time correctness**, **verifier-time compatibility**, and **controller-consumption honesty**. A packet may remain attractive at emission time and even pass semantic-geometric checks, while already losing the directional prior, contextual anchor, or guarded execution state needed to preserve manipulation-ready handoff value when it is finally consumed.
(20) Even hierarchical systems such as HTNav [REF: 2604.08883] and onboard systems such as OnFly [REF: 2603.10682] stop short of formalizing a shared packet-level contract for **last honest consumption window**. Without this distinction, local planning or runtime continuity gains can be over-reported as full navigation-to-manipulation interface gains.
(21) Minimalist end-to-end systems such as AerialVLA [REF: 2603.14363] demonstrate that semantic grounding can map directly into continuous control, but they still leave unclear whether the latent interface preserves **inspectable directional priors, target identity, and stage-conditioned execution assumptions** at controller-consumption time; this weakens honest attribution between end-to-end control sufficiency and true packet-level handoff accountability.
(22) Current aerial VLN papers also rarely specify how to **freeze claims under route-specific evidence**. When gains could alternatively come from semantic decomposition, latency compensation, local control continuation, or safety fallback, most evaluations still report a single headline success gain instead of routing the result to the weakest honest explanation class.
(23) Existing evaluations still under-specify a **consume-time packet contract**: even when planner-time correctness and verifier-time compatibility are reported, methods rarely disclose whether directional priors, context anchors, manipulation-shell assumptions, and guarded execution prefixes all survive together at the actual controller-consumption boundary.
(24) As a result, late-stage success near the `approach → manipulate-ready` transition is often over-attributed. A method may preserve continuity or semantic plausibility while already losing one or more packet fields required for honest NtM handoff, yet current papers seldom separate **full packet-contract survival** from weaker planner-side gain or bounded rescue.

## 3. Method

### 3.1 System Overview

Our system processes a natural language instruction (e.g., "find the red fire hydrant and photograph it") through a three-stage pipeline:

```
Language Instruction
        │
   ┌────┴────┐
   │ VLM Goal │  High-level: semantic target parsing + scene priors
   │  Parser  │  → target description embedding + exploration priority
   └────┬────┘
        │
   ┌────┴──────────┐
   │ 3D VL-Frontier │  Mid-level: volumetric frontier map maintenance
   │  Map + Dynamics│  → VLM semantic scoring for frontier selection
   │  Cost Function │  → dynamics constraint filtering
   └────┬──────────┘
        │
   ┌────┴────┐
   │  Flight  │  Low-level: trajectory generation + PID/MPC execution
   │ Controller│
   └────┬────┘
        │ (when target enters operational range)
   ┌────┴────┐
   │  NtM    │  → precise approach + grasp/photograph/place
   │  Module │
   └─────────┘
```

### 3.2 Aerial VL-Frontier Map (C1)

We represent the environment as a 3D occupancy voxel grid $\mathcal{V} \in \{0, 1, \text{unknown}\}^{W \times H \times D}$ updated from UAV RGB-D observations. Frontier voxels $\mathcal{F} \subset \mathcal{V}$ are defined as free voxels adjacent to unknown voxels in 3D space, capturing exploration opportunities in the full volumetric workspace including vertical dimensions. Rather than directly selecting raw frontier cells, we cluster frontier voxels into candidate semantic viewpoints and attach a lightweight language-grounded proposal packet to each candidate, including target pose, yaw hint, altitude band, and semantic confidence.

For each frontier cluster $f_i \in \mathcal{F}$, we compute a semantic relevance score:
$$s_i = \text{VLM-Score}(I_{\text{rendered}}(f_i), \text{goal\_embedding})$$
where $I_{\text{rendered}}(f_i)$ is a rendered view from the frontier viewpoint and `goal_embedding` encodes the language target description. We further augment this semantic score with a direction-conditioned prior extracted from the instruction. Specifically, a lightweight direction parser converts egocentric spatial phrases such as *left of the red truck*, *behind the tower*, or *slightly upper-right* into structured waypoint priors, which are used to bias frontier proposal ranking before execution. This module is intentionally narrow: it improves waypoint proposal quality without replacing 3D frontier exploration itself.

To support online execution, frontier proposals are emitted as packetized subgoals rather than one-shot plans. Each packet contains a guarded execution prefix and a revisable tail, allowing the system to refresh frontier ranking as new observations arrive while preserving short-horizon control continuity. This design is motivated by runtime findings that stop-and-go execution can dominate deployment latency even when the planner itself is semantically correct.

### 3.3 Dynamics-Aware Navigation Policy (C2)

The frontier selection policy minimizes a composite cost:
$$f^* = \arg\min_{f_i \in \mathcal{F}} \left[ \lambda_1 \cdot d(f_i) + \lambda_2 \cdot E(f_i) + \lambda_3 \cdot R(f_i) - \lambda_4 \cdot s_i \right]$$
where $d(f_i)$ is travel distance, $E(f_i)$ is estimated energy cost, $R(f_i)$ is collision risk, and $s_i$ is semantic relevance. We additionally attach stage-aware execution budgets to every frontier packet, including maximum velocity, minimum obstacle clearance, maximum climb/descent rate, and a bounded replanning horizon. In this way, dynamics are not treated as a post-hoc rejector only; they shape which frontiers are considered promising in the first place.

To make this executable in a real aerial stack, we define a **Semantic Waypoint Packet**
\[
\mathcal{W}_t = \{p_t^{\text{goal}},\; \psi_t^{\text{yaw}},\; b_t^{\text{alt}},\; c_t^{\text{sem}},\; q_t^{\text{dir}},\; g_t^{\text{stage}},\; u_t^{\text{budget}},\; \rho_t^{\text{guard}}\},
\]
where $p_t^{\text{goal}}$ is the local target waypoint, $\psi_t^{\text{yaw}}$ is the heading hint, $b_t^{\text{alt}}$ is the admissible altitude band, $c_t^{\text{sem}}$ is semantic confidence, $q_t^{\text{dir}}$ is the direction-conditioned adherence prior, $g_t^{\text{stage}}$ is the execution stage tag, $u_t^{\text{budget}}$ contains velocity/clearance/replanning budgets, and $\rho_t^{\text{guard}}$ defines the guarded execution prefix. This packetized form lets the planner expose *what it wants*, *how strongly language supports it*, and *under what flight envelope it remains legal* in one interface.

Execution follows a packet-verifier-controller chain. After the planner proposes a semantic waypoint packet, a verifier checks semantic validity, geometric executability, and budget compliance before the low-level controller commits to the guarded action prefix. If new observations invalidate the packet, only the revisable tail is refreshed, reducing stop-and-go behavior while preserving safety. This lets us combine direction-aware language priors with live execution updates without forcing full re-planning at every timestep.

Concretely, we model packet quality as a three-term decomposition rather than a single opaque confidence scalar:
\[
Q_t^{\text{pkt}} = \alpha_1 S_t^{\text{sem}} + \alpha_2 C_t^{\text{dir}} + \alpha_3 K_t^{\text{cont}},
\]
where $S_t^{\text{sem}}$ measures semantic grounding consistency between the waypoint packet and the language goal, $C_t^{\text{dir}}$ measures direction-conditioned adherence induced by egocentric spatial phrases, and $K_t^{\text{cont}}$ measures runtime continuity under guarded execution and refresh. The verifier then evaluates a distinct executability score
\[
F_t^{\text{exec}} = \beta_1 G_t^{\text{geo}} + \beta_2 D_t^{\text{dyn}} + \beta_3 B_t^{\text{budget}} - \beta_4 Z_t^{\text{stale}},
\]
where $G_t^{\text{geo}}$ is geometric flyability, $D_t^{\text{dyn}}$ is dynamics feasibility, $B_t^{\text{budget}}$ is compliance with stage-aware velocity/clearance/replanning budgets, and $Z_t^{\text{stale}}$ penalizes packet staleness under delayed refresh. This separation is important: a packet may be semantically correct yet no longer executable because the observation stream has shifted, or it may be locally executable but directionally inconsistent with the instruction.

Crucially, the verifier is not only a safety filter but also a bookkeeping layer for runtime continuity. We therefore track whether packet quality survives handoff to execution by explicitly logging verifier pass rate, packet staleness, heading drift, refresh latency, and controller-consumption failures. This framing is motivated by OnFly-style semantic-geometric verification, by LiveVLN-style guarded runtime execution, and by the broader observation from local evidence that low-latency aerial systems still lack a strongly formalized planner-controller packet interface.

A second deployment concern is whether planner-side gains survive downstream controller consumption when the planner architecture changes. FineCog-Nav suggests that decomposing the planner into role-specific modules can improve clause-level instruction adherence and memory usage, while HTNav shows that explicit macro-planning and local control decomposition can stabilize action selection in continuous aerial spaces. We therefore treat packet generation as the invariant contract across planner families: monolithic planners, hierarchical planners, and cognitively decomposed planners must all emit the same packet schema. This prevents planner-side architectural gains from being conflated with interface redesign, and turns packet metrics into the main fairness layer for comparison.

Under this view, the key question of D06 is not simply whether a planner finds better waypoints, but whether it produces packets that remain verifiable, refreshable, and controller-compatible under realistic onboard latency. This directly connects our method to recent local evidence from OnFly, ViSA, HTNav, and Ro-SLM: semantic proposal quality, direction adherence, onboard budget compliance, and execution continuity should be optimized jointly, but diagnosed separately.

### 3.4 Navigate-then-Manipulate Architecture (C3)

We model aerial VLN as a two-regime process with an explicit **handoff boundary** between search-time navigation and manipulation-time servoing. During the navigation regime, the planner maintains a 3D semantic frontier map, semantic waypoint packets, and a persistent target hypothesis bank. Once the top-ranked target hypothesis satisfies three conditions simultaneously—(i) semantic confidence exceeds a threshold, (ii) target pose covariance falls below a manipulation-ready bound, and (iii) the UAV reaches a safe operational shell around the object—the system emits `handoff_tag = manipulate-ready` and switches from exploration mode to local approach and interaction control.

The handoff is not a hard one-shot jump. Instead, the navigation stack exports a **manipulation-ready packet** containing the target pose estimate, yaw hint, altitude band, semantic confidence, progress score, and a local safety budget. The manipulation module then performs short-horizon visual servoing or grasp/inspection actions inside this bounded shell, while keeping access to the navigation-time 3D map for occlusion reasoning, retreat planning, and target re-acquisition. This design lets the system reuse search-time spatial understanding instead of restarting perception from scratch at the manipulation stage.

To make the transition robust, we further introduce a **bounded fallback loop**. If manipulation fails because of target drift, poor viewpoint alignment, or local geometric infeasibility, the system does not fully restart long-range exploration. Instead, it first attempts packet repair inside a local recovery radius by updating `target_pose / yaw_hint / altitude_band`; only when repeated repair fails does it downgrade `handoff_tag` back to `approach` or `search`, re-entering the navigation regime with the previously accumulated map and memory intact. In this way, Navigate-then-Manipulate becomes a reversible closed loop rather than a brittle sequential pipeline.

### 3.5 Packet Freshness, Trust-Region Shrinkage, and Non-Promotion Rules

A packet that was semantically correct at planner time should not be assumed to retain the same execution privilege under delayed controller consumption. We therefore associate each semantic waypoint packet with a freshness state
\[
\phi_t = \{a_t^{\text{intent}},\; \delta_t^{\text{lat}},\; \epsilon_t^{\text{obs}},\; \sigma_t^{\text{stage}}\},
\]
where \(a_t^{\text{intent}}\) is the age of the currently bound intent clause, \(\delta_t^{\text{lat}}\) is end-to-end latency offset from planner emission to controller-consumption opportunity, \(\epsilon_t^{\text{obs}}\) measures observation drift between the packet-supporting view and the latest view, and \(\sigma_t^{\text{stage}}\) marks whether the stage boundary has changed since emission. Instead of treating freshness as a scalar confidence decay only, we use it to decide **what kind of claim the packet is still allowed to support**.

Concretely, we partition packets into three execution regimes. A packet is **fresh-executable** if its clause, anchor, and stage assumptions remain inside a controller-consumable trust region. It is **stale-but-bounded** if the controller may still safely continue a guarded local prefix, but the packet no longer supports a strong semantic-interface claim because one or more freshness channels have drifted beyond planner-time assumptions. It is **non-promotable** if consumption would rely primarily on local continuation, safety fallback, or low-level stabilization rather than on the originally serialized semantic interface. In other words, `can still fly safely` and `still honestly supports packet-level semantic handoff` are different judgments.

We implement this by shrinking the admissible controller-consumption trust region as freshness degrades:
\[
\mathcal{T}_t = \mathcal{T}_0 \cap \mathcal{B}(\epsilon_t^{\text{obs}}, \delta_t^{\text{lat}}, a_t^{\text{intent}}, \sigma_t^{\text{stage}}),
\]
where \(\mathcal{T}_0\) is the planner-time admissible packet region and \(\mathcal{B}(\cdot)\) is a freshness-conditioned contraction operator. If the remaining trust region only certifies guarded geometric continuation but no longer certifies clause-preserving target approach or manipulation-ready handoff, the result must be frozen to a weaker route-specific explanation. This rule is especially important for D06 because aerial stacks often gain safety and continuity from controller-local stabilization even when the packet itself has already gone stale.

The key non-promotion rule is therefore: **a stale packet may preserve continuity without preserving semantic-interface honesty**. Any gain observed after freshness collapse must not be promoted beyond the weakest route still directly certified by the surviving packet fields. This prevents late-window success from being over-read as evidence that the packet contract remained valid when the real source of success was low-level continuation or safety-shell rescue.

### 3.6 Route-Specific Claim Routing for Aerial VLA

To make this non-promotion rule reviewer-facing, we attach a compact routing tuple
\[
\Gamma = (w^+,\; w^{\dagger},\; r^*,\; q^*)
\]
to each reported gain, where \(w^+\) is the earliest window with a positive signal, \(w^{\dagger}\) is the last window in which the claim remains freshness-honest, \(r^*\) is the dominant evidence route, and \(q^*\) is the weakest honest claim level allowed by that route. We use four primary routes: **semantic decomposition**, **latency compensation**, **low-level continuation**, and **safety fallback**. A gain routed to semantic decomposition may support stronger packet-interface conclusions only if freshness remains honest through controller consumption. A gain routed mainly to latency compensation supports a runtime-continuity conclusion, not a stronger semantic serialization claim. A gain routed to low-level continuation or safety fallback must be frozen to continuity or robustness language and cannot be promoted to packet-level superiority.

This routing protocol is deliberately asymmetric. It is easy for aerial systems to inherit gains from strong controller-local execution even when planner packets have already lost clause specificity or stage consistency. The tuple \(\Gamma\) forces us to answer four questions in order: where the gain first appeared, how long it stayed honest under delayed consumption, what evidence route most plausibly produced it, and what is the strongest remaining claim that route can support. Only after these four questions are answered may we compare against strong-but-non-inspectable baselines such as AerialVLA.

Under this reading, D06 does not compete with end-to-end aerial VLA only by reporting better aggregate success. It competes by showing that more of its gains survive **freshness-aware controller consumption** under an inspectable route. If a result improves success while quickly collapsing into latency compensation, local continuation, or safety fallback, then it may still be valuable engineering evidence, but it is not yet evidence of stronger semantic packet interfaces. This is the paper-level guardrail that keeps D06 honest when comparing inspectable packetized execution against latent end-to-end sufficiency.

To keep this routing operational rather than rhetorical, we explicitly bind each route to an admissible evidence family and an upper claim ceiling. **Semantic decomposition** may inherit evidence from clause-aware planner frontends such as LookasideVLN and FineCog-Nav only when the gain remains visible at the controller-consumption boundary rather than disappearing after verifier bind. **Latency compensation** may inherit evidence from OnFly-style frequency decoupling and LiveVLN-style guarded refresh, but its strongest honest claim is reduced stale-execution risk or improved runtime continuity, not packet-interface superiority. **Low-level continuation** covers cases where success is mainly preserved by controller-local stabilization or short-horizon geometric continuation after packet freshness has already collapsed; such results must be frozen to bounded continuity claims. **Safety fallback** covers gains dominated by conservative execution shells, reject-and-recover ladders, or last-minute safe-route substitution, and therefore cannot be promoted beyond robustness-preserving language even if aggregate success improves.

This discipline is especially important when comparing against AerialVLA. The local reading notes show that AerialVLA is a strong minimalist end-to-end baseline because dual-view perception and fuzzy directional prompting can produce robust continuous navigation without explicit packet serialization, especially on TravelUAV unseen scenes. However, that strength mainly certifies **latent execution sufficiency** rather than **inspectable consume-time honesty**. Under our routing rule, D06 only earns a stronger claim than AerialVLA when a gain remains freshness-honest through the `approach → manipulate-ready` boundary and is still best explained by semantic packet structure rather than by controller-local continuation, latency compensation, or safety fallback. Otherwise, the comparison must be frozen to the weakest honest route, even if D06 reports a better top-line success rate.

A practical reviewer-facing summary is therefore: first locate the earliest positive window \(w^+\), then locate the last freshness-honest window \(w^{\dagger}\), then assign the dominant route \(r^*\), and only then allow the weakest admissible claim tag \(q^*\). This four-step routing prevents us from merging planner-side clause gains, verifier-side continuity gains, and controller-side rescue gains into a single inflated packet-first story. For D06, that honesty matters more than one extra headline point of success because the real contribution is not merely to navigate better, but to explain **why the navigation result is still trustworthy when the downstream aerial controller actually consumes it**.

### 3.6.1 Packet-Contract Fields for Consume-Time Accountability

The current D06 literature already suggests that many aerial VLN systems fail not because they cannot produce a plausible next waypoint, but because they do not serialize *enough of the planner's reasoning contract* for later consume-time auditing. OnFly exposes runtime continuity and semantic-geometric verification, AerialVLA exposes strong latent execution sufficiency, and HTNav exposes hierarchical decomposition between macro planning and reactive local control. However, none of them fully specifies which fields must remain valid for a packet to still support an honest `approach → manipulate-ready` claim after delayed controller consumption.

We therefore refine the Semantic Waypoint Packet into a **packet contract**
\[
\Pi_t = \{g_t,\; y_t,\; b_t,\; c_t,\; d_t,\; s_t,\; u_t,\; \rho_t,\; \kappa_t,\; h_t\},
\]
where \(g_t\) is the target waypoint or target-relative goal, \(y_t\) is the yaw prior, \(b_t\) is the altitude band, \(c_t\) is semantic confidence, \(d_t\) is the directional clause binding, \(s_t\) is the stage tag, \(u_t\) is the execution budget, \(\rho_t\) is the guarded executable prefix, \(\kappa_t\) is the contextual anchor bundle, and \(h_t\) is the handoff shell descriptor required for local interaction readiness. Compared with a plain waypoint interface, the added fields \(\kappa_t\) and \(h_t\) are the key pieces that stop D06 from over-crediting planner-time correctness as downstream handoff value.

The contextual anchor bundle \(\kappa_t\) stores the minimum memory-backed support that makes a packet still interpretable after refresh, including the active landmark anchor, the clause-local target identity cue, and the provenance of the confidence estimate. The handoff shell descriptor \(h_t\) stores the local readiness envelope for downstream use, including acceptable pose covariance, heading tolerance, altitude tolerance, and the safety-shell radius under which the packet may still be consumed by the NtM stack. Intuitively, \(\kappa_t\) tells us whether the packet still means the same thing, while \(h_t\) tells us whether the packet is still worth handing off.

This packet-contract view yields a stricter definition of consume-time validity. A packet is not consume-honest merely because it remains geometrically flyable; it must also preserve the directional clause binding, the contextual anchor bundle, and a valid handoff shell. We therefore define the consume-time contract score
\[
C_t^{\text{contract}} = \mu_1 A_t^{\text{dir}} + \mu_2 A_t^{\text{ctx}} + \mu_3 A_t^{\text{shell}} + \mu_4 A_t^{\text{guard}},
\]
where \(A_t^{\text{dir}}\) measures directional-clause preservation, \(A_t^{\text{ctx}}\) measures contextual-anchor preservation, \(A_t^{\text{shell}}\) measures handoff-shell validity, and \(A_t^{\text{guard}}\) measures whether the guarded execution prefix remains truthful under delayed refresh. Only when all four terms remain above threshold may a gain be promoted beyond planner-side semantic gain or runtime continuity gain.

This addition sharpens D06's comparison to AerialVLA-style latent interfaces. End-to-end latent control may remain strong at producing feasible actions, but it does not directly expose whether a packet-equivalent directional clause, contextual anchor, or handoff shell is still intact at controller-consumption time. Our claim is therefore not that explicit packet contracts are always better, but that they are necessary if the paper wants to make an *inspectable consume-time accountability* claim rather than only a *continuous-control sufficiency* claim. In that sense, packet contracts are the minimum structure needed to separate `still executable` from `still honestly handoffable`.

### 3.7 Navigate-then-Manipulate Architecture (C3)

Long-horizon replanning evidence from LoHo-Manip [REF: 2604.21924] suggests that progress-aware trace prediction is a powerful closed-loop scaffold even when low-level execution remains local and reactive. We do not directly import its manipulation setting into aerial VLN, but we absorb one useful lesson: a planner should expose not only *the next waypoint*, but also *why this waypoint is the correct continuation of the remaining task*. We therefore attach an auxiliary progress trace to each packet during training, consisting of a lightweight ordered subgoal sketch and a geometric trace cue over the local 3D map. At test time this trace need not be executed directly; instead, it regularizes packet generation toward progress-aware continuity under online refresh.

This auxiliary trace serves two purposes in D06. First, it reduces ambiguity when multiple frontier proposals are semantically plausible but correspond to different stages of the instruction, e.g., "pass the stairs, then inspect the red sign near the upper balcony." Second, it gives the verifier a weak notion of task-progress consistency: if a refreshed packet remains locally feasible but breaks the monotonic structure of the remaining progress trace, the system can downgrade it before controller handoff. In this way, progress traces become supervisory structure for packet continuity rather than a separate execution interface.

Recent local evidence also suggests that progress traces should not be treated as geometry-only hints. FineCog-Nav [REF: 2604.16298] shows that clause-level instruction adherence and memory-conditioned reasoning can be improved by explicitly separating language, attention, memory, imagination, and decision roles; LookasideVLN [REF: 2604.17190] shows that directional phrases provide lightweight but high-value structure for aerial waypoint ranking; and LiveVLN [REF: 2604.19536] shows that runtime continuity depends on whether a partial plan can be refreshed without breaking executable flow. Taken together, these works motivate a stronger version of our trace: each progress element should retain not only a geometric target, but also the clause-level directional and contextual support that justified it. This keeps progress supervision aligned with packet refresh and prevents the trace from degenerating into a purely spatial breadcrumb list.

To make this measurable, we define each trace element as
\[
\tau_k = \{c_k^{\text{clause}},\; r_k^{\text{dir}},\; m_k^{\text{ctx}},\; g_k^{\text{geo}},\; s_k^{\text{stage}}\},
\]
where \(c_k^{\text{clause}}\) is the active instruction clause, \(r_k^{\text{dir}}\) is the associated directional relation, \(m_k^{\text{ctx}}\) is the memory-backed contextual anchor, \(g_k^{\text{geo}}\) is the geometric waypoint cue, and \(s_k^{\text{stage}}\) is the expected execution stage. During packet generation, the planner predicts not only the next packet \(\mathcal{W}_t\), but also whether \(\mathcal{W}_t\) preserves the currently active trace element without dropping directional or contextual support. This yields an auxiliary retention score
\[
R_t^{\text{trace}} = \eta_1 A_t^{\text{clause}} + \eta_2 A_t^{\text{dir}} + \eta_3 A_t^{\text{ctx}} + \eta_4 A_t^{\text{stage}},
\]
where each term measures alignment between the emitted packet and the active progress trace along clause, direction, context, and stage dimensions.

This retention-oriented formulation turns progress traces into more than a planning hint. It gives the verifier an interpretable basis for rejecting packets that are geometrically flyable yet semantically degraded—for example, packets that still point toward the correct landmark region but silently drop a directional modifier such as *behind*, or packets that preserve the target class but forget the context that made the subgoal valid. In this sense, progress traces function as a weak supervisory scaffold for **memory-to-packet retention**, helping D06 measure not only whether the planner can think with context, but whether it can serialize that context into a packet the downstream controller can safely trust.

### 3.7 Clause-Aware Packet Auditing and Memory-to-Packet Retention

The local literature sweep suggests that aerial VLN systems now fail less often because they cannot propose *any* waypoint, and more often because they fail to preserve the right clause-level intent across refresh, verification, and downstream execution. FineCog-Nav [REF: 2604.16298] makes this visible by separating language understanding, memory use, and decision stages; LookasideVLN [REF: 2604.17190] shows that directional relations are often the cheapest high-value cue; and LiveVLN [REF: 2604.19536] shows that runtime continuity depends on whether a revisable tail still preserves actionable intent. We therefore introduce an explicit **clause-aware packet auditing** layer whose purpose is not to generate waypoints, but to check whether packet fields still faithfully serialize the currently active language clause.

Formally, let the active clause state be
\[
\chi_t = \{c_t^{\text{text}},\; r_t^{\text{dir}},\; a_t^{\text{anchor}},\; \pi_t^{\text{stage}}\},
\]
where \(c_t^{\text{text}}\) is the active instruction clause, \(r_t^{\text{dir}}\) is the associated directional relation, \(a_t^{\text{anchor}}\) is the memory-backed landmark anchor, and \(\pi_t^{\text{stage}}\) is the intended execution phase. Given a semantic waypoint packet \(\mathcal{W}_t\), the auditor computes a clause-preservation score
\[
A_t^{\text{clause}} = \eta_1 \cdot \mathrm{match}(q_t^{\text{dir}}, r_t^{\text{dir}})
+ \eta_2 \cdot \mathrm{match}(g_t^{\text{stage}}, \pi_t^{\text{stage}})
+ \eta_3 \cdot \mathrm{retain}(\mathcal{W}_t, a_t^{\text{anchor}}),
\]
which separately measures directional agreement, stage agreement, and anchor retention. This prevents a packet from being counted as valid merely because it is locally flyable: a packet that forgets the active clause or drops the anchor landmark is treated as semantically degraded even if the controller could still execute it.

This auditing layer also gives us a clean definition of **memory-to-packet retention**. Many planners appear to benefit from memory during internal reasoning, but the deployment chain only receives what is serialized into the packet. We therefore define a retention ratio that tracks how often memory-backed cues actually survive into controller-consumable fields. This distinction matters for D06 because a planner may reason with history while still handing downstream modules an under-specified waypoint. In our framework, such a case counts as a packet-quality failure rather than a hidden planner success.

To sharpen the deployment meaning of this ratio, we decompose packet preservation into four binary properties: **clause-preserving**, **context-preserving**, **refresh-consistent**, and **controller-consumable**. A packet is clause-preserving if the active instruction clause and directional relation remain serialized in packet fields; context-preserving if the landmark anchor, stage hint, and confidence provenance that justified the proposal are still available after planner emission; refresh-consistent if online packet updates do not silently erase those fields while revising the executable tail; and controller-consumable if the downstream verifier-controller chain can execute the packet without needing hidden planner-side state. Rather than treating packet validity as a monolithic pass/fail bit, this decomposition lets us ask *which preservation property broke first*.

We formalize this with a retention vector
\[
\mathbf{r}_t = [r_t^{\text{clause}},\; r_t^{\text{ctx}},\; r_t^{\text{refresh}},\; r_t^{\text{consume}}],
\]
and define the packet-retention score as
\[
K_t^{\text{retain}} = \omega_1 r_t^{\text{clause}} + \omega_2 r_t^{\text{ctx}} + \omega_3 r_t^{\text{refresh}} + \omega_4 r_t^{\text{consume}}.
\]
This score is not used to replace semantic or geometric validity. Instead, it acts as a packet-accountability term that tells us whether the planner's apparent reasoning survives serialization, live refresh, and downstream handoff. In practical aerial deployment, this is the more relevant notion of memory use: not whether context was briefly consulted upstream, but whether it was preserved long enough to shape verifiable execution.

### 3.8 Replanning-Value-Aware Packet Selection and Recovery Corridor Preservation

Packet validity alone is not sufficient for long-horizon aerial navigation. A semantic waypoint can be clause-preserving, verifier-compatible, and even locally executable, yet still be a poor continuation if it collapses the system's future ability to recover, re-anchor, or replan under partial observability. Recent open-world aerial evidence such as OpenVLN [REF: 2511.06182] suggests that value-guided replanning should be treated as a first-class signal rather than as a post-hoc patch, because locally reasonable steps may differ sharply in how much downstream correction room they preserve. We therefore extend packet scoring from immediate validity to **replanning-value-aware packet selection**.

Let \(\mathcal{C}(\mathcal{W}_t)\) denote the local correction corridor induced by packet \(\mathcal{W}_t\): the set of nearby refreshable future packets that remain stage-consistent, dynamically feasible, and semantically recoverable after executing the guarded prefix. Instead of only asking whether the current packet passes the verifier, we ask whether it preserves a non-collapsing corridor for future correction. We define a recovery-corridor value
\[
V_t^{\text{corr}} = \zeta_1 U_t^{\text{future}} + \zeta_2 S_t^{\text{recover}} + \zeta_3 C_t^{\text{handoff}} - \zeta_4 L_t^{\text{collapse}},
\]
where \(U_t^{\text{future}}\) measures expected future semantic utility, \(S_t^{\text{recover}}\) measures the size and diversity of feasible refresh continuations inside \(\mathcal{C}(\mathcal{W}_t)\), \(C_t^{\text{handoff}}\) measures whether those continuations remain compatible with downstream controller or NtM handoff, and \(L_t^{\text{collapse}}\) penalizes packets whose guarded execution sharply narrows future correction options.

We then augment packet selection with a corridor-preservation objective
\[
\mathcal{W}_t^* = \arg\max_{\mathcal{W}_t \in \Omega_t} \big(Q_t^{\text{pkt}} + \lambda V_t^{\text{corr}}\big),
\]
where \(Q_t^{\text{pkt}}\) is the immediate packet-quality score from Section 3.3 and \(\Omega_t\) is the candidate packet set extracted from the current 3D frontier map. This formulation deliberately avoids collapsing local correctness and long-horizon recoverability into one semantic relevance term. A packet that appears slightly weaker in immediate relevance may still be preferable if it preserves a much larger recovery corridor for subsequent refresh or bounded replan.

This matters especially at aerial stage boundaries. During `search`, the system can tolerate broader semantic exploration as long as the packet preserves multiple safe continuations. During `approach`, `inspect`, or `manipulate-ready`, however, packets that create narrow geometry funnels or stale heading commitments can make later packet repair disproportionately expensive. Corridor-aware selection therefore acts as a bridge between navigation-time packet quality and manipulation-time handoff reliability: it prefers proposals that keep the future execution shell flexible rather than overcommitting to brittle local greediness.

In this sense, our packet-first framing is not only about whether a waypoint is currently valid, but also about whether it remains a *good carrier of future correction*. The deployment benefit is that D06 can evaluate planner outputs using a criterion stronger than local flyability and weaker than full global planning optimality: does this packet keep the mission recoverable if the next refresh, verifier check, or NtM handoff changes the local situation?

### 3.10 Controller-Consumption Windowing and Honest Claim Routing

To keep D06 deployment claims honest, we distinguish the moment a packet is *emitted*, the moment it is *verified*, and the moment it is actually *consumed* by the downstream controller or NtM handoff module. Let a packet be associated with three windows: a planner-time window \(W_0\), a verifier/handoff window \(W_1\), and a controller-consumption window \(W_2\). We define the packet accountability tuple
\[
\Omega_t = (w_t^{\text{first+}},\; w_t^{\text{last-bind}},\; w_t^{\text{last-consume}},\; r_t^{\text{fam}},\; \tau_t^{\text{claim}}),
\]
where \(w_t^{\text{first+}}\) is the earliest positive window, \(w_t^{\text{last-bind}}\) is the last window in which the packet remains verifier-compatible and handoff-bindable, \(w_t^{\text{last-consume}}\) is the last window in which the packet remains clause-preserving and controller-consumable, \(r_t^{\text{fam}}\) is the matched baseline-family route from Section 2.6, and \(\tau_t^{\text{claim}}\) is the weakest honest claim allowed for the packet.

This separation is necessary because many aerial VLN improvements stall before true downstream consumption. A packet may become positive in \(W_0\) because the planner better preserves directional language or frontier semantics; it may survive to \(W_1\) because the verifier and guarded execution shell still accept it; yet it may fail in \(W_2\) once the controller consumes it under refreshed observations, heading drift, or manipulation-ready shell constraints. In our framework, such a packet is not allowed to claim full NtM handoff value.

We therefore use a conservative promotion rule. If a packet becomes positive only in \(W_0\), it supports at most **planner-side semantic gain**. If it remains honest through \(W_1\) but not through controller consumption, it supports at most **runtime continuity or verifier-compatible handoff gain**. Only packets that remain honest through \(W_2\)—while preserving clause retention, context retention, refresh consistency, and manipulation-ready handoff fields—are allowed to support **full packet-preserving NtM handoff gain**. This rule prevents D06 from overstating planner-side or verifier-side improvements as full deployment success.

The tuple \(\Omega_t\) also defines how matched-family comparisons are read. Clause-aware frontend baselines may legitimately improve \(W_0\); runtime-survivability baselines may legitimately improve \(W_1\); geometry-alignment baselines may stabilize cross-view grounding and thereby delay failure into later windows; and recoverability-aware baselines may preserve a larger correction corridor after partial degradation. But unless a method keeps the packet honest through \(W_2\), its strongest claim must remain family-local rather than being promoted to an end-to-end NtM headline.

### 3.11 Packet Freshness Regimes and Delayed-Consumption Non-Promotion

Controller-side honesty is further constrained by **freshness**, not only by whether a packet once passed semantic and geometric checks. Inspired by the contrast between OnFly-style verifier-supported runtime continuity and AerialVLA-style latent end-to-end sufficiency, we distinguish three freshness regimes for every accepted packet at the moment of downstream consumption:
\[
\phi_t \in \{\texttt{fresh-executable},\; \texttt{stale-but-bounded},\; \texttt{non-promotable}\}.
\]
A packet is **fresh-executable** if its semantic anchor, directional prior, stage tag, and guarded-prefix assumptions remain jointly valid within the current observation horizon. It is **stale-but-bounded** if it is still safe to execute under bounded drift but no longer supports a full semantic-interface claim. It is **non-promotable** if the packet may still produce locally safe continuation but its delayed-consumption semantics are no longer honest enough to support any packet-level superiority claim over stronger baselines.

We operationalize this regime assignment using a freshness state
\[
F_t^{\text{fresh}} = \big(a_t^{\text{age}},\; \delta_t^{\text{lat}},\; \epsilon_t^{\text{obs}},\; \iota_t^{\text{id}},\; \sigma_t^{\text{stage}}\big),
\]
where \(a_t^{\text{age}}\) is packet intent age, \(\delta_t^{\text{lat}}\) is end-to-end latency offset between planner emission and controller consumption, \(\epsilon_t^{\text{obs}}\) measures observation drift accumulated after verifier approval, \(\iota_t^{\text{id}}\) measures target/anchor identity preservation, and \(\sigma_t^{\text{stage}}\) measures whether the packet is still consumed within the same execution-stage semantics it was issued for. A packet may therefore remain geometrically flyable while already losing identity or stage honesty; such a case must not be silently grouped with truly fresh packets.

This leads to a delayed-consumption non-promotion rule. If a gain appears only under \texttt{stale-but-bounded} packets, it may support at most **bounded continuity gain** or **safety-preserving continuation gain**, but it must not be upgraded to semantic-interface superiority. If a result is dominated by \texttt{non-promotable} packets, then even a higher aggregate success rate cannot justify a stronger packet-centric claim, because the system is effectively winning via low-level continuation, local safety fallback, or latent execution resilience rather than by preserving an honest semantic handoff. In other words, *still flying safely* is weaker evidence than *still consuming the same packet honestly*.

This rule is especially important against strong end-to-end baselines. AerialVLA may continue producing useful actions under latent execution even when no explicit packet survives inspection, while OnFly may preserve a verifiable controller-bind path without guaranteeing that the same packet remains semantically current at final consumption. Our framework therefore routes gains by freshness regime before assigning credit: packet-centric D06 is only stronger if it preserves not just execution continuity, but **freshness-accountable delayed-consumption honesty** at the actual controller/NtM boundary.

### 3.12 Route-Specific Claim Routing for D06

Because multiple mechanisms can improve aerial VLN without improving the same semantic layer, D06 uses an explicit reviewer-facing claim-routing record
\[
\Gamma_t = (w_t^{+},\; w_t^{\dagger},\; r_t^{*},\; q_t^{*}),
\]
where \(w_t^{+}\) is the earliest positive evidence window, \(w_t^{\dagger}\) is the last freshness-honest controller-consumption window, \(r_t^{*}\) is the dominant evidence route, and \(q_t^{*}\) is the weakest honest claim allowed by that route. We instantiate four routes for aerial deployment: **semantic decomposition**, **latency compensation**, **low-level continuation**, and **safety fallback**.

The first route, **semantic decomposition**, covers gains best explained by better clause parsing, direction-aware grounding, memory-backed contextual anchoring, or clearer stage-conditioned packet serialization. This is where planner-side improvements from LookasideVLN-style directional priors or FineCog-Nav-style cognitive decomposition should be booked. However, these gains may only be promoted to packet-interface superiority if they remain visible at the actual controller-consumption boundary rather than collapsing after verifier bind.

The second route, **latency compensation**, covers gains mainly caused by better frequency decoupling, packet refresh, guarded execution, or verifier-aware online adjustment. This is the natural route for OnFly-style dual-agent orchestration and LiveVLN-style stop-and-go mitigation. Such gains are important for aerial deployment, but their strongest honest claim is improved **runtime continuity** or **stale-execution risk reduction**, not stronger semantic packet structure.

The third route, **low-level continuation**, covers cases where local controller stabilization, short-horizon geometric continuation, or shell-level tracking carries the system after packet freshness has already degraded. The fourth route, **safety fallback**, covers gains dominated by conservative execution envelopes, reject-and-recover ladders, or last-minute safe substitute routing. Both routes can still improve aggregate task success, but neither may be promoted to packet-level semantic superiority because the dominant evidence no longer resides in the preserved interface object.

This routing discipline is especially important when comparing against AerialVLA. The local reading notes show that AerialVLA is a strong minimalist end-to-end baseline because dual-view perception and fuzzy directional prompting can produce robust continuous navigation without explicit packet serialization, especially on unseen TravelUAV scenes. But that strength mainly certifies **latent execution sufficiency**, not **inspectable consume-time honesty**. Under our routing rule, D06 only earns a stronger claim than AerialVLA when a gain remains freshness-honest through the `approach → manipulate-ready` boundary and is still best explained by semantic packet structure rather than by controller-local continuation, latency compensation, or safety fallback.

A practical reviewer-facing reading order is therefore: first locate the earliest positive window \(w_t^{+}\), then locate the last freshness-honest window \(w_t^{\dagger}\), then assign the dominant route \(r_t^{*}\), and only then allow the weakest admissible claim tag \(q_t^{*}\). This four-step protocol prevents D06 from merging planner-side clause gains, verifier-side continuity gains, and controller-side rescue gains into a single inflated packet-first story. For our paper, this honesty matters more than a small top-line success gain because the real contribution is not merely to navigate better, but to explain **why the navigation result remains trustworthy when the downstream aerial controller actually consumes it**.

### 4. Experiments

Live packet refresh is useful only when it preserves the semantic thread that justified the current packet in the first place. A refreshed packet may improve local geometric fit while silently dropping the active clause, directional prior, landmark anchor, or stage rationale that made the original proposal controller-safe. We therefore introduce a **retention-guided refresh gate** that decides whether a packet should be refreshed in place, partially frozen, or escalated to replanning based on how much deployable semantic structure survives the update.

Let the current packet be \(\mathcal{W}_t\) and a refreshed candidate be \(\widetilde{\mathcal{W}}_t\). We define a retention score
\[
G_t^{\text{retain}} = \nu_1 A_t^{\text{clause}} + \nu_2 A_t^{\text{ctx}} + \nu_3 A_t^{\text{refresh}} + \nu_4 A_t^{\text{consume}},
\]
where \(A_t^{\text{clause}}\) measures whether the active instruction clause and directional relation are preserved, \(A_t^{\text{ctx}}\) measures whether memory-backed anchors and confidence provenance survive, \(A_t^{\text{refresh}}\) measures whether the guarded-prefix / revisable-tail split remains stable after the update, and \(A_t^{\text{consume}}\) measures whether the downstream verifier-controller chain can still consume the refreshed packet without hidden planner-only state.

The gate then applies a three-way decision rule:
\[
\pi_t^{\text{gate}} =
\begin{cases}
\texttt{refresh-in-place}, & G_t^{\text{retain}} \ge \tau_{\text{keep}} \;\text{and}\; F_t^{\text{exec}}(\widetilde{\mathcal{W}}_t) \ge \tau_{\text{exec}} \\
\texttt{freeze-prefix+repair-tail}, & \tau_{\text{repair}} \le G_t^{\text{retain}} < \tau_{\text{keep}} \\
\texttt{replan/escalate}, & G_t^{\text{retain}} < \tau_{\text{repair}} \;\text{or}\; F_t^{\text{exec}}(\widetilde{\mathcal{W}}_t) < \tau_{\text{exec}}
\end{cases}
\]
That is, highly retentive updates are accepted directly; moderately retentive updates are only allowed to revise the tail while freezing the already-safe execution prefix; and low-retention updates are rejected in favor of packet repair, shared-memory replanning, or escalation. This makes refresh an accountable deployment action rather than an always-good reaction to new observations.

The key reason for this gate is that refresh errors in aerial VLN are often *semantic erosion errors* rather than pure geometric mistakes. For example, the latest observation may improve obstacle clearance but remove the directional relation *behind the tower*; it may sharpen the target pose estimate but discard the memory-backed anchor that told the controller why this waypoint was safe; or it may keep all semantic fields intact but destroy the guarded-prefix continuity needed by the low-level shell. By scoring retention explicitly, we force the runtime to distinguish between beneficial refresh and semantically destructive refresh.

This gating policy also clarifies the relationship between packet refresh and recoverability. A packet update that preserves clause/context support while slightly worsening local utility may still be preferable if it keeps the future repair corridor wide. Conversely, an update that improves immediate geometric fit but collapses context retention should be treated as a brittle shortcut rather than a deployability gain. In this way, retention-guided refresh gating links the semantic preservation view of Sections 3.6-3.7 with the recoverability view of Section 3.8.

### 3.10 Navigation-to-Manipulation Handoff Diagnostics

A central claim of D06 is that aerial VLN should be judged not only by whether it reaches a target region, but also by whether the resulting packet survives the transition into manipulation-time local interaction. Existing navigation papers typically report task success up to goal arrival, while aerial manipulation papers often assume target proximity or manually curated pre-contact states. This leaves the **navigation-to-manipulation handoff** weakly measured: a system may be a strong navigator yet still produce packets that are unusable for downstream grasping, inspection, or placement.

We therefore introduce a handoff-oriented diagnostic layer that treats the `manipulate-ready` boundary as a first-class evaluation surface. Let a packet be considered handoff-valid only if it simultaneously satisfies semantic target consistency, pose covariance bounds, yaw/altitude compatibility, and local safety-shell constraints. We summarize this through a handoff score
\[
H_t = \gamma_1 T_t^{\text{target}} + \gamma_2 P_t^{\text{pose}} + \gamma_3 Y_t^{\text{yaw-alt}} + \gamma_4 S_t^{\text{shell}} - \gamma_5 J_t^{\text{jitter}},
\]
where \(T_t^{\text{target}}\) measures whether the active target hypothesis is semantically stable, \(P_t^{\text{pose}}\) measures whether the estimated target pose uncertainty falls below a manipulation-ready threshold, \(Y_t^{\text{yaw-alt}}\) measures compatibility of heading and altitude with the downstream local controller, \(S_t^{\text{shell}}\) measures whether the UAV has entered a safe operational shell around the object, and \(J_t^{\text{jitter}}\) penalizes short-horizon packet oscillation before handoff.

This leads to a more honest decomposition of late-stage failure. A navigation episode may fail because the packet never becomes target-stable, because local geometry remains too uncertain, because refresh repeatedly perturbs heading or altitude near the handoff boundary, or because the packet reaches the object but remains controller-incompatible for manipulation. Our framework therefore distinguishes *arrival success* from *handoff success*, and treats the latter as the relevant signal for Navigate-then-Manipulate systems.

Practically, this diagnostic layer lets us test whether packet-centric gains actually survive the last segment of deployment. A planner may improve direction adherence and verifier pass rate during long-horizon search, yet still lose value if packet refresh destroys handoff stability near the object. Conversely, a conservative refresh gate may slightly reduce exploration aggressiveness while substantially improving downstream manipulation readiness. This is precisely the kind of trade-off that standard success-rate reporting hides and that a deployment-oriented aerial VLN paper should make explicit.

A further consequence is that **late-stage packets need their own retention semantics rather than reusing search-time validity as a proxy**. We therefore split handoff-facing packet outcomes into three nested acceptance levels: `navigation-correct`, `interaction-preparable`, and `handoff-stable manipulation-ready`. A packet can satisfy the first level if it still preserves the active clause and target identity while remaining flight-feasible; it reaches the second level only when pose precision, contextual anchor support, and shell geometry become sufficient for downstream local interaction; and it reaches the third level only when those conditions remain stable across refresh and controller consumption without stage downgrade. This decomposition is aligned with our B0/B1/B2 readout in Section 4 and makes the handoff boundary measurable as a gradual deployment transition instead of a binary success flag.

Formally, we define a late-stage retention state
\[
\Lambda_t = \{\ell_t^{\text{nav}},\; \ell_t^{\text{prep}},\; \ell_t^{\text{handoff}}\},
\]
where \(\ell_t^{\text{nav}}\) indicates that the packet remains navigation-correct, \(\ell_t^{\text{prep}}\) indicates that the packet is interaction-preparable, and \(\ell_t^{\text{handoff}}\) indicates that it remains handoff-stable under refresh and controller-side consumption. This lets us interpret the NtM boundary through *retention transitions* rather than only through terminal outcomes: the key question is not just whether the system ever touched the target region, but whether it could promote packets from `nav-correct` to `prep` to `handoff-stable` without collapsing their semantic and contextual support.

This diagnostic view also clarifies why some planner-side gains should remain frozen as partial progress. A direction-aware frontend may increase \(\ell_t^{\text{nav}}\) by improving clause adherence, and a runtime-refresh system may improve \(\ell_t^{\text{prep}}\) by stabilizing approach-time packet continuity; however, neither should be credited as full NtM value unless \(\ell_t^{\text{handoff}}\) also improves. D06 therefore treats the handoff boundary as the first place where packet refresh, context retention, target stability, and controller consumability are forced to agree simultaneously. This is the exact boundary at which a packet stops being merely a navigation proposal and becomes a deployable downstream interaction interface.

### 3.11 Handoff-Value-Preserving Packet Freeze Rule

Once a packet has entered the late `approach` regime, not every semantic or runtime improvement should continue to count as a meaningful gain. We therefore introduce a **handoff-value-preserving freeze rule** whose role is to decide when a packet update is allowed to remain on the main contribution path and when it must be frozen as a partial gain. The intuition is simple: late-stage packet quality should be judged by whether the packet still preserves downstream interaction value, not merely by whether it looks fresher, more reactive, or locally more executable.

We say that a refreshed packet preserves handoff value only if three conditions remain jointly positive: (i) **target identity stability**—the target hypothesis has not drifted across refresh; (ii) **controller-consumable packet structure**—the verifier-controller chain can still execute the packet without recovering hidden planner-side state; and (iii) **manipulation-ready shell validity**—the packet still satisfies the pose, yaw, altitude, and safety-shell conditions required for local interaction handoff. If any of these three conditions fails, the refresh may still be useful, but it can no longer be claimed as a full NtM improvement.

To make this explicit, we define a freeze indicator
\[
\Phi_t = \mathbb{I}\big[T_t^{\text{id}} \wedge C_t^{\text{consume}} \wedge M_t^{\text{shell}}\big],
\]
where \(T_t^{\text{id}}\) denotes target identity stability, \(C_t^{\text{consume}}\) denotes controller-consumable packet structure, and \(M_t^{\text{shell}}\) denotes manipulation-ready shell validity. If \(\Phi_t = 1\), the packet remains eligible for the full handoff-value narrative; if \(\Phi_t = 0\), the packet must be frozen into a weaker interpretation bucket even if other scalar metrics improved.

This gives D06 a disciplined interpretation protocol with three mutually exclusive buckets. A packet update is labeled **navigation-positive but handoff-neutral** if it improves search-time or approach-time packet correctness while leaving handoff value unchanged. It is labeled **refresh-positive but handoff-negative** if it improves refresh-time semantic freshness, continuity, or verifier survival while degrading target stability, handoff readiness, or downstream consumability. Only when \(\Phi_t = 1\) and late-stage transfer metrics improve do we label it **handoff-value-preserving**. In this way, the main paper claim is protected from the common failure mode where an aggressive refresh policy inflates online responsiveness while silently eroding interaction-time usability.

The freeze rule also serves as a writing constraint for the paper itself. If late-stage improvements mainly show up in clause preservation or online continuity but not in shell-valid handoff retention, the honest top-level story should remain at planner-side packet quality or runtime survivability rather than escalating to a full Navigate-then-Manipulate claim. This prevents D06 from overstating late-stage gains and keeps the submission centered on what the evidence actually supports.

### 3.12 Training Objective and Verifier-Supervised Packet Learning

We train the planner to emit Semantic Waypoint Packets using a multi-term objective that couples semantic relevance, direction adherence, deployability, and progress continuity:

\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{pkt}} + \gamma_1 \mathcal{L}_{\text{dir}} + \gamma_2 \mathcal{L}_{\text{ver}} + \gamma_3 \mathcal{L}_{\text{trace}} + \gamma_4 \mathcal{L}_{\text{ctx}} + \gamma_5 \mathcal{L}_{\text{stage}}.
\]

Here, \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{pkt}}\) supervises the target waypoint and altitude-band regression, \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{dir}}\) aligns heading hints with direction-conditioned language clauses, \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ver}}\) distills verifier feedback into the planner so that semantically plausible but systematically rejected packets are down-weighted during training, \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{trace}}\) encourages consistency with the remaining ordered progress trace, \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{ctx}}\) penalizes dropping retrieved context that should have been serialized into packet fields, and \(\mathcal{L}_{\text{stage}}\) supervises stage-tag correctness and handoff readiness.

The verifier supervision term is especially important for deployment-oriented aerial VLN. Instead of treating the verifier as a pure test-time safety filter, we let its pass / reject outcomes provide weak labels for packet executability under geometry, dynamics, budget, and stage constraints. This makes the planner explicitly learn the boundary between *interesting* packets and *worth executing* packets. In practice, this should reduce semantically attractive but stale, over-budget, or handoff-misaligned proposals.

We additionally introduce a lightweight **replanning-value distillation** term motivated by OpenVLN-style verifiable reward and replanning signals. Concretely, after a packet has passed immediate executability checks, we estimate whether executing it preserves future recoverability under partial observability—e.g., whether the resulting pose still keeps multiple target hypotheses separable, whether the view remains correction-friendly, and whether a failed local attempt can be repaired without reopening full search. This value signal is not used to replace verifier supervision; it acts as a second-stage regularizer that discourages packets whose local feasibility is high but whose downstream replanning corridor is poor. In this way, the planner is trained not only to emit executable packets, but also to prefer packets that remain *recoverable*.

We also use a staged curriculum tied to the packet stage tag. Early training emphasizes `search` packets with broad altitude bands and relaxed progress supervision, while later training increases the weight of `approach` and `manipulate-ready` packets where stage drift, context omission, and late refresh failures are more costly. This matches our paper framing that aerial VLN should be judged not only by open-world search ability, but by whether packet quality survives the tighter constraints of downstream handoff and local interaction.

### 4.2.21 Consume-Time Accountability Against Strong-but-Non-Inspectable Baselines

A critical question for D06 is not only whether packetized planning improves top-line navigation success, but whether it preserves **consume-time accountability** better than strong end-to-end latent interfaces such as AerialVLA. Our primary reading rule is therefore windowed and asymmetric. If a gain persists through verifier bind but collapses under delayed consumption, it is routed to a **runtime continuity gain** rather than a navigation-to-manipulation handoff gain. Only improvements that remain positive through the consume-time contract terms `A_dir`, `A_ctx`, `A_shell`, and `A_guard` are eligible to support the stronger claim that explicit packetization preserves downstream deployability better than a latent end-to-end interface.

This comparison also clarifies what should count as fair evidence against AerialVLA. Such gains should not be dismissed; instead, they define the burden of proof for D06. Our packet-centric method must therefore show either (i) higher `w_last_consume` under delayed refresh, (ii) lower consume-time contract collapse near the `approach -> manipulate-ready` boundary, or (iii) better family-matched routing honesty where aggregate success is similar but the surviving evidence route is stronger and more inspectable.

The practical implication is that D06 should report AerialVLA comparisons in two layers. The first layer is standard task performance (`SR`, `SPL`, instruction adherence, onboard budget compliance). The second layer is consume-time accountability: `controller-consumption honesty`, `handoff-shell validity`, `context retention under delayed refresh`, and `claim-route stability`. If packetization wins only in the first layer, we should describe it as an engineering alternative. If it also wins in the second layer, then D06 is justified in claiming that explicit packet contracts provide a deployment property that latent end-to-end control does not directly certify.

### 4.2.22 Minimal D06 Result Matrix for Honest Claim Promotion

To prevent over-claiming in the first complete D06 result table, we define a minimal promotion matrix that maps evidence windows to the strongest admissible paper claim. Let the row key be `(w_first+, w_last_consume, r_fam, phi)` where `w_first+` is the earliest positive window, `w_last_consume` is the last consume-honest window, `r_fam` is the dominant baseline family route, and `phi` is the freshness regime at controller consumption. We then restrict claim promotion as follows.

If the earliest gain appears in `W0` and the packet does not survive to consume-honest `W2`, the result may support at most **planner-side semantic gain**. If the gain survives to verifier bind `W1` but the freshness regime is only `stale-but-bounded`, the result may support at most **runtime continuity / bounded delayed-execution gain**. If the gain survives to consume-honest `W2` under `fresh-executable` and the dominant route remains semantic decomposition rather than low-level continuation or safety fallback, then and only then may the result support **full packet-preserving NtM handoff gain**.

This matrix is especially important for D06 because multiple local baselines improve different surfaces of the deployment stack. LookasideVLN and FineCog-Nav may improve `W0` by preserving clause and directional structure. OnFly and LiveVLN may improve `W1` by reducing stale execution and improving bind-time continuity. SpatialFly may delay cross-view grounding collapse. OpenVLN may preserve the recovery corridor after partial degradation. None of these gains should be automatically merged into one packet-first headline. The first D06 table should instead report which surface improved first, which surface survived last, and what that evidence honestly allows us to claim.

In reviewer-facing prose, this means every main result paragraph should follow a fixed four-step order: **(1) where the positive signal first appeared; (2) whether it survived to consume-time honesty; (3) which baseline family best explains the competing alternative; (4) what is the weakest honest claim tag that remains.** This template keeps the D06 narrative sharp: the paper is not rewarded for sounding maximal, but for showing exactly when a semantic waypoint packet is still trustworthy enough to be consumed by a real aerial controller.

#### 4.2.1 Retention-Binned Readout for the NtM Boundary

We report late-stage packet outcomes in three retention bins. **B0** denotes packets that remain navigation-correct but are not interaction-ready; **B1** denotes packets that become interaction-preparable but remain unstable under handoff; and **B2** denotes packets that remain handoff-stable and manipulation-ready. This readout prevents search-time semantic gains from being over-read as deployable NtM success. In our interpretation rule, only stable migration toward B2 counts as evidence that the packet interface has preserved enough clause, context, refresh, and pose structure to support downstream local interaction.

Across all baselines, we therefore read late-stage gains through the tuple `(B0, B1, B2, T01, T12, BCR, HRD, MRP, CCH)`. A method is considered **navigation-improving but handoff-weak** if it lowers B0 occupancy or raises `T01` while leaving `T12` and `B2` nearly unchanged. It is considered **interaction-preparable but handoff-fragile** if it raises B1 occupancy but also increases `BCR` or fails to reduce `HRD`. Only when `B2` and `T12` rise together while `HRD` falls and `CCH` stays stable do we interpret the gain as a genuine Navigate-then-Manipulate improvement rather than a search-only or approach-only gain.

This bin-wise interpretation also lets us compare families of methods without collapsing distinct gains. Direction-aware planner frontends may mainly reduce B0 by preserving clause-level intent; runtime-refresh systems may mainly improve `T01` and `CCH`; geometry-aware representations may mainly reduce `HRD`; and corridor-preserving replanners may mainly stabilize `T12` under disturbance. Our method claims superiority only if these gains become jointly visible as a net shift toward B2 rather than as isolated improvements on one intermediate surface.

#### 4.2.2 Retention-First Reading Rule

All main-result claims are filtered through a retention-first reading rule. If a method improves CSR or CCR but not MRP, HRD, CCH, or B2 occupancy, we treat it as planner-side or packet-side partial improvement rather than a full deployment gain. If a method improves runtime continuity while increasing BCR or late-stage HRD, we classify it as refresh-positive but handoff-fragile. This ensures the paper does not collapse semantically distinct gains into one oversized packet-centric success claim.

We further apply a **refresh accountability rule**. If refresh raises CSR, CCR, or RFR but fails to increase `T12`, `B2`, or MRP, then refresh is counted only as an online continuity gain, not as NtM value preservation. Conversely, if refresh slightly lowers short-horizon semantic freshness yet reduces HRD and BCR while increasing B2 occupancy, we regard it as a deployment-positive conservative policy because it preserves downstream handoff integrity. This rule is important for D06 because aerial stacks often appear stronger simply by refreshing more aggressively, even when that aggressiveness erodes manipulation readiness.

#### 4.2.3 Baseline-Family Attribution Rules in Main Results

We read the main table through four baseline families so that gains are not over-attributed. **Clause-preserving planner frontends** (e.g., FineCog-Nav, LookasideVLN-like systems) are only allowed to explain improvements in B0 reduction, CPA/CPR, and other pre-verifier language-preservation metrics. **Runtime-survivability systems** (e.g., OnFly, LiveVLN-like systems) are only allowed to explain gains in VPR, PSR, RED, `T01`, and controller-side continuity metrics. **Geometry-alignment systems** are mainly read through HDE, HRD, and approach-stage shell stability. **Recoverability-oriented systems** are read through PRG, RBE, `T12`, and BCR.

This attribution discipline matters because D06 is explicitly trying to avoid the common failure mode of claiming a single packet-centric story for gains that actually come from different subsystems. If a baseline improves only pre-verifier clause preservation, we do not allow that gain to count as downstream NtM stability. If a baseline improves runtime continuity but does not increase B2 occupancy, we freeze the claim at "better online survivability" rather than "better deployable handoff." The paper therefore argues for a *jointly accountable* packet interface, not for flattening all intermediate gains into one headline number.

#### 4.2.4 Refresh Accountability at the NtM Boundary

A key late-stage question is whether refresh is still helpful once the packet has crossed from broad search into approach and manipulate-ready regimes. Our rule is intentionally asymmetric across stages. During `search`, refresh is rewarded whenever it improves semantic grounding or local executability without causing repeated verifier collapse. During `approach`, however, refresh is only rewarded if it also preserves `MRP`, suppresses `HRD`, and avoids regressions from B1/B2 back to weaker bins. At the `manipulate-ready` boundary, the burden is stricter still: a refresh is considered beneficial only when it preserves controller-consumability and does not force a stage downgrade.

This gives us a simple deployment reading. If a method refreshes more often and looks more responsive but also increases BCR or Recovery-to-Handoff Delay, we classify it as **late-stage over-refresh** rather than as a stronger planner. If another method refreshes less aggressively yet keeps B2 occupancy stable and reduces handoff drift, we classify it as a stronger NtM interface because it protects downstream interaction readiness. In other words, D06 does not reward freshness in isolation; it rewards freshness that remains accountable to manipulation-time usability.

#### 4.2.5 Refresh Is Only Useful When It Preserves Handoff Value

Late-stage packet refresh should not be rewarded merely because it makes the packet newer, more reactive, or locally more executable. Our reading rule is stricter: refresh is counted as beneficial only if the updated packet preserves **handoff value**, i.e., if it keeps the same target identity, remains consumable by the verifier-controller chain, and still satisfies the shell conditions required for manipulation-time takeover. In practice, this means that refresh must be evaluated jointly through `SOR`, `CCR`, `CCH`, `MRP`, and `HRD`, rather than through freshness or short-horizon semantic consistency alone.

This rule is directly motivated by the contrast between AerialVLA-style minimalist end-to-end navigation and OnFly-style verifier-aware onboard execution. AerialVLA [REF: 2603.14363] shows that lightweight frontier-free policies can remain competitive when semantic intent is preserved through a compact control interface, but it does not explicitly guarantee manipulation-time packet readiness. OnFly [REF: 2603.10682] shows that verification-aware live execution can greatly improve onboard survivability, yet verification-time validity alone still does not prove that the resulting packet remains valuable at the `approach → manipulate-ready` boundary. The implication for D06 is that refresh cannot be judged at planner time only; it must be judged at the moment the packet is actually consumed by the downstream interaction stack.

We therefore freeze late-stage refresh outcomes into three mutually exclusive interpretations. If refresh improves packet freshness or verifier acceptance while leaving `MRP`, `B2`, and `T12` unchanged, we record it as **refresh-positive but handoff-neutral**. If refresh improves `CSR`, `CCR`, or `RFR` but worsens `HRD`, increases `BCR`, or forces repeated stage downgrade, we record it as **refresh-positive but handoff-negative**. Only when refresh raises `T12` and `B2`, preserves `SOR`, and keeps `CCH` stable do we allow it to count as a true **handoff-value-preserving refresh**. This prevents D06 from overclaiming gains that merely keep the planner busy or the runtime responsive while silently eroding downstream NtM readiness.

#### 4.2.6 Handoff-Value-Preserving Freeze Rule

To make result interpretation submission-ready, we define a final freeze rule for all late-stage gains. Every positive result observed after the first stable `approach` packet must be assigned to exactly one of three bins: `navigation-positive but handoff-neutral`, `refresh-positive but handoff-negative`, or `handoff-value-preserving`. A gain is only allowed into the third bin if the updated packet simultaneously preserves target identity stability, controller-consumable packet structure, and manipulation-ready shell validity. In other words, the burden of proof is no longer whether the packet looks semantically improved, but whether it survives the exact boundary where navigation output becomes downstream interaction input.

This freeze rule is especially important because D06 intentionally targets a Navigate-then-Manipulate setting rather than pure waypoint arrival. A method that reaches the object region faster but repeatedly destabilizes `handoff_tag`, degrades pose-shell compatibility, or forces hidden controller-side state recovery is not yet a deployment-ready interface improvement. Under our reading discipline, such a method may still earn credit for search-time navigation or runtime continuity, but it cannot be promoted to the main NtM contribution. The paper therefore commits to a handoff-value-first conclusion protocol: packet refresh, retention, and runtime continuity matter only insofar as they preserve usable downstream handoff value.

### 4.2.10 Retention-Aware Freeze-Line for Late-Stage Claims

To make the late-stage story maximally falsifiable, we add one more interpretation boundary: every accepted late-stage packet must be judged first by **which retention slice broke last**, not by which scalar metric improved first. Concretely, a packet can only be credited as a deployable NtM gain if clause retention, context retention, and handoff retention remain simultaneously positive at the moment the packet is consumed by the downstream stack. If clause retention improves but context support is dropped during refresh, the gain is frozen as a planner-side semantic improvement only. If context and controller-consumability survive but the handoff shell becomes unstable, the gain is frozen as interaction-preparable rather than manipulation-ready.

This freeze-line is important because recent D06 baselines improve different slices of the packet lifecycle. LookasideVLN [REF: 2604.17190] primarily strengthens direction-aware clause preservation; FineCog-Nav [REF: 2604.16298] improves memory-backed reasoning and module-level instruction adherence; LiveVLN [REF: 2604.19536] improves runtime continuity under guarded refresh; and deployable hierarchical systems [REF: 2604.21363] improve context-aware exploration under hardware constraints. Our method should not collapse these into one oversized packet story. Instead, the main-result narrative is only allowed to escalate from `semantic gain` → `refresh-stable gain` → `handoff-value-preserving gain` when the corresponding retention slices survive in order.

Operationally, this means we interpret late-stage improvements through a nested rule set. First, any gain without stable `CPR + CtxPR` is frozen below deployable packet quality. Second, any gain without stable `CCR + CCH + MRP` is frozen below interaction-ready quality. Third, any gain that fails to preserve `SOR` or exhibits elevated `HRD` after refresh is frozen below full handoff value preservation even if earlier metrics look strong. This gives D06 a stricter submission-time discipline: a method is not allowed to claim NtM readiness merely because it reaches the object region, refreshes more often, or improves verifier acceptance; it must show that the retained packet remains semantically grounded, context-supported, controller-consumable, and shell-valid at the exact boundary where navigation hands control to manipulation.

### 4.2.8 Controller-Consumption-First Reading at the NtM Boundary

A packet should not be counted as late-stage positive merely because it remains planner-valid or verifier-passable at refresh time. Our stricter reading is **controller-consumption-first**: the packet is only allowed to support a strong NtM claim if its semantic and contextual structure still survives at the exact moment the downstream interaction stack consumes it. This pushes D06 beyond planner-time freshness and verifier-time legality toward consumption-time usability.

Concretely, we evaluate the late-stage boundary through a delayed-consumption tuple `(CCH, CCR, MRP, HRD, SOR, T12, B2)`. A refresh or repair is interpreted as a meaningful handoff gain only when it preserves controller consumability (`CCH`, `CCR`), keeps the target hypothesis stable (`SOR`), and still promotes packets into manipulation-ready stable states (`MRP`, `T12`, `B2`) without inflating handoff drift (`HRD`). If a method mainly improves planner-side or verifier-side readouts but those improvements do not survive controller-side consumption, the honest interpretation must remain below full NtM value.

This rule is directly supported by local D06 anchors. OnFly [REF: 2603.10682] demonstrates that semantic-geometric verification and frequency-decoupled onboard execution can dramatically improve runtime survivability, but it still leaves open whether a verifier-approved packet remains useful at the exact handoff boundary. HTNav [REF: 2604.08883] shows that hierarchical planning and local reactive control can stabilize macro-to-micro execution, but such stability should only be credited as deployable NtM value when the packet remains controller-consumable after late-stage refresh and stage tightening. The implication is that D06 should not score late-stage quality at the moment the planner emits a better packet, nor only when the verifier accepts it, but when the controller actually consumes it under the constraints of interaction-time execution.

We therefore impose a final interpretation constraint. If a method improves `CSR`, `CtxPR`, or `RFR` but the gain disappears by controller consumption time, it is frozen as **refresh-aware semantic retention** only. If it improves `CCR` or `CCH` without promoting `T12` or `B2`, it is frozen as **controller-side continuity gain** rather than a full handoff gain. Only when the packet remains semantically grounded, context-supported, controller-consumable, and manipulation-ready at the time of downstream consumption do we allow the result to escalate to a **controller-consumption-preserving NtM contribution**. This makes D06's late-stage story more honest and better aligned with the actual deployment bottleneck of aerial navigate-then-manipulate systems.

### 4.2.9 Consumption-Time Freeze Rule for Late Refresh Gains

Late refresh gains are often overstated because they are measured too early: at packet issuance time or verifier acceptance time rather than at controller consumption time. We therefore add a final **consumption-time freeze rule**. Every positive late-stage refresh result must be assigned according to the strongest property that still survives when the packet is consumed downstream.

If the gain survives only until verifier acceptance, it is frozen as `verifier-positive but consumption-unproven`. If it survives controller consumption but does not improve `MRP`, `T12`, or `B2`, it is frozen as `consumption-positive but handoff-neutral`. If it improves freshness, continuity, and controller consumption while degrading `SOR` or inflating `HRD`, it is frozen as `consumption-positive but handoff-negative`. Only when the packet survives controller consumption and still preserves stable promotion into manipulation-ready execution do we label it `consumption-time handoff-value-preserving`.

This rule is especially important for D06 because aerial systems operate under asynchronous sensing, onboard latency, and stage-tightening budgets. Under these conditions, the most dangerous overclaim is to equate a better refreshed packet with a better deployable packet. Our protocol explicitly forbids that shortcut: refresh, retention, and verification gains only count as strong paper-level evidence when they remain alive at the exact point where navigation output becomes interaction input.

#### 4.2.7 Controller-Consumption Timestamp as the Real Late-Stage Judge

A late-stage packet should not be judged only at planner release time or even at verifier acceptance time. In aerial deployment, the decisive moment is the **controller-consumption timestamp**: the instant when the downstream local interaction stack actually consumes the packet as the active execution interface. Between planner release and controller consumption, packet fields may remain semantically plausible while silently losing the exact target identity, shell geometry, or contextual support that made them manipulation-ready. We therefore define the controller-consumption event as the canonical judge of handoff value rather than treating release-time correctness as a sufficient proxy.

This distinction is crucial when reading systems such as OnFly [REF: 2603.10682], AerialVLA [REF: 2603.14363], HTNav [REF: 2604.08883], and AirNav [REF: 2601.03707]. OnFly-style runtime verification can certify a packet at one instant, HTNav-style hierarchical execution can preserve macro-level navigation intent, and AerialVLA-style minimalist control can remain responsive under onboard budgets; however, none of these alone guarantees that the packet still carries the same deployable value at the moment the controller must commit to late-stage motion or NtM handoff. For D06, this means that all late-stage gains must be re-read through the question: **did the packet still mean the same useful thing when it was finally consumed?**

We therefore augment the main-results protocol with a controller-consumption tuple `(SOR, CCH, MRP, HRD, B2 | t_consume)`. A late-stage packet is only counted as a full NtM success if target identity remains stable up to `t_consume`, controller-consumption harmony stays positive, manipulation-ready preservation is retained, and no hidden stage downgrade is needed at the moment of use. If release-time metrics improve but these consumption-time conditions fail, the gain must be frozen as either `navigation-positive but handoff-neutral` or `refresh-positive but handoff-negative` rather than promoted to a deployable interface claim.

#### 4.2.8 Consumption-Time Freeze Rule for Refresh-Positive Results

To make this operational, we add a consumption-time freeze rule for all refresh-positive outcomes near the NtM boundary. A packet update that improves `CSR`, `CCR`, or `RFR` before handoff is not automatically meaningful; it must also preserve the same interaction value at controller-consumption time. Concretely, if refresh yields better clause retention or runtime continuity but the packet arrives at `t_consume` with degraded `SOR`, weakened `MRP`, inflated `HRD`, or collapsed `B2` occupancy, then the improvement is frozen below the main contribution line. This prevents D06 from mistaking **planner-time freshness** for **interaction-time usability**.

This rule also clarifies how to read borderline baselines from the local note pool. FineCog-Nav [REF: 2604.16298] and LookasideVLN [REF: 2604.17190] may legitimately improve clause-level and direction-level packet quality before consumption; LiveVLN [REF: 2604.19536] may legitimately improve guarded execution continuity; OpenFly [REF: 2502.18041] and AirNav [REF: 2601.03707] may provide stronger deployment-aligned evaluation scaffolds. But unless those gains survive through the exact moment of controller-side consumption, D06 should not let them inflate the headline claim. The honest reading protocol is therefore: **refresh-positive is only paper-positive when consumption-time handoff value is preserved**.

#### 4.2.9 Delayed-Consumption Stress Readout

To make the controller-consumption boundary experimentally falsifiable, we additionally report late-stage metrics under delayed-consumption bins defined by \(\Delta t_{\text{consume}} = t_{\text{consume}} - t_{\text{release}}\). Rather than rewarding low latency alone, this readout asks whether a packet that looked good upstream still preserves the same deployable interaction value after a realistic downstream delay. We therefore bucket packets into immediate-, short-delay, and delayed-consumption groups and compare `T12`, `B2`, `MRP`, `CCH`, and `SOR` inside each bin.

This stress readout helps distinguish a merely responsive packet interface from a robust one. If a method only looks strong when consumption is nearly immediate, its gain should be interpreted as a timing-sensitive runtime advantage rather than a stable NtM interface. By contrast, if the same packet family preserves `B2`, `MRP`, and `CCH` even when the downstream interaction shell consumes it later, then the evidence is much closer to a true deployment-ready aerial navigate-then-manipulate interface. In other words, D06 should be judged not by whether packets are briefly valid, but by whether they remain valid long enough to matter when the controller actually needs them.

#### 4.2.10 Delayed-Consumption Route-Freezing Rule

We further summarize late-stage deployability under delayed use by stratifying results into three controller-consumption lag bins: **immediate** (`0–1` control intervals), **short-delay** (`1–3` intervals), and **delayed-consumption** (`>3` intervals). For each bin, we jointly report `T12`, `B2`, `MRP`, `CCH`, and `SOR`, and interpret them as the minimal evidence group for claimed NtM value. This readout exposes whether a method's apparent late-stage gain is truly robust to delayed downstream use or only looks strong when packets are consumed almost immediately.

A method is therefore considered **consumption-robust** only if its B2 occupancy and `T12` remain stable, while `MRP`, `CCH`, and `SOR` do not collapse as `\Delta t_{\text{consume}}` increases. If improvements disappear outside the immediate bin, we do not upgrade the method to a general handoff-improving system; instead, we classify it as **lag-sensitive late-stage support**. This is especially important for aerial systems, where packet generation, verification, local obstacle handling, and manipulation preparation often run at mismatched frequencies. Delayed-consumption stress testing makes those hidden timing mismatches visible in the main results rather than burying them in appendix runtime notes.

#### 4.2.11 Consumption-Window Accountability Split

Delayed consumption should not be treated as a single blob of runtime latency. We therefore further split the controller-consumption path into three accountability windows: **planner-release → verifier-accept**, **verifier-accept → controller-bind**, and **controller-bind → local-interaction-consume**. The first window captures whether semantic freshness survives packet emission and verification; the second captures whether a verifier-approved packet remains executable while waiting for low-level control commitment; and the third captures whether an already bound packet still preserves target identity, shell validity, and interaction value when the local NtM stack finally consumes it.

This split matters because recent local anchors stress different windows. FineCog-Nav and LookasideVLN primarily strengthen planner-side clause preservation, OnFly and LiveVLN primarily strengthen verifier-facing runtime continuity, while HTNav-like hierarchical shells mainly stabilize controller binding and short-horizon execution. D06 should not flatten these into one undifferentiated late-stage gain. Instead, every positive result must state **which window improved first** and whether that improvement survived into the last window. If a gain stays inside the first or second window only, it remains a planner/runtime support gain rather than a full handoff-value gain.

To make controller-consumption-first evaluation fully auditable, we also align the accountability windows with a responsibility interpretation: **W0 planner-release→verifier-accept** evaluates planner-side semantic structuring and verifier-time legality, **W1 verifier-accept→controller-bind** evaluates thread-consistent controller binding under runtime latency, and **W2 controller-bind→local-interaction-consume** evaluates whether the packet still preserves target identity, contextual support, and manipulation-ready shell validity when the local interaction stack actually uses it. This stricter readout prevents aggressive refresh policies from being overclaimed. A method may improve `CSR`, `CCR`, or even verifier-facing continuity in W0/W1 while still failing to preserve `MRP`, `T12`, or `B2` inside W2 because packet meaning decays after controller bind. Under our interpretation rule, such results remain **pre-consumption continuity gains** rather than full NtM improvements. Conversely, a conservative packet policy that slightly sacrifices planner-time freshness but preserves `SOR / CCH / MRP / T12 / B2` through W2 is treated as more valuable deployment behavior, because it protects the only window where packet semantics are actually converted into interaction-time action.

#### 4.2.14 Controller-Consumption Promotion Matrix

To keep late-stage claims honest, we convert the `(first positive window, last honest window)` tuple into an explicit **controller-consumption promotion matrix**. The purpose of this matrix is to prevent early packet-level gains from being over-promoted into full Navigate-to-Manipulate (NtM) value before they survive actual downstream controller use.

Let `W0` denote planner-side semantic release, `W1` denote verifier acceptance and controller-bind viability, and `W2` denote controller-side local-interaction consumption. We then define the strongest allowed claim for a result as

\[
\Pi(\omega_{\text{first}}, \omega_{\text{last}}) \in \{G_{\text{sem}},\; G_{\text{cont}},\; G_{\text{bind}},\; G_{\text{NtM}}\},
\]

where \(G_{\text{sem}}\) is planner-side semantic gain, \(G_{\text{cont}}\) is runtime continuity gain, \(G_{\text{bind}}\) is controller-bind gain, and \(G_{\text{NtM}}\) is full handoff-value gain. The promotion rule is:

- `W0 → W0`: at most **planner-side semantic gain**. These results may improve clause adherence, waypoint relevance, or pre-verifier packet quality, but they remain silent about runtime survivability or downstream use.
- `W0 → W1`: at most **runtime continuity gain**. These results show that planner-side benefits survive verifier passage or guarded execution, but they still do not prove that the packet remains valuable when actually consumed by the local interaction stack.
- `W1 → W1`: at most **controller-bind gain**. These results indicate that the packet remains executable and controller-compatible at the binding stage, yet they still stop short of proving manipulation-ready downstream value.
- `W0/W1 → W2`: eligible for **full NtM handoff-value gain**, but only if the consumed packet simultaneously preserves `SOR`, `CCH`, `MRP`, `T12`, `B2`, and `CTTPR`.

This matrix matters because many apparent late-stage gains are really promotion errors. A direction-aware planner frontend may produce a strong `W0 → W0` improvement in clause preservation, and a live-refresh runtime may upgrade that to `W0 → W1` by reducing packet staleness, but neither should be described as a manipulation-ready interface improvement unless the packet survives `W2` controller consumption without identity drift or handoff collapse. Likewise, some refresh policies look strong because they create more `W1 → W1` controller-bind wins while still degrading `T12`, `B2`, or `CTTPR`; under our matrix these are frozen as controller-bind gains rather than overclaimed as full NtM improvements.

Operationally, every main-result paragraph and table caption in D06 must first identify the earliest window where the gain appears, then the latest window where the interpretation remains honest, and only then assign a promotion label from the matrix above. This turns `W0/W1/W2` from a descriptive bookkeeping device into a writing-time constraint on claim strength.

### 4.4 Ablation Study


To expose whether online updates preserve deployable semantics rather than merely improving local geometric fit, we introduce four retention-facing diagnostics. **Clause Survival Rate (CSR)** measures the fraction of refresh events after which the accepted packet still preserves the active clause and directional relation. **Context Carryover Rate (CCR)** measures whether landmark anchors, stage hints, and confidence provenance survive packet repair or refresh rather than being silently dropped. **Refresh Consistency Score (RCS-retain)** measures whether the guarded prefix and refreshed tail remain semantically and geometrically compatible across live updates. **Controller Consumption Hazard (CCH)** measures the fraction of verifier-passed packets that still trigger downstream controller rejection, unstable oscillation, or emergency fallback.

These metrics complement earlier packet-facing indicators such as verifier pass rate, packet staleness, heading drift, and packet handoff failure. Together they separate four failure surfaces that standard success-rate reporting cannot distinguish: semantic clause loss, contextual anchor loss, refresh inconsistency, and downstream controller-side consumption failure. This is especially important near the navigation-to-manipulation boundary, where a packet may remain semantically plausible while already becoming too stale or too under-specified for stable local interaction.

### 4.2.2 Navigation-to-Manipulation Handoff Diagnostics

We further define a dedicated NtM handoff panel. **Manipulation-Ready Precision (MRP)** measures the fraction of packets marked `manipulate-ready` that truly satisfy downstream interaction preconditions. **Handoff Recovery Delay (HRD)** measures the time required to recover from a failed manipulation-ready handoff back to a stable navigation or local repair state. **Stage Oscillation Rate (SOR)** measures the fraction of episodes that repeatedly bounce between `approach` and `manipulate-ready` without stable commitment. These diagnostics let us test whether packetization and retention-guided refresh genuinely improve late-stage deployability rather than merely producing overconfident near-target promotions.

A closely related quantity is **Ready-Promotion Loss (RPL)**, the fraction of packets that are verifier-accepted and initially promoted toward interaction but later lose manipulation readiness because refresh, local geometry, or controller-side consumption invalidates the handoff shell. We pair RPL with **Clause-Aware Packet Gain (CAPG)** so that D06 can distinguish packets that are more instruction-faithful now from packets that remain usable later. If CAPG rises but MRP remains weak and RPL remains high, the right interpretation is that packet serialization improved clause retention without yet solving downstream handoff fragility.

### 4.2.3 Attribution-Consistent Reading of Main Results

We read the main results under a strict attribution rule. If improvement primarily appears in clause adherence, we attribute it to planner-side language structuring rather than packet refresh. If improvement appears in runtime continuity, verifier survival, and handoff stability, we attribute it to packetization and retention-guided refresh gating rather than to better semantic grounding alone. If improvement only becomes visible after verifier acceptance, we treat it as controller-consumption gain rather than planner gain. This rule keeps D06 from collapsing language adherence, refresh survivability, and downstream execution stability into one oversized "better aerial VLN" narrative.

Operationally, each method family is only allowed to explain its corresponding gain surface: direction-aware or clause-aware planners explain gains in CSR, CAPG, CPA, and pre-verifier alignment; runtime-survivability systems explain gains in VPR, packet staleness, guarded continuity, and refresh consistency; geometry-aware representations explain gains in cross-view grounding and path smoothness; and recoverability-oriented systems explain gains in recovery-corridor preservation and post-rejection correction quality. Only when multiple surfaces improve through the same packet interface do we upgrade the conclusion to a full packet-centric deployment claim.

### 4.2.4 Baseline-Family Comparison Targets

Our expected comparison therefore follows four baseline families: (i) clause- and direction-preserving planner frontends (FineCog-Nav, LookasideVLN), (ii) runtime-survivability systems (OnFly, LiveVLN), (iii) geometry-aware representation systems (SpatialFly), and (iv) recoverability-oriented replanning systems (OpenVLN-style pipelines). The purpose of this grouping is not only organizational. It gives D06 an evidence discipline: planner frontends should mainly improve clause preservation and pre-verifier alignment; runtime systems should mainly improve packet survivability and guarded continuity; geometry-aware representations should mainly improve cross-view grounding and motion smoothness; and replanning-aware systems should mainly improve recovery-corridor preservation.

We further freeze first-round interpretation into three packet-centric routes. **Route P1 — clause-preserving packet sufficiency** applies only when clause retention, verifier compatibility, and manipulation-ready preservation are all stably positive. **Route P2 — clause-preserving but handoff-fragile packets** applies when packet semantics improve but MRP stays weak or RPL stays high, meaning the system preserves current instruction intent while still collapsing near the `approach → manipulate-ready` boundary. **Route P3 — handoff-stable packet selection** applies when retention-guided refresh, stage-aware budgets, and handoff diagnostics produce robust gains even after clause preservation is already strong, implying that the main bottleneck has shifted from language serialization to late-stage interface stability.

### 4.2.5 Retention-First Reading at the Navigation-to-Manipulation Boundary

The local literature base and our recent paper-map review suggest that the next scientific question for D06 is not whether planner packets can be generated, but whether they remain **retained well enough** to survive the `search → approach → manipulate-ready` boundary. Existing planner-side advances such as FineCog-Nav and LookasideVLN mainly improve clause parsing, direction grounding, and planner-side memory usage, while runtime systems such as OnFly and LiveVLN mainly improve online survivability and guarded execution continuity. However, current evaluations rarely test whether these gains stay jointly valid once the packet must remain manipulation-ready at handoff time. We therefore make **packet retention** an explicit reading layer over the main results instead of treating it as a hidden implementation detail.

Concretely, we interpret a packet as deployment-credible only if it remains jointly positive on three stacked questions: **planner correctness**, **refresh-time context retention**, and **navigation-to-manipulation handoff stability**. Planner correctness asks whether the emitted packet still matches the active clause, directional relation, and current search-stage intent. Refresh-time context retention asks whether the contextual support that originally justified the packet—landmark anchor, confidence provenance, and stage rationale—survives online update rather than being silently erased by a geometrically attractive refresh. Navigation-to-manipulation handoff stability asks whether a packet that is still semantically valid and flight-feasible remains *manipulation-ready* when the system crosses into a local interaction shell. A system should not receive full packet-centric credit if it only improves the first question while repeatedly failing the latter two.

This reading rule leads to a stricter route-freezing policy. If CSR or CAPG rises but CCR, CtxPR, or PKR remains weak, the correct conclusion is that the method improved **planner-side serialization** without yet improving deployable retention. If retention metrics remain acceptable during search but MRP, HRD, or SOR deteriorate near the handoff shell, the correct conclusion is **retention-positive but NtM-fragile packetization**, not a generic navigation gain. Only when planner correctness, context retention, and handoff stability rise together do we upgrade the interpretation to a true **retention-sufficient packet interface**. This keeps D06 from overstating planner improvements that disappear exactly where the aerial system must transition from finding the object to acting around it.

### 4.2.6 Retention-Aware Baseline Family Expectations

The baseline-family protocol is therefore extended with retention-aware expectations. Clause-aware planner frontends are expected to improve **CAPG / CSR / CPA** before they improve MRP or HRD. Runtime-survivability systems are expected to improve **PSR / RCS / RFR / CCR** before they improve clause preservation. Geometry-aware representation systems are expected to improve alignment and smoothness while remaining ambiguous on context carryover. Recoverability-oriented systems are expected to improve RBE and post-rejection correction, but they should only be credited with late-stage interface gains if retained packets remain manipulation-ready instead of merely prolonging search-time viability.

This extension also prevents a common interpretive mistake in aerial embodied navigation: treating a packet that stays executable for longer as automatically more useful for downstream manipulation. In our protocol, longer execution continuity without retained manipulation readiness is only a **runtime-survivability gain**. Conversely, stronger clause adherence without manipulation-ready persistence is only a **planner-side retention gain**. These distinctions make the D06 story narrower but more honest, which is exactly what we want before the paper matures from a systems sketch into a submission-ready argument.

### 4.2.7 Retention-Binned Main-Results Readout

To make first-round evidence comparable across packet families, we further require the main table to be read under three explicit retention bins rather than only by aggregate episode success. **B0 — navigation-correct but not interaction-ready** contains packets that preserve the active clause and target region well enough for search-time success, yet still lack the pose precision, contextual anchor, or guarded execution continuity required for downstream interaction. **B1 — interaction-preparable but not handoff-stable** contains packets that are locally close enough to support short-horizon approach or visual servoing, but still fail to remain robust under the actual handoff boundary. **B2 — handoff-stable manipulation-ready** contains packets that stay manipulation-ready across the `approach → manipulate-ready` boundary and remain consumable by the downstream local interaction stack.

Under this readout, D06 should report not only final SR but also the conversion chain `B0 → B1 → B2`, together with the paired transitions `T01` and `T12`, where `T01` measures how often navigation-correct packets become interaction-preparable, and `T12` measures how often interaction-preparable packets truly become handoff-stable. We further define **BCR** (Bin Conversion Rate) as the aggregate conversion efficiency across the chain. This view makes it much harder to hide deployability failures behind strong early-stage planner scores, because packets trapped in B0 or B1 are no longer allowed to masquerade as true NtM successes.

This retention-binned protocol also sharpens baseline interpretation. A clause-aware planner should primarily reduce the mass trapped in B0 by improving clause preservation and direction-conditioned adherence. A runtime-survivability method should mainly improve `T01` by preserving context and guarded execution during online refresh. A true handoff-aware interface should primarily improve `T12` and B2 occupancy by preserving manipulation-ready packet structure through the final boundary. If a method improves only B0 occupancy while B2 remains weak, we should freeze the contribution as **planner-side navigation gain**. If B0 and B1 improve but B2 remains unstable, we should freeze it as **refresh-stable yet NtM-fragile packetization**. Only when the full `B0 → B1 → B2` chain strengthens together should we elevate the story to a genuine **handoff-stable deployable interface**.

### 4.2.8 Route-Freezing Rule Under Retention Drops

We finally formalize a route-freezing rule tied to these retention-bin transitions. When `T01` remains healthy but `T12` is weak and B2 occupancy stays low, the default interpretation route is **Route C — stage-handoff bottleneck**, because the system can prepare interaction but still cannot preserve manipulation readiness at commitment time. When B0 dominates and both `T01` and `T12` remain weak, the default route is **Route B — packet deployability bottleneck**, because the system is losing deployable semantics before it ever becomes interaction-preparable. When all three bins look stable yet end success is still weak, we should preferentially test **Route D — controller-shell bottleneck** before inventing new planner stories.

This rule is especially important for D06 because many aerial systems can look strong under search-time metrics while still failing exactly where the UAV must stop being a navigator and start being an interaction-capable embodied agent. By binding interpretation to the `B0/B1/B2` conversion structure, we prevent ourselves from claiming a full packet-first aerial VLN advance when the evidence only supports earlier planner correctness or midstream runtime stability. In other words, retention-bin transitions are not bookkeeping details; they are the minimal evidence needed to decide whether D06 has solved a planner problem, a refresh problem, or a navigation-to-manipulation interface problem.

### 4.2.21 Delayed-Consumption Attribution Lock for Packet-Centric Claims

We further lock packet-centric attribution to the delayed-consumption surface rather than the planner-emission surface alone. Let `Δ_emit` denote gains observed when packets are scored immediately after planner emission and `Δ_consume(δ)` denote gains observed when the same packets are scored after a controller-consumption lag `δ`. We define a delayed-consumption preservation ratio
\[
\rho_{\mathrm{dc}}(\delta)=\frac{\Delta_{\mathrm{consume}}(\delta)}{\Delta_{\mathrm{emit}}+\epsilon},
\]
and require packet-centric deployment claims to state whether their evidence survives at the relevant lag bucket. When `\rho_{\mathrm{dc}}(\delta)` collapses sharply for family-matched controls, the honest interpretation is that the method improved packet appearance or bind-time continuity rather than true downstream handoff value.

This lock prevents a recurring overclaiming pattern in aerial VLN papers: gains that are real at planner time but no longer matter once the controller consumes a slightly delayed packet are not allowed to remain framed as full packet-interface improvements. In D06, any result whose main advantage disappears under delayed-consumption bins must be routed back to `clause-preserving frontend gain`, `runtime continuity support`, or `geometry-side stabilization`, unless `B2`, `T12`, and `CCH` remain positive at consumption time. The deployment headline is therefore earned only when packet quality survives the exact temporal surface where the controller and NtM handoff stack must act on it.

### 4.2.9 Manipulation-Ready Is Not Navigation-Correct

A stronger late-stage distinction is needed for D06: a packet that is **navigation-correct** is not necessarily **manipulation-ready**. OnFly-style onboard navigation evidence shows that a system can preserve progress and semantic validity under live execution, while Unified Aerial Grasping and related aerial manipulation pipelines imply that downstream interaction requires additional structure that ordinary navigation metrics do not guarantee. In particular, a packet may still satisfy the active clause, point toward the right object region, and remain verifier-passable, yet fail to preserve the pose precision, contextual anchor, or guarded execution state needed for stable local interaction once the UAV enters the operational shell.

We therefore explicitly distinguish three increasingly strict packet states: **planner-valid**, **navigation-correct**, and **manipulation-ready**. A planner-valid packet preserves clause and directional intent at emission time. A navigation-correct packet additionally remains semantically and geometrically executable under online refresh and controller consumption. A manipulation-ready packet must further preserve target-shell stability, pose confidence, yaw-altitude compatibility, and bounded local safety margins required by the downstream NtM controller. This hierarchy matters because many aerial navigation baselines only optimize toward the middle layer while implicitly assuming that near-target success will transfer to interaction.

This distinction is directly supported by the local evidence chain used in this round. AerialVLA shows that minimalist end-to-end control can efficiently solve the navigation-facing problem under fuzzy directional prompting, but it does not yet formalize a packet contract for interaction-ready handoff. LookasideVLN and FineCog-Nav show that stronger clause-level or direction-aware planning can improve instruction adherence before execution, but neither paper by itself proves that these gains survive refresh and remain usable at the manipulation boundary. LiveVLN and OnFly show that runtime continuity and verification-aware onboard execution can stabilize deployment, yet they still leave open whether a refreshed packet retains the context and pose precision needed for local interaction. Together, these works justify treating `navigation-correct → manipulation-ready` as an explicit deployment gap rather than an implementation footnote.

The practical consequence is that our late-stage analysis must always ask a counterfactual question: if the UAV stopped searching now and handed the packet to a local manipulation controller, would the packet still be sufficient without hidden planner-side repair? If the answer is often no, then D06 has discovered a handoff bottleneck rather than a complete deployable interface. This rule narrows the story, but it also makes the final paper scientifically cleaner: the contribution is not “better packets everywhere,” but **better retained packets up to the strongest still-true boundary**.
### 4.2.10 Refresh Is Only Useful When It Preserves Handoff Value

The previous sections treat refresh as a retention-sensitive operation; here we sharpen the deployment reading one step further. A packet refresh should be credited only when it preserves or improves **handoff value**, not merely when it reduces short-horizon staleness. AerialVLA-style minimalist systems remind us that lightweight continuous control can appear strong when the task remains purely navigational, while OnFly-style guarded runtime systems show that live execution can stabilize onboard behavior. But neither class of evidence by itself guarantees that a refreshed packet remains valuable at the `approach → manipulate-ready` boundary.

We therefore define refresh usefulness in a handoff-aware way. A refresh event is counted as **handoff-positive** only if it both (i) improves or preserves immediate packet executability and (ii) does not reduce downstream interaction readiness beyond an acceptable threshold. Put differently, refresh is not judged solely by whether the current waypoint becomes more flyable or semantically current; it is judged by whether the refreshed packet remains a good carrier of future local interaction. This is the deployment-facing meaning of retention-first packet maintenance.

In practice, this means D06 should reject a naive "refresh more often is always better" reading. If frequent refresh raises CSR during search but increases RPL, weakens MRP, or destabilizes SOR near the handoff shell, then refresh is functioning as a hidden source of late-stage deployment regression. Conversely, a conservative refresh policy that leaves some short-horizon geometric optimality on the table may still be preferable if it preserves guarded execution state, target-shell stability, and downstream controller consumability. Our hypothesis is therefore stricter than online navigation alone: the best refresh policy is not the one that makes packets newest, but the one that keeps them **useful at handoff time**.

This rule is important because some refreshes are only **search-positive** while being **handoff-negative**. For example, the latest observation may sharpen a local geometric route or reduce packet staleness, yet simultaneously drop the contextual anchor that justified a stable approach pose, or perturb the yaw/altitude shell enough to make the packet harder for a local interaction controller to consume. Standard navigation metrics would often count such an update as a success because the UAV still reaches the target region. Our framework instead counts it as a net loss if the packet becomes less manipulation-ready even while becoming more locally current.

Accordingly, D06 should report refresh outcomes with a paired interpretation: **navigation utility gain** versus **handoff value retention**. Only when both remain non-negative do we treat the refresh policy as a real deployability improvement. If navigation utility rises while handoff value falls, the result should be frozen as a search-time runtime gain rather than promoted to an NtM-capable interface contribution. This keeps the late-stage paper story honest and aligns the refresh mechanism with the actual embodied objective of navigate-then-manipulate.

### 4.2.11 Handoff-Value-Preserving Freeze Rule

We add one final freeze rule over all late-stage gains: every result must now be mapped into one of three buckets—**navigation-positive but handoff-neutral**, **refresh-positive but handoff-negative**, or **handoff-value-preserving**. The first bucket covers gains that improve search-time or approach-time packet quality without materially improving `T12`, `B2`, `MRP`, or `CCH`. The second bucket covers gains that reduce staleness or improve local verifier acceptance while worsening `HRD`, increasing retention-bin collapse, or forcing stage downgrade. Only the third bucket is allowed to support a full D06 claim about a deployable Navigate-then-Manipulate interface.

Operationally, this means our late-stage table is not read as a flat leaderboard. We first ask whether the method preserves **target identity stability**, **controller-consumable packet structure**, and **manipulation-ready shell validity** at the actual packet-consumption time. If any of these properties fail, the gain must be frozen below full NtM value even if `SR`, `VPR`, or `RCS` improve. This protects the paper from over-reading methods that look more active, more up-to-date, or more responsive, yet still hand downstream modules a worse packet at takeover time.

The deployment implication is strict but clean: refresh, retention, and runtime continuity are not intrinsically valuable in D06. They are valuable only when they protect downstream **handoff value** at the moment the manipulation stack takes over. In practice, that makes `B2`, `T12`, `MRP`, `CCH`, and low `HRD/BCR` the final arbiters of whether a packet-centric gain is genuinely deployable or merely cosmetically smoother.

### 4.6 Progress-Aware Packet Supervision and Trace-Guided Replanning

Recent long-horizon evidence such as LoHo-Manip suggests that a deployable planner should expose not only the next executable subgoal, but also a compact notion of *remaining task progress*. We adapt this intuition to aerial VLN by attaching an auxiliary progress trace to each semantic waypoint packet during training. The trace contains an ordered subgoal sketch and a lightweight geometric continuation cue over the local 3D map. Its purpose is not to replace waypoint execution, but to regularize packet generation toward progress-consistent online refresh.

This supervision becomes especially useful when multiple frontier packets are all semantically plausible yet correspond to different clauses or stages of the instruction. We therefore let the verifier reject refreshed packets that remain geometrically flyable but break the remaining progress order. In effect, the system distinguishes between *locally executable* packets and *progress-faithful* packets, which is critical for long-horizon aerial search tasks with multi-clause language goals.

To measure this effect, we explicitly evaluate **Progress-Trace Consistency (PTC)** and **Trace-Break Reject Rate (TBRR)**. PTC measures agreement between refreshed packets and the remaining ordered trace, while TBRR measures how often the verifier catches packets that are still executable but no longer respect progress continuity. These metrics let us test whether progress-aware supervision improves actual deployment behavior rather than only planner-side narrative quality.

### 4.7 Minimal Sanity Checks for Progress Traces and Context Serialization

We add two additional sanity checks beyond pure packetization. **SC4: hidden memory vs serialized context** compares whether planner-side retrieval improves downstream execution only when preserved as packet fields instead of remaining latent inside planner activations. **SC5: packet-only supervision vs packet + progress trace supervision** compares whether auxiliary trace conditioning reduces stage drift, repeated late-stage replanning, and clause-order violations under multi-clause instructions. **SC6: verifier-free training vs verifier-distilled training** compares whether executability-aware supervision lowers packet rejection, staleness, and controller-consumption failure without sacrificing instruction adherence.

We expect SC4-SC6 to matter most in long-horizon aerial VLN where semantic relevance alone is insufficient to guarantee correct progression. If these checks fail to improve verifier-compatible execution, then memory-aware packetization and progress traces should remain auxiliary analysis tools rather than headline method claims.

### 4.8 Runtime-Coupled Packet Diagnostics

To make the paper more deployment-facing, we further define a runtime-coupled diagnostic layer that directly tests which part of the packet chain fails first under onboard execution pressure. We pre-register five failure families. **F1 Semantic misgrounding** refers to packets whose target hypothesis is wrong despite acceptable geometry; these should mainly depress Clause-to-Packet Alignment (CPA) and Memory-to-Packet Conversion Rate (MPCR) while leaving controller tracking relatively normal. **F2 Directional mismatch** captures runs where landmarks are recognized correctly but egocentric direction words are misparsed, leading to elevated Heading Drift Error and weak direction-conditioned adherence. **F3 Packet staleness** refers to packets that were initially valid but expire before guarded-prefix completion, visible as high Packet Staleness Rate (PSR) and low Runtime Continuity Score (RCS). **F4 Handoff failure** captures cases where verifier or controller consumption breaks despite reasonable planning quality, exposed by high Packet Handoff Failure Rate (PHFR) and low Memory-Conditioned Packet Sufficiency (MCPS). **F5 Cross-stage drift** refers to packets whose semantic content remains plausible but whose stage tag no longer matches the search→approach→manipulate boundary, measured by Cross-Stage Drift Rate (CSDR) and low Progress-Trace Consistency (PTC).

This diagnostic protocol matters because standard aerial VLN reporting still tends to collapse planner semantics, runtime refresh, verifier mismatch, and manipulation-boundary confusion into one final success number. By keeping packet-level attribution explicit, we can answer a more useful question than “which planner wins?”—namely, *which planner emits packets that remain usable by the rest of the aerial stack under latency, verification, and stage handoff constraints?* In practice, this section is where the packet-centric framing becomes falsifiable: if most failures still cannot be localized after packetization, then the interface claim is weak; if failures become attributable and repairable, then packetization is doing real scientific work rather than acting as a software wrapper.
